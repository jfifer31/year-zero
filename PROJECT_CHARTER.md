# Project Charter

## 1. Project name

**Year Zero Education**

---

## 2. Mission

Year Zero Education exists to derive, from first principles, an educational framework for young people living in a world increasingly shaped by artificial intelligence and abundant access to intelligence.

The project’s guiding question is:

> What should young people learn, experience, practise and demonstrate in order to become flourishing, capable and adaptive adults in the Intelligence Era?

The aim is not to modernise a conventional curriculum by adding AI tools. The aim is to re-examine education from its foundations and construct a curriculum hypothesis that can be publicly scrutinised, tested and improved.

---

## 3. Initial release objective

The initial release objective is a **Version 0.1 open-source curriculum hypothesis** for KS3–KS4 learners, with a KS3-first focus.

Version 0.1 should be:

- derived through a transparent research chain;
- supported by explicit evidence where evidence exists;
- clear about assumptions where evidence is incomplete;
- honest about normative judgements;
- careful with future projections;
- usable by parents, educators, mentors and motivated learners;
- structured for public testing and revision.

Version 0.1 is not a proof of effectiveness. It is the first serious testable version.

---

## 4. Why KS3–KS4?

The project begins with KS3–KS4 because early and middle adolescence are developmentally important periods for:

- identity formation;
- agency;
- motivation;
- belonging;
- self-regulation;
- social comparison;
- moral reasoning;
- abstract thought;
- practical capability;
- future orientation.

It is also the period where many learners begin to experience the full pressure of conventional assessment pathways, social media, emerging independence and increasingly powerful digital tools.

The first release should be especially useful for KS3 while remaining extensible toward KS4.

---

## 5. Core premise

Artificial intelligence may change the conditions under which young people learn, work, think, create, coordinate, decide and build meaningful lives.

This does not mean the future is predetermined. It means education should be designed with serious attention to plausible changes in:

- access to knowledge;
- access to intelligence;
- cognitive work;
- creative production;
- labour markets;
- institutional trust;
- social coordination;
- truth and misinformation;
- personal agency;
- human judgement;
- meaning and identity.

The project must avoid both AI hype and AI dismissal. It should treat the future as uncertain but not unknowable.

---

## 6. What the project is trying to produce

The project aims to produce:

1. A defensible research chain.
2. A set of human and future-facing assumptions that are visible and contestable.
3. A model of flourishing suitable for the project’s purposes.
4. A map of plausible AI-shaped future conditions.
5. A set of human invariants across plausible futures.
6. A derived set of enduring human functions.
7. A capability model for young people.
8. A map of knowledge, experience and practice that can cultivate those capabilities.
9. An evidence-of-learning model.
10. A Version 0.1 curriculum architecture.
11. A release package suitable for public critique and early testing.

---

## 7. What the project is not trying to produce yet

The project is not initially trying to produce:

- a legally recognised qualification;
- a school inspection framework;
- a complete replacement for national curriculum obligations;
- a universal answer for every culture and context;
- a commercial course;
- a definitive theory of human nature;
- a prediction of the future;
- a finished assessment system;
- a claim of proven educational impact.

Those may become later discussions, but they are not Version 0.1 goals.

---

## 8. Design philosophy

The project follows an engineering-led research model.

This means:

1. Define the problem clearly.
2. Identify constraints.
3. Build from first principles where possible.
4. Use evidence where available.
5. Make assumptions explicit where evidence is incomplete.
6. Build the simplest complete architecture that can work.
7. Test weak points adversarially.
8. Publish an initial version.
9. Gather feedback.
10. Revise through versioned iteration.

The goal is not to wait until certainty exists. The goal is to avoid pretending that uncertainty does not exist.

---

## 9. Research chain

The research chain is the central structure of the project: it moves from human foundations and AI-shaped future conditions into convergence, derivation, learning evidence, curriculum architecture and release readiness.

The canonical phase sequence lives in [`ROADMAP.md`](ROADMAP.md). The stage navigation table and guide links live in [`research/README.md`](research/README.md). The dependency discipline, pass-forward principle and method rules live in [`docs/RESEARCH_OPERATING_SYSTEM.md`](docs/RESEARCH_OPERATING_SYSTEM.md).

---

## 10. Research discipline

The project separates empirical findings, working assumptions, normative judgements, speculative projections and later-stage design decisions. No agent or contributor may present one claim type as another.

The full operational taxonomy, handling rules, evidence standards, confidence language, uncertainty requirements, pass-forward protocol and adversarial review rules are defined in `docs/RESEARCH_OPERATING_SYSTEM.md`.

---

## 11. Pass-forward principle

Each stage must produce a pass-forward memo that states what later stages may use.

Later stages may not freely reinterpret earlier outputs. They may only rely on what has been explicitly passed forward, including stated uncertainties and limitations.

This protects the project from hidden drift.

---

## 12. Public release principle

The project should be publicly understandable without being intellectually weakened.

Public materials should:

- explain the logic clearly;
- avoid hype;
- avoid false certainty;
- invite challenge;
- distinguish evidence from judgement;
- make uncertainty visible;
- help non-specialists understand why the work matters.

---

## 13. Ethical commitments

The project must be especially careful because it concerns young people.

It must avoid exposing private learner data, publicising sensitive learner reflections, claiming proven impact without evidence, treating children as experimental subjects without safeguards, encouraging people to abandon statutory obligations or obscuring the experimental status of Version 0.1.

The canonical rules for safeguarding, learner privacy, consent/assent, public testing, AI logs and learner data are in `docs/ETHICS_AND_PUBLIC_TESTING.md`.

---

## 14. Definition of a successful Version 0.1

Version 0.1 succeeds if it is:

- coherent;
- traceable;
- usable;
- honest;
- open to critique;
- structured for testing;
- capable of generating genuinely novel insight about curriculum design;
- valuable enough for educators, parents, learners and researchers to engage with.

It does not need to solve education. It needs to create the first serious, public, inspectable version of a better question.
