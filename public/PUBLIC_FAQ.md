# Public FAQ

## 1. What is Year Zero Education?

Year Zero Education is an open-source research and curriculum project.

It aims to derive a future-facing educational framework for young people growing up in a world increasingly shaped by artificial intelligence and abundant access to intelligence.

---

## 2. Why is it called Year Zero?

The name signals a first-principles reset.

It does not mean ignoring the past or dismissing existing education. It means temporarily stepping back from inherited assumptions and asking what education should be if we were designing it from the ground up for the Intelligence Era.

---

## 3. Is this a school?

No.

Year Zero Education is not a school, academy, tutoring service or formal education provider.

It is an open-source curriculum research project.

---

## 4. Is this a finished curriculum?

No.

The first public release will be **Version 0.1**, which means it is a curriculum hypothesis.

It should be used cautiously, criticised seriously and improved over time.

---

## 5. Who is it for?

The initial focus is KS3–KS4 learners, with a KS3-first release.

It may be useful for:

- home-educated learners;
- alternative provision settings;
- mainstream school enrichment;
- project-based learning groups;
- parents thinking about future skills;
- educators exploring curriculum redesign;
- learners interested in building a portfolio of meaningful work.

---

## 6. Does it replace the national curriculum?

No.

Version 0.1 should not be treated as a legal or formal replacement for statutory curriculum requirements.

It may become a supplement, enrichment framework, alternative learning pathway or research-based curriculum hypothesis, depending on context.

---

## 7. Does it replace exams?

No.

The project explores evidence of learning beyond conventional exams, including portfolios, artefacts, demonstrations, reflection and transfer tasks.

That does not mean it currently provides formal qualifications or recognised credentials.

---

## 8. Is this anti-school?

No.

The project is not anti-school. It is pro-serious education.

It asks whether the structure, goals and evidence of education need to change in response to AI and wider social conditions.

Schools, home educators, alternative providers and independent learners may all find parts of the project useful.

---

## 9. Is this just about teaching AI skills?

No.

AI literacy matters, but the project is deeper than tool use.

The central question is what human capabilities matter when access to intelligence becomes more abundant.

That includes judgement, agency, meaning, ethical responsibility, attention, collaboration, creativity, embodiment, truth-seeking and practical capability.

---

## 10. Will learners use AI tools?

Probably, but not as the whole point.

AI tools may be used for inquiry, feedback, simulation, comparison, planning and accessibility.

Learners should also practise checking AI outputs, making their own judgements, explaining their reasoning and doing human-only practice where needed.

---

## 11. Is this evidence-based?

The project is evidence-disciplined.

That means it uses evidence where available, labels assumptions where evidence is incomplete, and separates values from empirical findings.

Version 0.1 will not be “proven” in the sense of demonstrated educational impact. It should be treated as a traceable research-derived hypothesis.

---

## 12. How does the research process work?

The project follows eleven stages:

1. Human Baseline
2. Human Flourishing Model
3. AI Future Conditions Discovery
4. Life Within Future Conditions
5. Human Invariants Analysis
6. Enduring Human Functions
7. Capability Derivation
8. Knowledge, Experience and Practice Mapping
9. Evidence of Learning and Verification Architecture
10. Curriculum Architecture and Version 0.1 Design
11. Verification, Falsification and Release Readiness

Each stage produces a report, review, ledger, pass-forward memo and public summary.

---

## 13. What is a curriculum hypothesis?

A curriculum hypothesis is a structured proposal that can be tested, criticised and improved.

It is stronger than a loose idea, but weaker than a proven system.

Version 0.1 should be coherent enough to use and honest enough to revise.

---

## 14. Can parents use it?

Yes, once materials are released, parents may use them for exploration, enrichment or home education support.

However, parents should understand that Version 0.1 is experimental and should not be treated as guaranteed or formally recognised.

---

## 15. Can teachers use it?

Yes.

Teachers may use it to:

- review curriculum assumptions;
- design enrichment projects;
- explore capability-based learning;
- test evidence-of-learning approaches;
- support AI-era education discussions.

Any school use should respect local policies, safeguarding requirements and workload constraints.

---

## 16. Can learners use it independently?

Some older or motivated learners may be able to use parts independently.

However, younger learners should have adult guidance, especially where AI tools, online sharing or public portfolios are involved.

---

## 17. Will it be free?

The project is intended to be open-source and freely accessible.

If future services, tools or support structures are created, the project should remain clear about what is open, what is paid and what claims are supported by evidence.

---

## 18. How can I contribute?

You can contribute by:

- challenging claims;
- suggesting better evidence;
- testing materials;
- reporting implementation feedback;
- improving documentation;
- identifying safety or accessibility risks;
- helping make the public explanation clearer.

Read `CONTRIBUTING.md` first.

---

## 19. Can learner work be shared publicly?

Only with care.

Learner work should not be shared publicly if it reveals personal information, private reflections, images, AI logs, school records or anything sensitive.

Where possible, use anonymised summaries or synthetic examples.

---

## 20. What would count as success?

The project succeeds if Version 0.1 becomes a serious, usable and traceable starting point for rethinking education in the Intelligence Era.

It does not need to be perfect. It needs to be clear, honest, useful and improvable.
