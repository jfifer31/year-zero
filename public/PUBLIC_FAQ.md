# Public FAQ

## 1. What is Year Zero Education?

Year Zero Education is an open-source research and curriculum project. It aims to derive a future-facing educational framework for KS3–KS4 learners through a staged and publicly inspectable process.

Read the [Project Charter](../PROJECT_CHARTER.md) for the canonical mission and scope.

## 2. Why is it called Year Zero?

The name signals a first-principles reset. It does not mean ignoring the past or dismissing existing education. It means temporarily stepping back from inherited structures and asking what education should be trying to achieve.

## 3. Is this a school or finished curriculum?

No. It is not a school, tutoring service, formal education provider or finished curriculum.

The planned Version 0.1 will be a curriculum hypothesis: a structured proposal that can be criticised and tested, not a proven intervention.

## 4. Who is it for?

The initial curriculum hypothesis is intended for KS3–KS4 learners, with a KS3-first focus. The public project also serves parents, educators, researchers, reviewers and contributors.

## 5. Does it replace the national curriculum or exams?

No. Version 0.1 will not replace statutory requirements, recognised qualifications or conventional exams.

## 6. Is this anti-school?

No. The project questions inherited curriculum assumptions, not the commitment of schools or educators. Its outputs may eventually be useful across school, home-education, alternative-provision and informal settings.

## 7. Is this just about AI skills?

No. AI is part of the changing context, but the project is investigating broader questions about human agency, judgement, development and flourishing. It has not yet derived a final capability model.

## 8. Will learners use AI tools?

No final decision has been authorised. Later stages will examine where AI might support learning, where it might weaken required practice, and what safeguards are needed.

## 9. Is this evidence-based?

The project is evidence-disciplined: it uses evidence where available, labels assumptions and values, preserves uncertainty and requires adversarial review.

That process does not make Version 0.1 proven. The canonical standards are in the [Research Operating System](../docs/RESEARCH_OPERATING_SYSTEM.md).

## 10. How does the research process work?

Eleven stages move from human and future conditions towards curriculum architecture and release review. Every completed stage must produce an execution plan, ledger, report, review, pass-forward memo and public summary.

See the [roadmap](../ROADMAP.md) and [research-stage index](../research/README.md).

## 11. What is a curriculum hypothesis?

A curriculum hypothesis is a coherent proposal with visible reasoning, assumptions and limitations. It is designed to be tested and revised rather than presented as final truth.

## 12. Can parents, teachers or learners use it now?

There is no authorised curriculum to use yet. Current files may be read and reviewed, but the Version 0.1 container must remain structural until the relevant stages populate it.

## 13. Will it be free?

Repository content is openly licensed. See the [licence](../LICENSE) for the terms that apply to educational content and software.

## 14. How can I contribute?

You can challenge claims, suggest evidence, identify missing perspectives, improve documentation or raise safety and accessibility concerns. Read [CONTRIBUTING.md](../CONTRIBUTING.md) first.

## 15. Can learner work be shared publicly?

Public contributors should not submit learner work, private learner information, images, videos or AI logs. Use broad anonymised observations only and follow the [ethics rules](../docs/ETHICS_AND_PUBLIC_TESTING.md).

## 16. What would count as success?

Version 0.1 should be coherent, traceable, usable, honest about uncertainty and valuable enough to test and improve. It will not count as proof of educational effectiveness.
