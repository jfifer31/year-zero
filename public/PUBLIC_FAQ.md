# Public FAQ

## 1. What is Year Zero Education?

Year Zero Education is an open-source research and curriculum project. It aims to derive a future-facing educational framework for young people growing up in a world increasingly shaped by artificial intelligence and abundant access to intelligence.

For the full mission, scope, values and success definition, read `PROJECT_CHARTER.md`.

---

## 2. Why is it called Year Zero?

The name signals a first-principles reset.

It does not mean ignoring the past or dismissing existing education. It means temporarily stepping back from inherited assumptions and asking what education should be if we were designing it from the ground up for the Intelligence Era.

---

## 3. Is this a school?

No.

Year Zero Education is not a school, academy, tutoring service or formal education provider.

It is an open-source curriculum research project.

---

## 4. Is this a finished curriculum?

No.

The first public release will be **Version 0.1**. **Version 0.1 is a curriculum hypothesis, not evidence of proven effectiveness.**

It should be used cautiously, criticised seriously and improved over time.

## 4. Who is it for?

The initial focus is KS3–KS4 learners, with a KS3-first release. It may be useful for home-educated learners, alternative provision settings, mainstream school enrichment, project-based learning groups, parents, educators and learners interested in meaningful portfolios of work.

---

## 5. Does it replace the national curriculum or exams?

No. Version 0.1 should not be treated as a legal or formal replacement for statutory curriculum requirements, recognised credentials or conventional exams.

The project explores evidence of learning beyond exams, including portfolios, artefacts, demonstrations, reflection and transfer tasks. That does not make it an official assessment system.

---

## 6. Is this anti-school?

No. The project is not anti-school. It is pro-serious education.

Schools, home educators, alternative providers and independent learners may all find parts of the project useful.

---

## 7. Is this just about teaching AI skills?

No. AI literacy matters, but the project is deeper than tool use. The central question is what human capabilities matter when access to intelligence becomes more abundant.

That includes judgement, agency, meaning, ethical responsibility, attention, collaboration, creativity, embodiment, truth-seeking and practical capability.

---

## 8. Will learners use AI tools?

Probably, but not as the whole point. AI tools may be used for inquiry, feedback, simulation, comparison, planning and accessibility.

Learners should also practise checking AI outputs, making their own judgements, explaining their reasoning and doing human-only practice where needed.

---

## 9. Is this evidence-based?

The project is evidence-disciplined: it uses evidence where available, labels assumptions where evidence is incomplete and separates values from empirical findings.

That means it uses evidence where available, labels assumptions where evidence is incomplete, and separates values from empirical findings.

**Version 0.1 is a curriculum hypothesis, not evidence of proven effectiveness.** It should be treated as a traceable research-derived hypothesis.

---

## 11. What is a curriculum hypothesis?

A curriculum hypothesis is a structured proposal that can be tested, criticised and improved. It is stronger than a loose idea, but weaker than a proven system.

Version 0.1 should be coherent enough to use and honest enough to revise.

---

## 12. Can parents, teachers or learners use it?

Yes, once materials are released, they may be used for exploration, enrichment, curriculum review, low-stakes experimentation or independent learning with appropriate adult guidance.

Users should understand that Version 0.1 is experimental and should not be treated as guaranteed, formally recognised or suitable for every learner or context.

---

## 13. Will it be free?

The project is intended to be open-source and freely accessible. If future services, tools or support structures are created, the project should remain clear about what is open, what is paid and what claims are supported by evidence.

---

## 14. How can I contribute?

You can challenge claims, suggest better evidence, test materials, report implementation feedback, improve documentation, identify safety or accessibility risks and help make public explanation clearer.

Read `CONTRIBUTING.md` first.

---

## 15. Can learner work be shared publicly?

Only with care. Do not share learner work publicly if it reveals personal information, private reflections, images, AI logs, school records or anything sensitive. Prefer anonymised summaries, synthetic examples or fictionalised composites.

The canonical rules for safeguarding, learner privacy, consent/assent, public testing, AI logs and learner data are in `docs/ETHICS_AND_PUBLIC_TESTING.md`.

---

## 16. What would count as success?

The project succeeds if Version 0.1 becomes a serious, usable and traceable starting point for rethinking education in the Intelligence Era.

It does not need to be perfect. It needs to be clear, honest, useful and improvable. The canonical success definition is in `PROJECT_CHARTER.md`.

---

## 17. How does the research process work?

The project follows an eleven-stage research chain from human foundations and AI-shaped future conditions through curriculum architecture and release readiness. Each stage produces a report, review, ledger, pass-forward memo and public summary.

For the phase sequence, read [`ROADMAP.md`](../ROADMAP.md). For the stage navigation table and guide links, read [`research/README.md`](../research/README.md). For dependency discipline and method rules, read [`docs/RESEARCH_OPERATING_SYSTEM.md`](../docs/RESEARCH_OPERATING_SYSTEM.md).
