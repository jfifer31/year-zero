# Public Summary — Stage 3: AI Future Conditions Discovery

**Plain-language title:** What AI might technically become — without pretending to predict one future

---

## What question did this stage ask?

Stage 3 asked what AI and AI-adjacent technologies may plausibly become over the coming years. It looked at foundation models, coding agents, software interfaces, robotics, AI for science and medicine, compute infrastructure, open and edge AI, safety, misuse, synthetic media and AI companions.

It did not ask what education should do yet. It did not design a curriculum. It did not predict one future.

---

## How was this stage run?

The stage used the existing Stage 3 source base only. Specialist agents each worked from their assigned source rows, then a chaired synthesis, evidence audit and red-team review tested the strongest claims. The final thesis and chapter order were chosen only after that discussion.

---

## What did it find?

The most useful finding is that AI futures are not just about one line going up called “intelligence.” AI becomes powerful when capability can be turned into reliable action.

That conversion is easier in some places than others.

- In software, AI can often get quick feedback from tests, code execution and benchmarks.
- In robotics, AI has to deal with gravity, bodies, safety, cost and messy environments.
- In science and medicine, AI can generate candidates quickly, but proof still requires experiments, trials and validation.
- In infrastructure, AI depends on chips, data centres, energy, water, capital and supply chains.
- In society, AI changes trust through synthetic media, companions, automated judgement, misuse risks and governance.

The report’s simplest takeaway is: AI may move fastest where reality can answer cheaply, and more slowly where reality is expensive to verify.

---

## What remains uncertain?

A lot remains uncertain.

- Recursive self-improvement is not established.
- Coding-agent benchmarks do not prove full real-world autonomy.
- Robotics may progress, but broad physical deployment is still hard.
- AI for science may speed up discovery steps, but not necessarily validated outcomes.
- Open and edge AI may widen access while making governance harder.
- Synthetic media may raise trust costs, but it does not automatically destroy truth.
- AI companions may matter socially, but their long-term effects are not settled.

---

## What does this mean for the next stage?

Stage 4 can now build future-world analyses using technical condition bands from Stage 3. These bands include software acceleration, agentic tool use, robotics constraints, scientific discovery acceleration, infrastructure limits, open/edge AI divergence, governance pressure and ambient trust risks.

Stage 4 must still combine these with the authorised human baseline from Stage 1R and the flourishing model from Stage 2. Stage 3 alone does not decide what education is for.

---

## What should readers not conclude yet?

Do not conclude that AI will follow one inevitable path.

Do not conclude that AGI timelines have been established.

Do not conclude that coding agents will replace software engineers.

Do not conclude that robots will arrive on software timelines.

Do not conclude that AI has solved medicine, science or engineering.

Do not conclude that governance frameworks make AI safe.

Do not conclude that this stage decides curriculum, subjects, capabilities, pedagogy or assessment.

---

## Plain-language takeaway

Stage 3 says that the future of AI will depend less on a single intelligence curve and more on where AI can act reliably. Some domains may accelerate quickly because they give AI cheap feedback. Others may remain slow because proof, bodies, infrastructure, law, safety and trust are expensive. That map is now ready for Stage 4, where the project will explore what human life might look like inside different plausible AI-shaped conditions.
