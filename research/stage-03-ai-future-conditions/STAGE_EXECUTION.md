## 2. Stage objective

Stage 3 maps plausible technical trajectories in AI and AI-adjacent technologies so Stage 4 can later construct lived future worlds. It investigates what AI systems are becoming technically capable of, which adjacent technologies they may accelerate, which constraints could slow or redirect them, and which signposts should trigger revision. It does not model life within futures, derive capabilities, design curriculum, recommend tools or forecast one future.

## 5. Research question tree

### 5.1 Primary question

What scientifically plausible AI and AI-adjacent technology trajectories could shape future conditions, and what uncertainties, bottlenecks and signposts should Stage 4 use when constructing future worlds?

### 5.2 Secondary questions

1. How are foundation models changing in capability, architecture, modality, reasoning, memory and tool use?
2. What is technically plausible for coding agents, AI research automation and self-improving systems, and what remains speculative?
3. How might AI change software creation, interfaces and digital work environments?
4. What is plausible in robotics and embodied AI given data, hardware, safety and cost constraints?
5. Where is AI already accelerating science, medicine and engineering, and where does validation remain the bottleneck?
6. How do compute, chips, energy, data, edge deployment and open/sovereign AI shape possible futures?
7. Which safety, misuse, synthetic media, companion, surveillance or neurotechnology trajectories deserve inclusion or watchlist status?

### 5.3 Adaptive horizon-scan inclusion tests

A discovered trajectory receives its own chapter or formal section only if it:

- has credible source support;
- could materially alter future conditions;
- is not adequately covered by another chapter;
- has plausible development pathways and bottlenecks;
- matters for Stage 4 world-building.

Trajectories that are credible but not yet central are recorded as watchlist items.

| Signpost identification | Future-facing claims need revision discipline. | What evidence should change the stage. | Automated policy response. | Signpost register. |
| Technical condition bands | Stage 4 needs inputs, not predictions. | Plausible clusters of technical futures. | Lived-world scenarios. | Pass-forward bands. |

## 13. Reopened synthesis protocol — 23 June 2026

Stage 3 was reopened because the existing report was a compressed technical scaffold rather than a fully developed specialist synthesis. The next pass must use the existing Stage 3 source rows as the evidence base and must not pre-commit to a central thesis, chapter sequence or final report structure before specialists have spoken and discussed the material.

### 13.1 Cognitive quality protocol

All specialist agents must use the project-level **Agent Cognitive Quality Protocol** in `AGENTS.md`. Outputs must be public and structured, not hidden chain-of-thought. Each specialist must explicitly state:

- authorised source IDs;
- scope and exclusions;
- central mechanism or tension;
- empirical findings, projections, uncertainties and rejected overclaims;
- bottlenecks and counterarguments;
- surprising or under-discussed insight;
- possible narrative role without imposing final chapter structure;
- what the Master Coordinator should preserve, weaken or exclude.

### 13.2 Durable logging protocol

Because this synthesis may exceed a single context window, every major step must be logged before moving on.

Use `STAGE_LEDGER.csv` canonical record types as follows:

| Process item | Ledger record type |
|---|---|
| Specialist talk | `traceability` |
| Discussion point | `challenge` |
| Editorial decision | `decision` |
| Weakened claim | `challenge` |
| Open question | `uncertainty` |
| Resume marker | `decision` |

`STAGE_EXECUTION.md` carries the readable progress state. `STAGE_LEDGER.csv` carries structured evidence and decision records.

### 13.3 Specialist talk prompt

> Using only your assigned Stage 3 source rows, explain what your evidence most deeply reveals about where AI technology may be heading. Do not assume the final report structure. Do not force your material into the user’s initial categories if the evidence suggests a different shape. Identify mechanisms, bottlenecks, surprises, tensions, open questions, and what other specialists need to understand.

Each talk must include:

- authorised source IDs;
- central mechanism or tension;
- current state;
- plausible trajectories;
- bottlenecks;
- counterarguments;
- underestimated or surprising insight;
- what another specialist domain must understand;
- possible narrative role;
- claims to pass forward;
- claims to weaken or reject;
- what must not be overclaimed.

### 13.4 Cross-agent discussion rules

After specialist talks, the Master Coordinator chairs a discussion. Agents should respond only when they can:

- connect two domains;
- challenge an overclaim;
- identify a shared bottleneck;
- explain why a technical pathway should move earlier or later in the eventual narrative;
- flag a claim that should be weakened;
- identify a Stage 4 handoff implication.

No generic agreement. No repetition. No central thesis or chapter order until after discussion.
