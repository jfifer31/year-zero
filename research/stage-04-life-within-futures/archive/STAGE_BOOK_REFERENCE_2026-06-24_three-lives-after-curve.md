# Three Lives After the Curve

## The Fork in the Curve

Nobody in these pages knows they are living in a future.

They do not wake in the morning and think about grand theories of technology and society. They wake because someone is calling from downstairs, because a younger brother has lost one shoe, because the rent reminder has arrived, because rain has found a way through a roof, because a parent needs help, because a friend has sent a voice note that sounds wrong, because school starts at half past eight and the bus is late again.

That is how the future arrives for most people. Not as a thesis. As weather. As an app that works until it does not. As a form that fills itself and still leaves you responsible. As a video nobody can prove. As a job title that did not exist when you were fourteen. As a teacher praising the wrong thing. As a hospital letter rewritten in language you can understand. As a door that knows everyone except you.

These are three possible lives, each beginning in 2029, each returning in 2037, each ending in 2045. They share the same years because the years are the experiment. The people are different. The worlds are different. In one, artificial intelligence improves steadily and becomes ordinary infrastructure, uneven but recognisable. In another, progress accelerates sharply and society manages, imperfectly, to build enough public systems, verification, coordination and care to keep up. In the third, the same acceleration arrives faster than trust, law, work and meaning can absorb it.

The dates are not prophecies. They are stages on a map.

The lives are the point.

# Part I — The Linear World

## 2029 — The Homework Looked Too Clean

Maya Hussain was fourteen the first time a teacher accused the wrong person.

It happened in English after lunch, when the classroom smelled of damp blazers and deodorant sprayed too close to skin. Rain ticked against the windows. Someone had drawn a tiny face on the corner of the whiteboard and Miss Carter had not noticed yet. Maya sat three rows back beside Tia Morgan, who had arranged her highlighters in colour order, and behind Ella Price, who was whispering into her sleeve because her school-approved assistant had misunderstood “rewrite this like I’m not arsed” and produced something with the phrase “muted disengagement.”

They were doing speeches. Persuasive writing. The topic was whether public transport should be free for under-eighteens. Everyone had been told to draft at home, upload the speech, and be prepared to read one paragraph aloud.

Miss Carter had printed three anonymous extracts. She said she wanted to talk about voice.

The first one was obviously Dylan’s because it said “moreover” twice, and Dylan had learned the word moreover the previous Tuesday and started using it like a new haircut. The second was Tia’s, though Miss Carter did not say so, because it was neat and slightly bloodless and had exactly the kind of rhetorical question Tia would use after watching a revision video.

The third was lovely. Too lovely. The sentences rolled. The buses became “small democratic rooms on wheels.” The rain on the windows became “the weather pressing its face against public life.” The conclusion made half the class groan because it was good in a way that annoyed them.

Miss Carter looked tired before she even began. “Now,” she said, holding up the page, “this is an example of what happens when we outsource our thinking.”

A few people turned around. Jonas Reed, near the radiator, went red.

Maya felt it before she understood it: the small wrongness in the room, like a picture hung slightly tilted. Jonas did draw well. He also wrote like that sometimes. Not always. Only when he forgot someone might read it.

Miss Carter asked him to stay behind.

After class, everyone had something to say. Ella said he was stupid for not “dumbing it down.” Tia said Miss Carter probably had a detector. Dylan said detectors were fake. Someone else said Jonas’s mum paid for the private tutor that rewrote everything in your own “style profile,” which sounded made up but not impossible.

Maya said nothing. She had watched Jonas’s hands as he packed his bag. He had folded the paper once, twice, then again until it was a small square. She knew embarrassment when she saw it. Not guilt. Embarrassment.

That afternoon, school went on as if nothing had happened. Maths, where the online platform gave hints so generous that half the class could finish without understanding. Science, where Mr Baines showed them an AI-generated animation of mitosis and then spent ten minutes trying to make the projector recognise his laptop. Tutor time, where they were reminded that AI use must be “responsible, transparent and learning-focused,” which meant nothing and sounded like a poster.

The school itself looked almost exactly as it had when Maya arrived in Year 7. Same corridors, same bells, same laminated behaviour system, same radiator that clanked in Humanities, same girls crying in the toilets during friendship emergencies, same boys kicking bottle caps under the benches. The future had not arrived like a spaceship. It had arrived as another login.

Maya lived on the edge of Sheffield, close enough to the city to feel its pull, far enough out that buses, money and who owned a decent laptop still decided more than people admitted. Her family lived in a small semi-detached house on a road of pebble-dash, wheelie bins, doorbell cameras and one elderly neighbour who swept the pavement every Saturday as if standards were the only thing keeping the country together.

Her mother, Samina, worked in NHS administration. In 2029 she still mostly handled appointment letters, patient calls, referrals and missing forms, though half her day had become arguing with automated systems that confidently filed things in the wrong place. She was practical, funny when tired, and convinced that if Maya kept her head down and got decent grades she would “have choices.” She used that phrase often enough that Maya had started to hate it.

Her father, Imran, was a mechanic at a commercial garage that serviced vans, delivery fleets and council vehicles. He was good with his hands, patient with engines, less patient with software dashboards that told him what the engine had already told him by sound. He liked old car shows, sweet tea, and saying “they’ve made this stupid on purpose” whenever a subscription diagnostic tool refused to unlock a repair procedure.

Maya’s younger brother, Ayaan, was eight, bright, restless, obsessed with football statistics and battle games. He talked to AI systems more naturally than he talked to adults. Maya loved him, resented him, protected him, and occasionally wanted to leave him on a bus.

At home that afternoon, Samina was at the kitchen table with her work headset around her neck, not on a call but not free either. The NHS portal had changed again. She was staring at a patient record and rubbing the side of her phone with her thumb.

“You’re back early,” she said, though Maya was not.

“A teacher said Jonas used AI but I don’t think he did.”

“Who’s Jonas?”

“No one.”

Samina gave her the look mothers give when “no one” has already become someone. “Did he?”

“I don’t know. Maybe. But I don’t think so.”

“Then he can show his drafts.”

Maya opened the fridge and looked inside without wanting anything. “What if he doesn’t have drafts?”

“Then he learns to keep drafts.”

This was such an adult answer that Maya shut the fridge harder than necessary.

Later, she sat on her bed with her blazer still on and opened her own speech. It was fine. It had a joke about buses being late because they were “expressing their individuality,” which Ella had liked. But now it looked wrong to her: too normal in some places, too improved in others. She had asked the assistant for “better verbs” and it had given her “accelerate,” “liberate,” “transform.” She had changed some back. Had that counted? Would Miss Carter think the bus joke was hers? It was hers. Mostly. Ella had helped. The assistant had suggested “vehicular betrayal,” which made them laugh for five minutes and did not make it into the final.

Ayaan came into her room without knocking, holding his tablet.

“Can you make it do Rashford but as a dragon?”

“No.”

“Maya.”

“No.”

“You did it yesterday.”

“That was different.”

He climbed onto the bed anyway and leaned against her shoulder. His hair smelled of the mango shampoo their mum bought in bulk. On the tablet, an assistant waited in a rounded little box, patient and blank. Ayaan spoke to it like a cousin. “Make a football dragon but not scary and with boots.”

The image appeared in stages: wings, shirt, smoke, boots with impossible studs. Ayaan laughed with his whole body.

Maya watched him save it without wondering who had made it. For him, the question did not exist yet. He had asked. The thing had appeared. He loved it. That was enough.

At dinner, Imran complained that the new fleet diagnostics system had locked him out of a van because the subscription had expired.

“Engine’s right there,” he said, pointing with his fork as if the van were in the kitchen. “I can hear what’s wrong with it. But no, some cloud thing in Rotterdam says I’m not authorised to know.”

Nani, visiting for the evening, said clouds were for rain and people should stop putting important things in them.

Maya laughed. Samina laughed too, too loudly, and then her work phone buzzed. The AI scheduler had moved two clinics into the same room again.

“See?” Imran said. “Artificial intelligence.”

“Artificial, yes,” Samina said, picking up the phone. “Intelligence, we’ll see.”

Maya took that line to school the next day and Ella repeated it to three people as if it were hers.

Jonas came back to English and did not look at anyone. Miss Carter did not apologise, exactly. She said, “In future, I need to see process evidence from everyone.” Then she gave the class a new rule: all written assignments must include planning notes, version history, and a short reflection on tool use.

Everyone groaned.

Ella whispered, “So now we fake that too.”

Maya tried not to laugh. Miss Carter saw her and looked disappointed, which was worse than angry.

At lunch, Maya found Jonas behind the sports hall drawing the caretaker’s trolley. Not the whole trolley, just one wheel and the shadow underneath. He did not look up when she stood beside him.

“I liked the bus thing,” she said.

He shaded the tyre. “Everyone liked it too much.”

“That’s annoying.”

“Yeah.”

“Did you use it?”

He knew what she meant. Everyone knew now. “For spelling.”

“That’s allowed.”

“Apparently not if you’re weird.”

Maya sat on the low wall. The concrete was cold through her tights. Across the yard, Ella was showing people Ayaan’s football dragon and claiming her “little cousin” made it with a jailbreak. Tia was revising for a biology quiz nobody else remembered. Boys chased each other with wet leaves.

Jonas said, “My dad says soon nobody will care who made anything.”

Maya thought about this. It sounded too big, so she brought it down to size. “Your dad sounds cheerful.”

“He’s a graphic designer.”

“Ah.”

“He says if I want to draw I should learn plumbing.”

“My dad would agree. He says pipes are honest.”

Jonas smiled, barely. “Pipes leak.”

“Still honest.”

They sat there until the bell rang.

That evening, Maya did something she did not tell anyone about. She opened her speech file and wrote a paragraph without the assistant. No suggestions. No better verbs. No outline. It was slower and worse and more hers, though after a while she could not tell whether that mattered or whether she only wanted it to matter because Jonas had looked so ashamed.

She wrote about waiting at the bus stop near Nani’s, where the shelter had one cracked panel and the timetable was always wrong. She wrote about how adults with cars talked about buses as if buses were an idea, not a place where you stood in the rain with your schoolbag getting heavier and someone’s music leaking from their headphones. She wrote that free bus travel would not transform young people’s lives, because teachers loved the word transform and it made everything sound fake. It would just mean you could go somewhere without asking.

When she read it aloud two days later, nobody clapped because classes did not clap unless forced. But Miss Carter looked at her differently. Not amazed. Just awake.

Afterwards, she wrote one comment at the bottom: “This sounds like you.”

Maya folded the paper once and put it in her bag. She did not know why the comment made her so pleased. It was only four words. It did not give her a grade. It did not prove anything.

Still, on the bus home, with condensation running down the window and Ella sending six voice notes about a fight in PE, Maya kept taking the paper out and looking at it.

This sounds like you.

In 2029, that was still a thing a person could say and mean.

## 2037 — Exceptions

Maya’s job title was Service Resolution Associate, which made it sound as if she resolved services. What she mostly resolved was confusion.

She was twenty-two and worked on the second floor of a council-health partnership building that used to be a bank. The vault downstairs had become secure records storage. The marble counter remained in reception because nobody could afford to remove it and because older people trusted marble. Every morning Maya walked past it with a coffee she could not really afford and a badge that opened some doors but not others.

The system assigned cases by confidence score.

Green cases went through automatically. Appointment changes, prescription reminders, equipment deliveries, travel reimbursements, basic care-plan updates. The AI handled those with a smoothness that still impressed people who remembered being on hold.

Amber cases came to people like Maya. Contradictions, complaints, missing documents, language issues, family disputes, safeguarding flags, patients who did not respond properly, patients who responded too much, forms where the facts fit but the person did not.

Red cases went to senior staff, legal, clinicians or social workers, depending on which part of the machine had started smoking.

Maya lived in amber.

By 2037, most jobs had not vanished in the clean dramatic way people used to argue about. They had been hollowed, stretched, merged, renamed and wrapped around systems. Entry-level admin was thinner. Customer service still existed, but many human workers handled exceptions, anger, ambiguity and accountability. Offices were smaller. More people worked through platforms, agencies, public-private service contracts and AI-managed workflows. The phrase “human in the loop” survived mostly because nobody had found a better way to describe being blamed last.

That Tuesday began with a man called Mr Kenwright, seventy-nine, marked as “refusing mobility adaptation.” The generated case summary said he had declined grab rails three times and was therefore at increased fall risk. The recommended action was “motivational engagement call with compliance framing.”

Maya hated phrases like that. They sounded like putting a hand over someone’s mouth.

She opened the call transcript. Mr Kenwright had not refused grab rails. He had refused white plastic grab rails because they reminded him of the hospital where his wife died. He wanted metal ones like the old rail by his back step. The procurement system had treated this as preference complexity and reissued the standard offer twice.

Maya looked at the clock. Her dashboard wanted eighteen amber closures before lunch. She had done three.

She called him anyway.

Mr Kenwright answered with suspicion, as if the phone itself had offended him. Ten minutes later he was telling her about his wife’s roses. Fifteen minutes later he admitted he had nearly fallen getting out of the bath. Twenty minutes later Maya had messaged a local installer, overridden the standard equipment code, and written a note she hoped nobody would audit too closely.

The system prompted: “Would you like to summarise this call?”

“No,” Maya said aloud, though nobody was near her desk.

Her colleague Priya looked over the partition. “Talking to the box again?”

“It’s being rude.”

“It learns from you.”

“Then it should know better.”

Priya laughed and went back to her own queue.

At lunch Maya met Ella outside a noodle place that had replaced the betting shop near the tram stop. Ella wore a black jacket with a tiny camera pin she said was for “customer protection” and Maya said was for “management spying.” She worked as a floor lead at a hotel where half the booking issues were handled by AI and the other half by Ella smiling at furious people until they remembered they were in public.

“You look dead,” Ella said.

“You look expensive.”

“Fake expensive. Important difference.”

They ordered the cheapest bowls and sat by the window. Outside, a delivery robot waited at the crossing behind three human pedestrians, patient and slightly pathetic. A teenage boy kicked it, not hard enough to damage it, just hard enough to prove he existed. The robot made a polite warning sound. Nobody looked surprised.

Ella said, “You still doing old people admin?”

“Care coordination.”

“Same thing with lanyards.”

“What do you do? Smile at rich men whose rooms aren’t ready?”

“Experience recovery.”

Maya snorted broth.

They talked about Tia, who had finished university and was doing a graduate scheme in regulatory analytics. She sent voice notes at midnight about being “behind” despite having achieved every measurable thing a person could achieve. They talked about Jonas, who was making physical signs, murals and bespoke interiors for places that wanted “human-made authenticity” and could pay for it. They did not talk about the fact Maya had kissed him twice the previous winter and then stopped replying properly because everything felt too uncertain and she hated being uncertain in front of someone who noticed.

Ella stirred noodles around her bowl. “My brother’s been laid off again.”

“From the warehouse?”

“Not laid off. Reclassified. They love a new word. He’s on flexible maintenance response now.”

“What does that mean?”

“He waits at home until robots break.”

“That sounds…”

“Crap? It is crap.”

Maya thought of her dad, who had adapted better than he admitted. The garage now serviced electric vans, delivery pods, council maintenance bots and old diesel vehicles kept alive by stubborn tradesmen. Imran complained constantly about software locks, but younger mechanics came to him when sensors disagreed with sound. He had become valuable because he did not trust dashboards completely.

Ella said, “School never told us half the jobs would be arguing with machines.”

“School barely told us how to argue with people.”

“You were good at that.”

“No I wasn’t.”

“You were. Quietly. You always knew when someone was chatting rubbish.”

Maya wanted to dismiss this but kept it. Praise still embarrassed her unless it arrived sideways.

Back at work, her afternoon case involved a woman whose benefits review had been flagged because her care-work income, rent support and irregular platform payments did not match predicted household patterns. The system suggested possible fraud. The woman’s name was Alina. She had uploaded seven documents, three of them wrong because the portal kept asking for “earnings evidence” without saying from which employer, and one of her employers existed only as a subcontractor inside an app.

Maya read the generated summary. It was tidy, plausible and almost useless.

She called Alina, who answered while walking somewhere windy. A child cried in the background. Alina spoke fast, apologised often, and kept saying, “I sent it already.” Maya could hear the shape of panic: not dramatic, just worn.

The AI assistant filled the side panel with suggested questions.

Confirm identity.

Ask for current address.

Clarify income period.

Request missing documentation.

Maya muted the suggestions.

“Alina,” she said, “I believe you sent it. I’m trying to work out why they can’t see it.”

There was silence. Then the woman exhaled, and in that sound Maya heard the whole stupid cruelty of systems that made people prove the same thing until they sounded guilty.

By five-thirty, Maya had closed eleven cases, not eighteen. Her productivity marker glowed yellow. Priya’s was green. Two new policy updates appeared in her inbox. The system reminded her that unresolved amber cases increased downstream risk exposure.

She stood up, stretched her back, and looked around the office. Rows of people wearing headsets. Screens full of summaries, risk flags, generated empathy prompts, compliance timers. Humans in the loop. Humans as the loop. Humans as the soft part where the hard parts failed.

On the tram home, she saw a schoolgirl in uniform using her phone camera to scan a worksheet. The girl frowned, listened to whatever explanation came through her earbuds, then copied something into an exercise book. Beside her, two boys argued about whether a goal clip was real or generated. An older woman watched them with open disapproval. None of this felt futuristic. It felt like Tuesday.

At home, Samina had made dal and rice and was watching a drama on mute while her own assistant read out tomorrow’s appointment conflicts. She had moved to part-time after her department restructured. Officially she now handled “patient pathway exceptions,” like Maya. Unofficially she trained new systems to do the work she used to do, then corrected them when they misunderstood people with accents, grief, dementia, bad Wi-Fi or complicated lives.

Ayaan, now sixteen, came in wearing his college hoodie and dropped his bag in the hallway.

“Don’t leave that there,” Samina said.

“I won’t.”

“You have.”

“In a minute.”

He opened the fridge and asked the household assistant what snacks had “high protein but not depressing.” Maya watched him, tall now, all elbows and confidence, moving through AI as if it were air. He did not ask whether it made him lazy or clever. He asked whether it could make the thing he wanted easier.

Later, Maya sat on the back step with her dad while he smoked half a cigarette he claimed not to be smoking. The summer evening was warm. Somewhere a neighbour’s robot mower nudged helplessly at the same patch of fence.

“You like your job?” Imran asked.

Maya considered lying. “Sometimes.”

“That’s better than most.”

“Sometimes I think I’m just cleaning up after systems people pretend work.”

He nodded. “That is many jobs.”

“Did you know that when I was at school?”

“I knew work was mostly fixing things other people designed badly.”

Maya laughed. “You could’ve told me.”

“You wouldn’t have listened.”

He was right. At fourteen she had thought adult life would arrive with categories. Job, home, person, plan. Instead it arrived as a queue of amber cases.

Her phone buzzed. Jonas.

You free Saturday? Need help choosing colours for a café wall. Owner wants “local but premium” and I may walk into traffic.

Maya smiled before deciding whether to smile.

She typed: Sounds serious. What colour is local?

He replied: Damp brick? Greggs bag? Existential grey?

She laughed properly, and her dad looked over.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

But it was not nothing. It was a small opening. Adult life, she was learning, was not built from the big decisions people asked about at school. It was built from replies, calls, meals, missed deadlines, yellow productivity markers, someone believing you had sent the form, someone asking about colours because they trusted your eye.

That night, Maya dreamed about the bus speech she had written at fourteen. In the dream, Miss Carter’s comment had changed.

This sounds like you had become This proves it was you.

She woke before dawn with the uncomfortable sense that those were not the same thing.

## 2045 — Whose System Knows You

By thirty, Maya had learned that every system had a personality, though officially none of them did.

The council-health platform was cautious, defensive, obsessed with audit trails. Her bank assistant was falsely friendly and became colder whenever she challenged a fee. The supermarket system knew what she bought when sad and offered discounts with the tact of a drunk aunt. Her housing cooperative’s maintenance agent was slow but honest. The private medical concierge used by people in the richer part of the city sounded like a calm doctor in a film and could get a dermatology appointment in forty-eight hours.

Maya did not have that one.

She had the public integrated care system, which was better than it used to be and worse than it claimed. She also had herself, which many people had, though not everyone trusted it.

She was now a senior resolution lead for North Sheffield Care and Access, responsible for a team of human reviewers who handled disputed AI decisions in health, housing adaptation, elder support, disability services and family care. The title sounded important enough that her mother printed the appointment message and stuck it on the fridge. The pay was decent if she did not think about London. The work mattered if she did not think too long about why it existed.

She lived in a flat above a bakery with Jonas, who made physical interiors for places trying not to look generated. He had become quietly successful. Not rich, but booked. His clients wanted hand-painted signs, imperfect tiles, reclaimed wood, murals with local references, menus that looked as if someone had argued over them. The more synthetic the world became, the more some people paid for evidence of touch.

Maya teased him about selling authenticity to people who would photograph it for feeds verified by commercial provenance systems.

He said, “You literally sell trust for a living.”

“I rent it. Very different.”

They were not married. They had talked about children in the indirect way people talk when both want and fear the answer. Maya liked children. She liked going home after borrowing other people’s more.

On the morning the chapter opens, Maya is late because the building door refuses to recognise her.

It is raining. The bakery downstairs is venting warm air that smells of cardamom buns. Maya has a meeting at nine with a regional audit panel, and her building access app has decided her identity token expired at midnight. She stands under the narrow awning while the door camera blinks.

“Manual override requested,” the building agent says.

“I know. I requested it.”

“Please confirm your resident phrase.”

“Blue cups in the cupboard.”

“Voice confidence insufficient.”

Maya stares at the camera. “I have lived here for five years.”

“Please remain calm.”

That is when she starts kicking the bottom of the door. Not hard. Just enough.

Mrs Alvarez from the second floor opens it from inside wearing slippers and a raincoat.

“System again?”

“System again.”

“You should use the private verifier my son installed.”

“I work for the public service. I’m making a political point.”

“You’re wet.”

“Also that.”

On the tram, Maya reviews the audit file through her glasses. The case concerns a fifteen-year-old boy, Callum, whose school attendance intervention has triggered a family-support review. The system has combined attendance records, behaviour notes, council tax arrears, parental work instability and neighbourhood risk scoring into a recommendation for mandatory family compliance sessions.

Maya dislikes the smell of it immediately.

Not because the system is necessarily wrong. That is the difficult part. The boy has missed school. His mother has missed appointments. The household is under stress. There may be real need. But the generated summary has the smoothness she distrusts most: every fact lined up in the same direction, no friction, no embarrassment, no human mess. A story too clean.

She opens the raw notes.

Callum’s father left in 2042. His mother works night shifts supervising automated cleaning fleets in a hospital. Callum is often late because he takes his younger sister to breakfast club when the neighbour cannot. Behaviour note: “low engagement; frequently tired; suspected AI completion of written work.” Teacher comment: “Verbally capable when engaged.” Attendance officer comment: “Mother defensive.” Portal note: “Household slow to respond to digital nudges.”

Maya closes her eyes.

The tram passes her old school. The fence is new, higher, with a visitor-verification kiosk by the gate. Otherwise the building looks the same: brick, glass, rain, a cluster of pupils in black blazers moving with the same mixture of urgency and reluctance. For a second she sees herself there, fourteen, holding a printed speech like proof of life.

Her own school records probably still exist somewhere, folded into training data or archived in a permissions layer. Grades. Attendance. Conduct points. Tool-use declarations. A teacher comment: This sounds like you. She wonders if any system would know what that meant.

At work, the office has fewer desks than in 2037 and more small rooms for calls nobody wants overheard. Her team includes Priya, now part-time after having twins; a former teacher called Marcus; a young reviewer named Saf who trusts AI summaries too much because she grew up with better ones; and Daniel, who used to work in fraud detection and sees deception everywhere.

Their job is not to reject the machine. Their job is to keep it from becoming the only witness.

The morning meeting is about throughput. It is always about throughput when nobody wants to say money.

A regional manager appears on the wall display from Leeds, face softened by bandwidth correction. He thanks them for “maintaining human assurance capacity” and explains that amber-case volume has risen eighteen percent after the latest policy expansion. There are new tools to help reviewers decide faster. Confidence scoring has improved. Suggested resolution language is now “trauma-informed.” Appeals can be clustered by pattern.

Maya listens, taking no notes.

When questions open, she asks, “Are we getting more home visits?”

The manager smiles carefully. “The new remote-assessment tools should reduce the need for travel.”

“That wasn’t my question.”

A pause. Priya looks down. Marcus looks pleased.

The manager says home visits are expensive, risk-assessed, and not always necessary.

Maya says, “Neither are wrong decisions.”

This is the sort of line people quote later if you have enough status and call “difficult” if you do not. Maya has acquired just enough status to survive it, though not enough to enjoy it.

After the meeting, Saf comes to her desk. She is twenty-four, bright, kind, and permanently accompanied by three AI overlays because she hates missing anything.

“I looked at the Callum case,” Saf says. “The recommendation is within policy.”

“I know.”

“The household pattern is high-risk.”

“I know.”

“Then what’s the issue?”

Maya turns her screen so Saf can see the raw notes. “What does his mum do?”

“Night-shift fleet supervision.”

“And what time is he late?”

Saf reads again. Something shifts in her face. Not a revelation. Just a small human re-weighting.

“Oh,” she says.

“Yeah.”

The world, Maya thinks, is mostly hidden in the oh.

At lunch, she walks to her mother’s house. Samina has retired early, officially for health reasons, unofficially because the last restructuring took the part of the job she understood and left the part where she had to apologise for systems she could not change. She volunteers now at a community advice room attached to the library, helping older people read letters from agencies that claim to be written in plain English.

Nani has died by then. The house still contains her: a tin of sewing things, a prayer mat, recipes nobody follows correctly, the insistence that guests must eat.

Ayaan, twenty-four, is there too, visiting between contracts. He works in sports analytics for semi-professional clubs, mostly translating AI performance models into things coaches will accept. He is charming, restless, and better paid than Maya expected. He still leaves bags in hallways.

Over lunch, Samina asks whether Maya has thought more about children.

Maya says, “Subtle.”

“I am being subtle. If I was not subtle you would know.”

Ayaan grins into his plate.

Maya says she and Jonas are talking.

Samina says talking is good but not forever.

Maya wants to say the world is expensive, childcare is a maze, work is heavy, she is tired, Jonas wants to but worries, she wants to but worries, everything feels like opening a door to a room she cannot see. Instead she says, “I know.”

Her mother reaches across and squeezes her wrist. “No one is ready.”

This is both comforting and useless, as many true things are.

In the afternoon, Maya visits Callum’s flat.

Remote assessment has already been done. The home environment score is moderate concern. The fridge image was inconclusive because the camera angle missed the lower shelves. The mother’s stress indicators were elevated. Callum’s school engagement pattern suggested “avoidant orientation.”

The flat itself smells of toast and laundry that took too long to dry. Callum is taller than his file makes him sound, with bitten nails and a fringe he keeps pushing out of his eyes. His sister hides behind the sofa and watches Maya as if Maya might issue a fine.

His mother, Natalie, is pale with night-shift exhaustion and angry before Maya sits down.

“I’ve told them,” Natalie says. “I’ve told them and told them. He takes her in when I’m coming off shift. I can’t be in two places.”

Maya says, “I know.”

“You don’t.”

“No. But I read it.”

Callum looks up then. He has expected another adult who has not read anything.

Maya asks him about school. He shrugs. She waits. Silence is one of the few tools no software has improved.

Eventually he says, “They think I cheat.”

“Do you?”

He gives her the look teenagers give when adults ask stupid questions. “Everyone does.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“Sometimes.”

“What parts?”

He shrugs again, then says, “Writing it posh.”

Maya almost smiles. “Why?”

“Because if I write it normal they say explain more. If it explains more they say it’s not mine.”

There it is again: the old knot in a new mouth.

She asks what he likes. He says nothing. His mother says he fixes things. He glares at her. She says he fixed the neighbour’s food printer and the old stairlift sensor and his sister’s tablet. Callum says that does not count.

Maya says, “Why not?”

“Because it’s just messing about.”

She thinks of her father listening to engines. Jonas mixing paint by eye. Ella calming furious guests before the sentiment monitor catches up. Her mother finding the missing fact in a patient record. All the things that are “just” something until the world breaks and needs them.

On the way back, Maya dictates a recommendation. Not mandatory compliance. Transport adjustment. Young-carer assessment. School meeting with tool-use and practical-skills review. Human attendance officer, not automated nudges. No punitive escalation while family-support plan is active.

The assistant suggests more formal language. She accepts some, rejects most.

At home, Jonas is painting a sample board at the kitchen table. Three shades of green. One is too expensive-looking. One is too dental. One is almost right.

“How was your day?” he asks.

“Door didn’t know me. Manager said assurance capacity. Mum asked about babies. Teenager reminded me school is still weird. I saved democracy.”

“Busy.”

“You?”

“Cafe owner wants handcrafted imperfection but symmetrical.”

“Monster.”

He holds up the sample board. “Which one?”

Maya points to the almost-right green.

“You didn’t even think.”

“I did eight years of training in pretending to think.”

He smiles. She leans against the counter and watches him paint another stroke. His hand is steady. The brush leaves a faint ridge no generator would have put there unless asked.

Later that night, after dinner, after messages, after she checks whether Mrs Alvarez got her prescription delivery, after Jonas falls asleep with one arm over his head, Maya sits by the window and looks down at the street.

A delivery pod moves past in the rain. A teenager on a bike swerves around it. The bakery sign glows warm below. Somewhere a system is predicting tomorrow’s care risks, tomorrow’s traffic, tomorrow’s purchasing needs, tomorrow’s likely complaints. Somewhere else a person is about to be misunderstood by it.

Maya does not think this in grand terms. She does not think agency, verification, recognition, infrastructure. She thinks about Callum’s face when he realised she had read the notes. She thinks about her mother’s hand on her wrist. She thinks about the door refusing to know her and Mrs Alvarez opening it anyway.

In 2045, the world knows more about people than it ever has. It can model, flag, summarise, predict, recommend, translate, generate and remember.

Still, sometimes, someone has to come downstairs and open the door.

# Part II — The Kept Curve

## 2029 — The Year the Homework Started Talking Back

Noor Rahman was late because Sami had hidden one shoe in the washing machine.

Not hidden, exactly. Filed. That was what he said when Farah found it under a wet towel, one black school shoe lying there with the solemnity of evidence. Sami was nine and believed objects belonged in categories. Socks went in drawers, cereal in cupboards, announcements in his notebook, shoes by the door. Except when the categories changed. Today he had decided that things which had touched puddles belonged with things that became clean.

Farah stood in the kitchen holding the shoe between two fingers. Her care uniform was still damp at the shoulders from the rain. “Noor,” she called, in the voice that meant she was already late and trying not to be.

Noor was at the table with her blazer on over pyjamas, one knee pulled up, watching the school homework assistant rewrite her paragraph about An Inspector Calls. It had changed “Sheila begins to understand her responsibility” into “Sheila’s moral awakening functions as a critique of generational complacency.” Noor liked the sound of it and hated it immediately. Nobody in Year 9 said generational complacency unless they wanted to be punched or become a teacher.

“Two minutes,” Noor said.

“You said two minutes six minutes ago.”

“That was a different two minutes.”

The assistant pulsed in the corner of her tablet: Would you like this in a more natural student voice?

Noor stared at it. That was the thing about it. It knew. It knew when it sounded fake. It knew how to make itself sound less fake. Last week Mr Hanley had given a whole assembly about academic integrity, with slides that had probably been made by AI and a face like he was personally holding back the sea.

“You are not in trouble for using support,” he had said, “but you are in trouble if the work is not yours.”

Everyone had nodded as if that cleared things up.

Noor deleted “generational complacency” and typed, Sheila starts to see that acting innocent can still hurt people.

That sounded like her. Mostly.

Sami came in wearing one shoe and one sock, hair flat on one side and wild on the other. “The route has changed,” he said.

“What route?”

“The bus route. The lady voice said roadworks near the Moor. It will make us nine minutes late unless we walk to the tram stop.”

The lady voice was the family’s public assistant, a council-approved one with a blue check that meant it was allowed to help with transport, forms and health appointments but not high-stakes financial decisions, a phrase Noor had once made it define and then regretted. It lived in Farah’s phone and spoke with the cautious brightness of someone trying to calm a dog.

Farah looked at the time and closed her eyes. “Fine. Shoes. Bags. Now.”

In 2029, this was what the future felt like: not flying cars, not robots cooking breakfast, but a small calm voice telling you the bus was ruined before you reached the stop.

Noor lived in Sheffield with her mum, Farah, her dad, Imran, and Sami. Farah worked in social care, mostly with older adults who wanted to stay at home. Imran used to service buses and delivery vans; now he was retraining on electric fleets, route-optimisation systems and semi-autonomous depot tools. Sami was funny, literal, easily overwhelmed by noise, obsessed with train announcements and weather maps. Noor loved fixing things that were not meant to be fixed: cracked phone screens, old headphones, bike chains, cheap lamps, the family toaster. She liked dancing but hated performing unless Leila made her. She liked maths when it felt like a lock clicking open and disliked it when it became neat pages of working for marks.

School looked the same as it had in photos from when Farah was young. Corridors smelled of wet coats and floor cleaner. The radiators clanked. The toilets were places where people filmed things they should not. There were posters about attendance, vaping, consent, resilience, reading for pleasure, AI honesty and knife crime, all peeling at the corners with equal sincerity.

Noor found Leila Price by the lockers, doing eyeliner in the reflection of her phone. Leila’s phone was cracked in a perfect diagonal line that made every face look split by lightning. She said she was keeping it like that for aesthetic reasons, but really her mum had said not until Christmas.

“You finished English?” Leila asked.

“Kind of.”

“Kind of you did it or kind of the robot did it?”

“Kind of Sheila learned not to be a cow.”

Leila snorted and nearly stabbed herself in the eye. “Put that. I dare you.”

Jai Singh passed them with two boys from football, blazer open, hair damp but somehow on purpose. “Morning, Noor,” he said, and kept walking before she could make her face normal.

Leila saw. Leila always saw. “Pathetic.”

“Shut up.”

“He uses Premium Mentor.”

“Who cares?”

“You do. Because you’re shallow and attracted to subscription services.”

Noor shoved her, but not hard. Jai’s family had Premium Mentor, which students were not supposed to use for assessed work but absolutely used for assessed work. It made flashcards, generated practice questions, listened to you explain things aloud, corrected your reasoning and, according to Theo Marlow, could simulate examiners with different levels of meanness. Noor had the school version, which froze whenever too many people logged in and once told her that mitochondria were “the vibes of the cell.”

First lesson was maths. Miss Okafor made them put tablets face down.

“Brains first,” she said. “Machines after.”

Noor liked Miss Okafor because she did not pretend. Some teachers spoke about AI like it was either Satan or a magic pencil. Miss Okafor treated it like a clever student who lied when embarrassed. Useful, but not in charge.

They were doing simultaneous equations. Noor understood the first one, lost the second, got angry at the third, then understood all three at once and felt the tiny private pleasure of a lock opening. She looked around to see if anyone had noticed, which was stupid because of course they had not.

At lunch, Theo showed them a video of a warehouse robot falling into a stack of crates.

“It’s fake,” Leila said.

“No, it’s real. My brother sent it.”

“Your brother sends fake real things.”

“It’s from his depot.”

The robot in the clip bent with a kind of tragic politeness, one arm windmilling as if trying to apologise. A human worker in a yellow vest stepped back, then forward, then back again, unable to decide whether to rescue a machine that cost more than his house.

Noor laughed until she saw the man’s face. He looked scared. Not of being hurt. Of being blamed.

“Dad says they’re getting more semi-auto vans,” she said. “Not driverless. Just annoying.”

“What does that mean?” Leila asked.

“Means the van tells you you’re braking wrong.”

“My mum already does that.”

Theo replayed the robot fall in slow motion. “They’re good in clean spaces. Bad when stuff is weird.”

“Everything is weird,” Noor said.

That afternoon, in English, Mr Hanley handed back their essays. Noor got a 7. Leila got a 6 and said it was because Mr Hanley hated women. Jai got an 8 and looked modest in a way that made Noor want to throw a glue stick.

At the bottom of Noor’s essay Mr Hanley had written: Some strong original phrasing. Be careful not to let the assistant smooth out your best observations.

She read it twice.

She should have felt pleased. She did, partly. But there was another feeling under it, like someone had praised a drawing and then said they could see where her mum had helped. Original phrasing. Best observations. Which bits were hers? The sentence about acting innocent, yes. The thing about the ring as a symbol, maybe not. The conclusion had been half suggested by the assistant, then changed by Noor, then improved by the assistant again. Did that make it hers? Did Mr Hanley know? Did she?

After school, Farah messaged: Can you collect Sami? Care review running late. Sorry jaan.

Noor sent back a thumbs-up and walked through rain that had become personal. Sami’s primary school was louder than hers, all shrieks and tiny coats. He came out with his hood up and his hands over his ears.

“Bad day?” she asked.

“Medium bad.”

“What number?”

“Six point eight.”

“Specific.”

“They changed the lunch menu because of supplier optimisation.”

“Supplier what?”

“The chips didn’t come.”

“Tragedy.”

“It was.”

On the walk home, Sami told her that the school assistant had put him in a reading group based on “fluency profile,” but he liked the old group because Oliver was there and Oliver knew not to touch his pencil case. Noor felt a flare of anger so fast it embarrassed her. It was only a reading group. It was also not only a reading group.

At home, Imran was at the kitchen table watching a training module. On the screen, a cheerful animation showed an electric bus depot as if it were a dollhouse. Coloured lines ran from chargers to vehicles to scheduling systems to maintenance alerts.

“Your dad has homework too,” Farah said, taking off her shoes with a groan.

Imran paused the video. “Except mine tells me I am obsolete in six friendly modules.”

“Dad.”

“I’m joking.”

He was half joking. Noor could tell by the way he rubbed his thumb against the side of his mug.

Later, after dinner, after Sami’s bath argument, after Farah fell asleep on the sofa with one hand still holding her phone, Noor sat on the floor beside the coffee table and opened her science homework. The assistant offered to explain photosynthesis through football, K-pop, cooking, Minecraft or “your personal interests.”

She chose cooking because she did not want a machine knowing her personal interests.

The explanation was good. Annoyingly good. Leaves were kitchens. Sunlight was energy. Glucose was stored food. Stomata were doors that had to open without letting too much water escape. Noor imagined tiny kitchens in every leaf on the spider plant dying heroically by the window.

She looked at Farah asleep, at the blue council assistant badge glowing faintly on her phone screen, at Imran’s paused bus depot, at Sami’s weather maps spread across the carpet, at her own paragraph about responsibility.

The world did not feel like it was ending. It felt like everyone had been given help they did not quite understand how to refuse.

That night Leila sent a voice note: “If Mr Hanley asks tomorrow, I’m saying my essay is spiritually mine.”

Noor laughed into her pillow.

Then she replayed it twice, because Leila sounded happy, and Noor liked having proof.

## 2037 — The Things That Passed Inspection

By twenty-two, Noor had learned that most systems failed in boring ways.

Not dramatic red lights. Not sirens. Not screens flashing ACCESS DENIED while a villain laughed somewhere in the cloud. Mostly, systems failed because the address field did not accept flats above shops. Because Mrs Donnelly’s son filled in the form for her and wrote “independent” when he meant “stubborn.” Because the damp sensor in a bedroom was accurate but the landlord’s contractor uploaded photographs from a different house. Because an AI care planner scheduled a visit at 8:10 each morning without knowing the bus from Burngreave was cancelled twice a week.

Noor’s job was to catch those things before they became someone’s life.

Her badge said Civic Verification Apprentice — Housing, Care and Retrofit Pathway. Leila said it sounded like a job invented by a committee that had never kissed anyone. Noor did not argue. It did.

The office was on the third floor of an old department store that had been turned into a public services hub. Downstairs there was a library, a clinic desk, a legal navigation pod, a warm room in winter, a repair cafe on Thursdays and a little kiosk that sold bad coffee to people emerging from appointments with the stunned look of those who had finally been believed by a form.

The building had three AI systems Noor used every day. The first was the public assistant, which handled intake: applications, summaries, eligibility checks, translation, appointment prep. The second was the planning model, which generated options: care schedules, retrofit plans, adaptations, risk assessments. The third was the verifier, which tried to break the first two.

Noor trusted the verifier most because it was rude.

Potential conflict: proposed ramp angle unsuitable for listed entrance dimensions.

Potential missing context: applicant reports dizziness but no medication review attached.

Potential dignity issue: care schedule permits hygiene visit but no meal preparation despite stated fatigue pattern.

Dignity issue. That was new language since the 2034 reforms. Before that, systems had optimised for cost, time and risk. Then there had been a scandal in Leeds: thousands of care plans technically compliant, emotionally brutal. People put to bed at 6:15 p.m. because route optimisation decided darkness counted as evening. A parliamentary inquiry followed. Everyone said lessons had been learned. Noor’s mum said lessons were always learned after the people who needed them had already been taught something else.

Farah still worked in care, though now she called herself a “home continuity worker,” which made her laugh so much she had to sit down when the new contracts came in. The AI did notes, medication reminders, mobility prompts, family updates, risk flags and loneliness checks. Farah did the things it could not: noticed when someone had stopped making tea because they no longer saw the point, knew which daughters needed bluntness and which needed gentleness, remembered that Mr Iqbal liked the window open even in February because he had grown up in a house where fresh air meant health.

“It helps,” Farah admitted once, as if confessing to a crime. “But if they cut visits because it helps, then it doesn’t help.”

Noor remembered that. She wrote versions of it in comments no one thanked her for.

On the morning the chapter opens, Noor is assigned a review case in Darnall. A woman called Rosa Mendez has applied for home adaptations after a fall. The planning model recommends a low-cost package: bathroom rails, stair sensors, medication prompts, weekly physio check-in, no structural changes. The risk score is moderate. The budget impact is low. The verifier raises two concerns but nothing severe.

Noor visits because something in the file bothers her. Not a grand instinct. A small itch. The daughter’s messages are too polished. The photographs show clean rooms but no clutter at all, which usually means someone cleaned before the assessment or someone is hiding how they live.

Rosa’s flat is above a closed betting shop. The stairs smell of damp plaster and old smoke. Inside, everything is neat in the way of people who are trying to prove they deserve help. Rosa is seventy-one, hair pinned back, cardigan buttoned wrong. Her daughter Elena joins by video from Manchester, apologising three times in the first minute because she is at work and her lunch break is short.

The public assistant translates when Rosa slips into Spanish. Noor understands some Urdu from home, school French she cannot use and enough Spanish from the assistant’s captions to know when the tone is not matching the words.

The plan looks fine until Rosa shows Noor the bathroom.

There is a step. A stupid, ugly, two-inch step between the hallway and the bathroom, tiled the same colour as the floor, almost invisible unless you are looking down. Rosa fell there, not on the stairs. The model has seen photographs of the bathroom. It has identified the rails, the sink, the tub, the turning radius. It has not understood the step because the step is visually boring.

Noor crouches and takes a picture with a scale marker.

The assistant says in her ear, “Would you like to update hazard annotation?”

“No,” Noor mutters. “I’d like to kick somebody.”

Rosa smiles, not understanding the words but catching the feeling.

On the tram back, Noor messages Theo. They are not dating. They have not dated for six months. They still send each other things because the friendship had survived the romance by becoming stranger and more honest.

Invisible threshold problem again, she writes.

Theo replies with a picture of a robot arm holding a sponge over a sink. My guy spent 3 mins deciding if water is an object.

Theo works for a small robotics maintenance firm that services hospital logistics, eldercare pilots and food warehouses. Robots have improved enormously since the falling-crate videos of 2029, but Theo says the public still imagines either useless metal toddlers or perfect humanoid servants. Reality is more boring and more interesting: machines that are brilliant in mapped corridors, hopeless with unexpected pets, excellent at lifting, bad at guilt, tireless until a seal wears down and nobody ordered the part.

At lunch, Noor meets Leila outside the hub. Leila arrives in a coat that changes pattern when she moves, not expensively but cleverly. She works as a live-events designer, which in practice means she manages mixed human-AI performances for brands, weddings, memorials, local festivals and people who want their thirtieth birthday to look like a music video. She has three hundred thousand followers across platforms, though she says only forty thousand are “real real,” which is now a recognised distinction.

“You look dead,” Leila says.

“You look like a butterfly sponsored by debt.”

“Thank you.”

They buy falafel wraps from a van whose ordering system recognises Leila and offers her “the usual with performance-day sodium adjustment.” Noor finds this creepy. Leila finds it convenient. This is one of the ways they have become different.

Leila is excited because she has been invited to pitch for a civic arts commission: a public memory installation for the old steelworks district, using family archives, generated reconstructions and live testimony. It could be beautiful or awful. Possibly both.

“They need authenticity review,” Leila says, rolling her eyes. “Like I’m going to make fake grandads for fun.”

“People do.”

“Badly.”

“That’s not the point.”

“It is a little bit the point.”

They argue, gently at first, then not. Noor says generated memory needs consent, provenance, source boundaries. Leila says people with money have been editing family history forever; now normal people get tools too. Noor says tools are not the same as permission. Leila says Noor has started speaking in policy.

That hurts because it is partly true.

Leila softens first. “Sorry. I’m just nervous.”

Noor pokes at her wrap. “I know.”

“I want it. Like, actually want it.”

“Then don’t make fake grandads.”

Leila laughs, and for a second they are fourteen again, hiding by the lockers, making fun of words adults used when they did not understand their own rules.

In the afternoon, Noor uploads the bathroom step evidence. The system recalculates. The recommended package changes: floor levelling, temporary wash support, occupational therapy review, daughter consultation, landlord enforcement notice. The budget impact jumps. The verifier flags it as justified. A human supervisor approves it after eleven minutes.

Noor should feel triumphant. Instead she thinks about all the steps nobody photographed.

After work she goes to her parents’ house for dinner. Sami is seventeen now, tall and narrow, still obsessed with weather but more private about it. He is doing an internship with the regional climate-response service, mostly labelling local flood images and checking sensor anomalies. He loves it because weather is allowed to be complicated without being rude.

At dinner, Imran talks about orbital compute.

He has become interested in it through depot forums, where men who used to argue about engines now argue about launch mass, solar efficiency and whether billionaires should be allowed to put server farms above everyone’s heads. There are early platforms, mostly experimental: high-altitude data relays, orbital solar-linked compute trials, defence-adjacent infrastructure nobody explains clearly. The headlines make it sound like intelligence will soon pour from the sky. Theo says half the claims are investor poetry. The other half are worrying.

“Imagine,” Imran says, tearing naan, “all that sunlight. No clouds. No rain. Just power.”

Farah says, “And still they will send me a rota with no lunch break.”

Noor laughs because everyone does, but later she stands in the garden with Sami while he points his phone at the sky. One of the brighter moving dots might be a compute platform. Or a communications satellite. Or nothing special.

“Do you think it’s good?” Sami asks.

“What?”

“All of it.”

She knows he does not mean satellites.

The proper answer would involve access, governance, verification, power, safety. The sister answer is harder.

“I think good things can still squash people if nobody watches where they’re stepping,” she says.

Sami considers this. “Like Rosa’s bathroom.”

Noor looks at him.

“Mum told me,” he says.

“Course she did.”

He grins. “Invisible threshold problem.”

Noor leans her shoulder against his. Above them, the sky is full of moving lights and old stars, and she cannot always tell which is which.

## 2045 — The Pressure in the Pipes

By thirty, Noor no longer trusted any plan that arrived without a list of people it would inconvenience.

This made her annoying in meetings.

The floods came after three days of rain so heavy it seemed personal. Not apocalyptic. People used that word too easily now. This was not the end of the world. It was the world behaving badly in familiar places: drains backing up, ground floors swallowing brown water, traffic rerouted, care visits missed, tempers made sharp by damp socks and insurance portals.

The regional response system had predicted the flood line within twelve centimetres in most districts. That was the miracle everyone talked about on the news. It had failed by nearly a metre on three streets in Attercliffe. That was where Noor spent the morning.

Her job title had changed twice since 2037. The current one was Local Verification Lead, Civic Resilience Network. Leila said it sounded like Noor had been promoted to checking the weather’s homework. The work was part emergency response, part infrastructure triage, part social negotiation, part arguing with models that were usually right and sometimes right in useless ways.

The response room was in the same old department-store hub, though now the top floor had been reinforced, cooled and filled with public compute racks that hummed behind glass. Most heavy processing ran elsewhere: regional centres, national infrastructure, university systems, contracted private capacity, occasional bursts from orbital platforms when the queue justified the cost. The orbital systems were real now, but not magic. They were expensive, politically sensitive and mostly reserved for climate modelling, materials search, defence verification, disaster response and frontier research. They did not beam wisdom into kettles. They provided compute under rules people fought about constantly.

Noor sat at a long table with wet boots, a stale pastry and six live feeds.

One showed flood-depth estimates. One showed road access. One showed care-risk households. One showed building integrity. One showed public reports with confidence scores. One showed the argument log, which was always the most human feed because it recorded where people had said no.

A generated response plan suggested moving temporary housing units to a school car park.

Noor rejected it.

The system asked for reason.

She typed: GCSE exams. Also no accessible toilets after 6 p.m. Also site is where teenagers gather after displacement. Risk of conflict/policing escalation. Use sports centre overflow and mosque hall if consent confirmed.

The system paused for 1.2 seconds, which in 2045 felt like a pointed silence.

Alternative plan generated.

Noor approved three parts, rejected two, sent one to human review and flagged the mosque consent as urgent.

Across the room, a young coordinator named Ben said, “The model’s asking whether we can deprioritise the Cliffe Street retrofit appeal because predicted recovery time is under threshold.”

“No,” Noor said.

“You didn’t look.”

“I know Cliffe Street.”

“It says—”

“I know what it says.”

She did look, because she was not stupid. The system was not wrong exactly. Cliffe Street had lower structural risk than other zones. But the residents were mostly older tenants, many with limited English, and the last time their repairs had been delayed a landlord used the delay to argue the damage was pre-existing. The system knew property records. It did not know grudges unless someone had recorded them in a format grudges could wear.

She added a note. Historical enforcement failure. Prior landlord noncompliance. Community trust risk. Keep appeal active.

Ben watched her. He was twenty-four, good-hearted, too smooth with systems because he had grown up believing they wanted reasons. Noor had grown up knowing systems often wanted you to get tired.

At noon, Leila called.

Noor nearly declined. Then she saw the message preview: It’s Mum’s shop.

Leila’s family ran a hair and beauty supply shop near one of the affected streets. Noor remembered it from school: narrow aisles, wigs on foam heads, nail polish in impossible colours, aunties gossiping near the till, Leila’s mum pretending not to notice when they stole chewing gum and then adding it to their tab.

On video, Leila looked older than thirty for the first time Noor could remember. Not physically. Something in the way she held her mouth.

“The claims assistant says we need provenance on stock value before release of emergency funds,” Leila said.

“Okay. Do you have inventory?”

“It flooded, Noor.”

“Digital?”

“Mum’s system was old.”

“How old?”

“Don’t make that face.”

“I’m not.”

“You are. You’re making your civic face.”

Noor closed her eyes. Around her, the response room moved with controlled urgency. People murmured to agents, agents murmured back, printers spat labels for physical kits because in emergencies paper became useful again.

“Send me what you have,” Noor said.

“I don’t want special treatment.”

“You’re not getting special treatment. You’re getting me being horrible but efficient.”

Leila laughed once, badly.

The emergency funds process had improved since 2037. Public verification could reconstruct some losses from supplier records, payment histories, tax data, photographs, local business registries and customer attestations. It could also trap people in loops when records were incomplete. Intelligence abundance had not eliminated bureaucracy. It had made bureaucracy faster, more capable and sometimes more humiliating because now the system could ask for the exact missing thing in perfect language every twelve seconds.

Noor assigned the case to a small-business recovery pathway and marked herself conflicted. That meant she could not approve funds, but she could help Leila understand the evidence request. Rules mattered more when you loved someone. Otherwise love became one more corruption channel with a nicer voice.

At four, she left the hub for field verification. The streets smelled of rain, petrol, sewage and wet plaster. Drones moved above rooflines, mapping damage. Small delivery robots carried medical packs where roads were closed, escorted by human volunteers because people trusted machines more when someone in muddy trousers walked beside them.

Noor wore council waterproofs and carried a tablet, a physical notebook, two protein bars, spare socks, and Sami’s old habit of checking the sky whenever she stepped outside.

At Cliffe Street, a woman shouted at her before she reached the door.

“You people said it wouldn’t come this high.”

Noor did not say, I didn’t. She had learned that “you people” meant anyone attached to the shape of authority.

“We got it wrong here,” Noor said.

That helped a little. Not enough.

Inside, the waterline marked the wall like a dirty ruler. The resident, Mrs Adeyemi, showed Noor the cupboard where she kept medication, the swollen kitchen units, the plug sockets, the place where her grandson had slipped. The system captured images, estimated repair categories, generated claim packets. Noor asked questions that were not on the form because the form did not know Mrs Adeyemi’s grandson had stopped speaking after the slip, embarrassed by fear.

On the way out, Mrs Adeyemi said, “Are you the computer person?”

Noor almost said no. Then yes. Then something clever about being the person between.

“I’m Noor,” she said. “I check what it misses.”

Mrs Adeyemi nodded as if this was a real job. It was, Noor thought, maybe the realest job she had ever had.

That evening, she went to Leila’s mum’s shop. The shutters were up. Inside, everything smelled of river water and conditioner. Leila stood ankle-deep in ruin, lifting boxes onto a counter already full.

For a while they did not talk. Noor picked up what could be saved. Leila sorted hair extensions, bottles, brushes, packets of lashes gone soft in their cardboard. Generated loss estimates hovered on Noor’s tablet, precise and insufficient.

“Remember when we used to come here after school?” Leila said.

“You used to pretend you worked here.”

“I did work here.”

“You sat on the counter and judged people’s eyebrows.”

“That was brand identity.”

Noor smiled.

Leila’s mother, Nadine, came in carrying tea in a flask because even disaster did not cancel hospitality. She hugged Noor with one arm, hard.

“They say the system can help,” Nadine said.

“It can,” Noor said. “We’ll make sure it does.”

She meant it, and because she meant it, she felt the weight of all the ways it might not.

Later, after the emergency lights came on outside, after Nadine went home, after the first claim bundle was submitted with supplier histories, customer attestations and photos from Leila’s old promotional feeds, Noor and Leila sat on overturned crates behind the counter.

Leila looked around the shop. “I spent years making unreal things look real,” she said. “Now I need a machine to believe my real things existed.”

Noor did not answer quickly. She was tired enough that her thoughts had lost their professional edges.

“Yeah,” she said. “That’s horrible.”

“I thought you’d say something useful.”

“I’m off duty.”

“No you’re not.”

Noor leaned her head back against the wall. “No. I’m not.”

This was the part nobody had explained when people talked about abundance. There was more help now. More intelligence, more medicine, more warning, more translation, more adaptation, more ways to repair. Noor had seen people live who would have died. She had seen homes made safe, cancers caught early, legal threats answered, lonely people connected, flood barriers designed in months instead of years. She believed in the systems. She had given her adult life to them.

But help did not remove need. It revealed it. It multiplied the number of things that could be done and therefore the number of things someone had to choose not to do first.

Her phone buzzed. Sami.

You eaten?

Noor sent back a picture of the protein bar.

He replied: That is a rectangle of sadness.

She laughed so suddenly Leila looked over.

“What?”

“Sami.”

“Tell him I say hi.”

Noor did.

A moment later Sami replied: Hi Leila. Rain band easing 23:40. Also Noor lies about food.

Leila smiled, and for a second the ruined shop held all their ages at once: fourteen with cracked phones, twenty-two with falafel wraps and arguments, thirty with wet feet and systems waiting for reasons.

Outside, drones hummed. Somewhere above the clouds, perhaps, orbital compute platforms turned sunlight into calculations for flood models, protein folding, legal simulations, battery chemistry, defence audits, crop forecasts and things Noor would never understand. Down here, Leila’s mum needed money before the mould set in. Mrs Adeyemi needed repairs. Ben needed to learn when to doubt a threshold. Farah needed Noor to come round on Sunday. Imran needed help with his knee appointment. Sami needed someone to pretend his jokes were annoying.

The future had not become simple. It had become crowded with possible help.

Noor stood, knees stiff.

“Come on,” she said. “Let’s take pictures of everything before your mum starts cleaning.”

Leila groaned. “She already started.”

“Of course she did.”

They moved through the shop, documenting damage. Leila held up a ruined packet of neon nails and said, “Do you think the verifier understands tragedy?”

“No.”

“Good.”

“Why good?”

Leila placed the nails carefully in the salvage pile. “Some things should have to be told.”

Noor looked at her friend, really looked: tired, vain, kind, impossible, still here.

“Then tell it properly,” Noor said.

Leila lifted her phone. “I always do.”

And because the lights were bad and the floor was wet and the world was too much, Noor held up her tablet to help with the glow.

# Part III — The Broken Curve

## 2029 — The Girl in the Video

Leena Iqbal was fourteen when everyone saw the video of Tilly.

It arrived before registration, passed from phone to phone beneath desks and inside blazer sleeves. In it, Tilly Barnes stood outside the changing rooms in her PE shirt, saying things she would say but not in that order, with a smirk that was almost hers and a voice that had the flat little shine of cheap generation. The video was crude if watched carefully.

Nobody watched carefully.

They watched quickly, repeatedly, with hands over mouths.

Leena first saw it on Reece Adeyemi’s phone. He did not show her to be cruel. He showed her because showing was the local currency of alarm.

“Is it real?” he asked.

Leena knew the answer should matter. Instead she felt the horrible secondary thing: that by lunchtime, the question would matter less than the fact of the video having existed.

School continued.

That was one of the first things she learned about catastrophe. It did not stop the timetable. Leena went to science, where Miss O’Neill explained rates of reaction while three girls behind her whispered Tilly’s name. She went to English, where they were meant to discuss a poem about memory and everyone was on the homework platform pretending to annotate. She went to maths and watched Mr Collier threaten to confiscate phones he could not actually remove from the room because half the class used them for access codes, translation, medical alerts, parent messages and quiet AI help they were not supposed to have.

Leena lived above a row of shops near Harehills in East Leeds. Her mother, Samira, worked in NHS patient booking and later in care triage. Her father, Imran, was an electrician who took pride in not needing anyone’s app to tell him how a house was wired. He was funny when relaxed, sharp when money was bad. Her younger brother, Yusuf, was eight, obsessed with goalkeepers and space launches. Her grandmother, Dadi, lived nearby and pretended not to understand voice assistants because she disliked being listened to.

Leena was clever in patches, forgetful in others, quick to spot when someone was lying, slow to admit when she was frightened. She was small for her age and privately furious about it. She had dark hair she straightened badly before school, eczema on the backs of her hands, and a habit of pushing her thumb into the soft bit of her palm when she was trying not to cry.

Her best friend was Tilly: loud, rich in confidence but not money, always on the edge of trouble. Their friendship was built out of shared bus rides, stolen chips, voice notes, and the strange loyalty of girls who knew each other’s home lives too well.

The school had a policy. The policy was printed in colour and displayed near reception. It said synthetic media incidents would be investigated seriously. It did not say what to do when the deputy head could not tell whether a video was fake, the parents had already seen it, the students had saved it, and the safeguarding lead was waiting on a verification service that cost more than the school wanted to spend.

Tilly insisted it was fake. Then she insisted it was “obviously” fake. Then she laughed too loudly and said she did not care. By break, she was in the toilets with Leena, sitting on the closed lid, one trainer pressed against the opposite cubicle door.

“I didn’t say that,” Tilly said.

“I know.”

“You don’t know. You just believe me.”

Leena had no answer to that. She hated Tilly for making the distinction and loved her for needing her to.

The day widened around the incident. Leena’s mother messaged three times because a scam call had used a cousin’s voice asking for money. Her father sent a voice note warning Leena not to answer unknown numbers, then followed it with another telling her to ignore the first one if it sounded weird, which made no sense and made perfect sense. Yusuf wanted help making a goalkeeper montage for school, and the tool kept adding saves he had never made.

Homework that night was a geography essay on coastal erosion. The class group chat filled with versions: human-ish, AI-ish, deliberately bad, polished, translated, shortened. Leena wrote hers herself for twenty minutes, got stuck, asked a free assistant for a structure, changed the words, then felt guilty in a way she could not explain. She had done the work and not done the work. She understood more than before and trusted herself less.

Her father said school was simpler in his day. Her mother said nothing was simple when you were young. Dadi said if someone wanted to shame a girl, they would use whatever tools were available.

The next day Tilly was suspended for fighting, not for the video. She hit a boy who played the clip noise when she walked past. The boy claimed he never touched his phone. Nobody could prove it. His agent-generated behaviour statement was polite, detailed and probably written by his older sister’s subscription assistant.

Leena began carrying a notebook. At first it was for drawings: trainers, rooms, little folded worlds. Then she started writing down things people said and when they said them. Not because she was a natural investigator. Because memory suddenly felt too soft.

At parents’ evening, her English teacher praised a geography paragraph and said it sounded “very mature.” Samira beamed. Leena smiled. She did not say she used an assistant for the plan. She did not know whether that meant the praise was false.

Across the hall, Tilly’s mum was crying quietly beside a display of student poems about identity.

Leena did not think, verification burden is becoming a structuring condition of social life.

She thought: If Tilly says she didn’t, why isn’t that enough?

## 2037 — Proof of Person

Leena was twenty-two and late for work because her building’s entry system had decided she was two people.

The downstairs door recognised her face but not her tenancy token. Her tenancy token recognised her account but not her face. The cheap housing agent told her to blink twice, turn left, remove reflective accessories, repeat the phrase displayed on screen. The phrase was: I confirm this access attempt reflects my present intention.

She said it three times in the stairwell with a bag of laundry over one shoulder.

A neighbour, Mr Pavel, let her out through the maintenance door. He was eighty or maybe sixty-five; people who had spent years fighting housing systems often aged unevenly. He asked if she could look at a letter later. Not a letter, he said, a pre-action eligibility clarification. Leena said yes before checking whether she had time.

Her workplace was not called a call centre. Nobody called anything a call centre now. It was a Resolution Support Hub on the edge of Manchester, run by a contractor that handled overflow verification for councils, insurers, care providers and benefit systems. The office had plants nobody watered, privacy pods with broken seals, motivational wall text about dignity, and a quiet room where staff went after claimants threatened suicide or agents threatened litigation on claimants’ behalf.

Leena’s job was to turn messy lives into acceptable evidence.

By 2037, AI capability had accelerated across software, logistics, media, finance, customer service, legal paperwork, hiring and personal administration. The world had not collapsed, but it had become harder to prove what happened. Agents negotiated bills, filed applications, assembled evidence packs, disputed penalties, scheduled care, produced design options, simulated conversations, made purchases, impersonated customer service, and sometimes acted without anyone understanding the permission chain.

Large firms and wealthy households used verified stacks: audited agents, insurance-backed identity, provenance channels, private security, human review. Poorer households used cheap assistants, open models, cracked agents, grey-market automation and favours from someone who knew someone.

The economy had reorganised around trust. Some jobs vanished into software workflows. Others grew around checking, witnessing, repair, care, physical presence, compliance, dispute resolution, machine supervision and reputation management. A person’s value often depended less on what they could produce than on whether someone believed they could be trusted with a system.

Leena was good at it. She hated that she was good at it.

That morning, a woman said she had attended a hospital appointment. The hospital agent said she had not. Her phone location said she was nearby but not inside. The bus record placed her three minutes away. The clinic camera was down. The woman’s daughter had a video, but the metadata was corrupted. The woman cried because she could not afford to lose transport support.

Leena listened. She asked for small details: what the waiting room smelled like, whether the vending machine was still broken, which nurse called her in. The woman remembered the nurse’s shoes. Leena knew systems did not like shoes. She wrote it down anyway.

Between cases, the wider world leaked in.

A minister denied a leaked recording. The denial was itself flagged as probabilistically manipulated. A supermarket chain paused autonomous delivery in three cities after coordinated agent fraud. A celebrity sued a companion company for training grief models on private family archives. A local hospital announced AI-assisted triage improvements, then quietly added a human appeals queue because the model kept downgrading people who described pain indirectly or in languages the system handled poorly.

Leena did not follow all of it. She caught fragments while eating crisps at her desk, while waiting for a claimant to upload the correct file, while ignoring messages from Yusuf asking whether she could verify an account for “a project.” Yusuf was twenty, funny, restless, underemployed, brilliant at finding side doors in systems that were meant to have no doors. Leena worried about him with the exhausted irritation of an older sibling who had already spent years being right.

Her own life was small in the way young adulthood can be small even when history is large. She shared a flat with two women she liked and one man nobody liked but who always paid rent. She had a complicated almost-relationship with Anika, who worked in food logistics and could make any room feel like a temporary party. She sent money home when she could. She still drew tiny rooms, now in an old design tool that generated materials she could not afford to print.

The central case of the day came when Tilly reappeared.

Leena had not seen her properly in years. Tilly had dropped out of sixth form, worked beauty, delivery, moderation, then vanished into a string of accounts and jobs Leena only half-understood from posts that might not have been hers. Now she appeared in Leena’s queue under a different surname, requesting emergency identity restoration.

At first Leena thought it was coincidence. Then the video call opened and there was Tilly, older but not enough, her hair dyed copper, her face too still.

“I didn’t know it would come to you,” Tilly said.

It should have been against policy for Leena to continue. She should have transferred the case. She almost did. But Tilly was trying not to cry in the same way she had tried not to cry in the school toilets, by making her mouth cruel.

Tilly’s problem was not one fake video now. It was a nest of them. Someone had used her image and voice across debt applications, adult content, delivery accounts and political spam. Some were fake. Some were real. Some were real but taken out of context. Some she could not remember consenting to because the contracts were agent-written and the work was desperate. The system did not need to decide whether she was innocent. It only needed to decide she was high-friction.

Leena asked procedural questions. Tilly gave procedural answers. Underneath, both of them remembered being fourteen.

They did not hug. Leena did not save her. Instead she discovered the limits of her competence. She could help assemble an evidence bundle. She could flag coercive synthetic reuse. She could request human review. She could write a note that sounded official enough to be read. But she could not restore the simple thing Tilly needed in 2029: for being herself to be evidence enough.

Later, Anika told Leena she cared too much.

Leena said, “It’s literally my job.”

“That’s not what I mean.”

Their almost-relationship bent under the pressure of Leena’s inability to leave work at work. Anika liked people, but she did not like being cross-examined by someone who called it noticing. Leena checked messages for tone changes, asked where photos came from, distrusted convenience. Her skill had become a social defect.

The class difference in AI access was everywhere. Anika’s cousin, who worked for a verified corporate stack, had an agent that handled disputes before they became problems. Leena’s clients arrived after systems had already labelled them inconsistent. Wealth did not mean better truth. It meant fewer occasions where truth had to be begged for.

The day ended with Leena visiting her parents. Her father had received an automated warning from an insurer claiming his electrical work might be non-compliant based on image analysis from a property database. He was insulted less by the money than by the machine’s confidence.

“I wired half that street,” he said.

“I know.”

“They can come and look.”

“They won’t.”

“Then what do they want?”

Leena looked at the letter. What they wanted was not truth exactly. They wanted him to respond in the correct format, within the correct window, with evidence their system could digest.

She took out her notebook, the old habit made adult, and started listing what he had.

## 2045 — The Witness

Leena was thirty and owned three kinds of proof.

The first was official: a certified identity stack she paid for monthly and resented more than her electricity bill.

The second was communal: a verification circle run out of the cooperative housing block, where neighbours witnessed documents, recorded disputes, shared trusted devices, archived agreements and taught each other which systems could be challenged and which ones only punished resistance.

The third was private: notebooks in a fireproof box beneath her bed.

People laughed when they saw the notebooks. Then they asked if she had spare paper.

By 2045, intelligence was abundant in some places and rationed in others. Not because models could not answer, but because permission, compute, energy, insurance, authentication, legal status and infrastructure decided who got reliable action. The richest zones had near-continuous AI mediation: health monitoring, legal defence, personal agents, adaptive interfaces, automated logistics, private education layers, predictive maintenance, financial optimisation, domestic robotics in controlled environments, carefully permissioned public spaces. Outside those zones, people lived among fragments: open models running locally, municipal systems, black-market agents, outdated public tools, surveillance vendors, patched devices, community-run verification circles, scams and brittle infrastructure.

There had been no single takeoff the public could point to. Instead, there were stories: the year automated code audits outpaced human review; the year three major identity networks failed; the year private medical discovery clubs were exposed; the year a warehouse strike was broken by remote robotics; the year children started using dead relatives’ voice models like toys; the year nobody knew which minister had actually said what.

The world was not ruined. People still flirted, queued, cooked, grieved, gossiped, decorated their flats, sang badly, borrowed money, kept secrets and fell asleep with lights on. But trust had become labour, and those with least spare labour paid most.

The morning began before dawn. Leena woke because her father’s care allocation had changed overnight. The care system said his mobility score had improved. It had not. An automated home assessment appeared to have used footage from a good day, a repaired stair rail, and a generated compliance summary from a subcontractor that no longer existed under the same name.

Samira was furious in the kitchen, still in her dressing gown, speaking too loudly to an appeals agent.

“No, I am not satisfied with the escalation pathway.”

The agent was calm. Agents were always calm. Calm had become one of the sounds of power.

Leena stepped in not because her mother could not handle it, but because handling it had become a second job and Samira already had one. This was part of 2045: care was abundant in language and scarce in bodies. There were scheduling systems, triage models, companion voices, fall detectors, medication drones in some districts, robotic lift assists in premium care facilities, predictive risk dashboards. But a person still needed another person to notice when an old man was pretending he could stand because he hated being watched.

Imran joked from the next room that he was fine. Nobody answered, because everyone loved him and nobody believed him.

The world outside was bright with systems. Delivery lanes hummed in restricted corridors. Cheap domestic robots existed but were mostly rented, insured and useless in cluttered flats. Public screens displayed authentication marks beside news footage, though people had learned that a mark proved a chain, not a truth. Children grew up asking whether a clip was “clean” before asking whether it was funny.

Leena worked as a witness-mediator for a civic legal cooperative. This meant she spent her days accompanying people through disputes where the facts were unstable and the official channels assumed good documentation from people whose lives produced bad documentation: informal workers, migrants, disabled people, carers, teenagers, the elderly, people with chaotic housing, people whose phones had been stolen, people who trusted the wrong agent.

She was respected locally. This gave her a kind of status she never expected: not fame, not wealth, but being asked into rooms when something had gone wrong. She was also tired in a way rest did not fix.

The main trouble arrived when Yusuf appeared after months of avoiding the family.

He was twenty-four, thinner than she liked, wearing a jacket too expensive for him and carrying a device wrapped in a tea towel. He said he needed her to witness something. Leena said absolutely not before she knew what it was.

Yusuf had been working with an open-agent collective called Lantern. Officially, Lantern helped people audit exploitative systems: housing allocations, wage theft, care downgrades, false fraud flags. Unofficially, it broke into vendor environments, released model behaviour logs, cloned restricted tools and ran counter-agents against corporate enforcement systems. Depending on who was speaking, Lantern was mutual aid, cybercrime, resistance, terrorism, or a brand.

Yusuf insisted they had found proof that a regional care contractor had been using synthetic compliance records to downgrade expensive patients, including their father.

Leena wanted the proof. Leena did not want Yusuf to have obtained it.

This was the adult version of the Tilly problem. Truth existed somewhere inside compromised evidence. The moral direction was clear enough to feel simple and legally tangled enough to ruin people.

Leena met Yusuf in an old shopping centre converted into mixed clinics, repair stalls, cheap work pods and verification kiosks. The place contained a man arguing with a pension agent, a teenager renting compute minutes for a music model, a mother scanning medicine packaging against counterfeit warnings, a robotics repair stall with three idle humanoid torsos used mostly for warehouse parts, and a prayer room with a sign asking people not to bring active recording devices inside.

Yusuf’s evidence was ugly, partial and probably real. There were logs, generated reports, internal messages, model outputs, care-score changes and suppressed human objections. There was also illegal access, missing context and a chain of custody that would make an official reviewer smile politely while discarding it.

Leena said, “You’ve made it easier for them to ignore.”

Yusuf snapped back, “No, you make it easy. You and your forms and your proper channels.”

The argument was not ideological to them. It was sibling-shaped. He thought she had become obedient. She thought he had become reckless. Both were partly right. Their father’s care sat between them, not as a symbol but as the immediate fact of a man who needed help standing up.

Outside, the newsfeeds mentioned orbital compute trials by a private consortium promising clean solar-powered inference at massive scale, but access was pre-sold to defence, finance, pharma and sovereign clients. People joked that the sky was thinking for billionaires. Nobody in Leena’s block expected it to lower their care bills.

There were rumours of self-improving research systems, but ordinary people encountered the rumour as pricing, policy and fear: sudden new capabilities in fraud, automated law, drug discovery, surveillance, defence systems, personalised persuasion. Nobody knew what was true. The uncertainty itself became part of the weather.

Leena’s personal life was not suspended for the plot. She was seeing Anika again, or almost seeing her, after years apart. Anika now coordinated human teams in a semi-automated food resilience network. She was warmer than Leena, less suspicious, better at joy. She told Leena that if trust required perfect proof, nobody got loved.

Leena said that was exactly the kind of thing people said before being conned.

Anika said, “And you wonder why I left.”

The emotional hinge of the day was Leena deciding to act as witness for evidence she could not fully purify. Not to declare it unquestionably true, not to launder it into certainty, but to assemble a public chain strong enough that ignoring it became an act people could see.

She brought in the verification circle. Mr Pavel, older now and still impossible to schedule, remembered a similar downgrade. A former care worker identified generated phrasing in reports she had been pressured to approve. A local journalist with a verified human-source channel agreed to review the material. Anika helped route food deliveries when families attended a public hearing. Samira, who had spent her life navigating systems, recorded a statement so controlled it frightened Leena.

The hearing was not triumphant. The contractor denied wrongdoing. The council claimed procurement limits. The vendor blamed legacy integration. The care model could not be examined fully because of proprietary safety protections. Yusuf’s methods became the story for a while, as Leena knew they would.

But the story did not vanish.

Other families came forward. The contractor paused automated downgrades. A parliamentary committee requested documents. Yusuf went underground for a few weeks and sent Leena one message: sorry but also not sorry. Their father’s care was restored temporarily, which was both everything and not enough.

The final day was quiet.

Leena sat with her father while a human care worker helped him to the chair. The worker was late, underpaid, irritable, gentle. A machine could probably do parts of the lift in a better-funded home. Here, the worker used her hip, her hand, her judgement, and a joke about men who pretended not to need help.

Afterward, Leena walked home past a group of schoolchildren in uniform. One girl was crying because someone had made a fake of her singing. Another was laughing too loudly. Their teacher was trying to get everyone onto the bus.

Leena could have told them things. She could have told them to keep records, to trust slowly, to learn which systems count and which people matter, to never assume the truth wins by being true.

She told them nothing. They would not listen. She would not have listened.

At home she opened one of her tiny room models, the kind she had made since she was fourteen. This one was a kitchen no bigger than a shoebox. There was a chipped mug on the table, a paper calendar, a window painted with rain. Nothing in it verified anything. It was useless except that she made it, and when she looked at it she knew exactly where every piece came from.

Anika arrived later with food.

Leena did not ask where the receipt was.

That was not society repaired, not trust restored, not technology defeated. Leena had learned that proof was necessary and insufficient. Some things had to be checked. Some things had to be witnessed. Some things had to be risked.

## Coda — What the Three Lives Hold

Maya, Noor and Leena never meet.

They live in neighbouring possibilities. In each, school remains recognisable long after the world begins to change around it. The timetable survives. Homework survives. Grades survive. Parents still ask what their children want to be. Teachers still try to see effort through the work in front of them. Young people still care more about friends, embarrassment, desire and belonging than about the historical importance of the systems entering their lives.

The difference is what the world asks of them later.

Maya grows into a world where AI becomes infrastructure by accumulation. Nothing breaks cleanly. Nothing is solved cleanly. The burden settles into the amber cases, the exceptions, the human residue of automated systems. She learns that society can depend on judgement while treating judgement as an inefficiency.

Noor grows into a world where acceleration is partly absorbed. The systems are better, the public tools stronger, the disasters more survivable, the help more real. But help does not remove choice. It multiplies the number of things that can be done and therefore the number of things someone must decide not to do first.

Leena grows into a world where acceleration outruns trust. The machines can answer, generate, detect, route, simulate, optimise and argue. The scarce thing is not output. It is being believed. It is having a person, institution or community able to say: this happened, this matters, this is enough to act.

Across all three lives, the same small human facts persist. People want to be seen. They want to matter. They want enough control over their own days that help does not become capture. They want work that does not quietly humiliate them. They want proof when proof is needed, and mercy when proof is impossible. They want tools that open doors without owning the hallway.

The future does not ask whether humans will be replaced.

It asks what kinds of humans the world will make easier to become.
