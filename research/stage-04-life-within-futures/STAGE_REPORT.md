## 2. Executive summary

- Stage 4 combines the authorised Stage 1R human baseline, Stage 2 flourishing model and Stage 3 AI technical condition bands to model lived human pressures under plausible AI-shaped conditions.
- The central finding is not that “AI changes everything.” It is that AI changes life where technical capability becomes verified, affordable, institutionally permitted and socially trusted action.
- Agency may expand for people who can direct, verify and refuse AI-mediated systems; it may contract for people subject to opaque defaults, surveillance, automated judgement or non-credible exit.
- Status does not disappear when tools become more capable. It is likely to migrate toward access, authenticity, judgement, reputation, institutional permission and control over verification.
- Competence becomes harder to read from visible output when AI assistance is normal, which matters for learning, work, trust and identity.
- Belonging and recognition remain scarce human goods even if interaction, companionship and generated communication become abundant.
- Work and contribution pressures may shift from “can a person produce?” toward “does a person matter, decide, care, repair, judge or take responsibility?”
- Infrastructure, access, language, institutional adoption, family support and governance can produce very different AI futures inside the same society.
- Adolescents are not a separate species in these futures, but Stage 4 identifies an adolescent modifier: developing agency, peer status, identity formation and institutional dependency make AI-mediated life unusually consequential for KS3–KS4 learners.
- Stage 4 does not establish invariants, derive capabilities or design curriculum. It passes only draft candidates for Stage 5 to test after user review and final audit.

### 2.1 One-paragraph summary

Stage 4 models human life under plausible AI-shaped conditions as a problem of mediated agency. Humans remain embodied, socially embedded, status-sensitive, meaning-seeking and variable; flourishing remains tied to security, agency, competence, recognition, meaning, contribution and future possibility; AI futures vary not by one intelligence curve but by feedback, verification, embodiment, infrastructure, governance and trust. The resulting lived world is not simply more automated. It is more mediated: more action travels through systems that must be trusted, afforded, interpreted, refused and held accountable. [Sources: S1R-PF-01–S1R-PF-10; S2-PF-01–S2-PF-07; S3-F-001–S3-F-010; S4-CLM-001]

## 6. Central synthesis

### 6.1 The thesis this stage can defend

AI futures become human futures only when capability enters the channels of ordinary life: tools, institutions, families, workplaces, peer groups, public information systems, markets, devices, regulations and habits. The central pressure is therefore not “more intelligence” in the abstract. It is the reorganisation of human agency through systems that can act, recommend, filter, generate, judge, simulate, impersonate, automate or verify.

That makes the future less like a single rising line and more like a set of uneven negotiations. A person may gain power in one domain and lose it in another. A teenager may be able to create astonishing work and still be more dependent on opaque platforms. A worker may produce more with less effort and find that visible output says less about their competence or contribution. A family may gain practical support while losing privacy or bargaining power. A community may gain translation, coordination and access while facing new questions about authenticity, trust and responsibility.

Claim status: cross-source synthesis, moderate confidence. [Sources: S1R-PF-03, S1R-PF-05, S2-PF-02, S2-NRM-003, S3-F-001, S3-F-002, S3-F-008, S4-CLM-001]

### 6.2 What this changes in the reader’s model of the future

The common mistake is to imagine AI futures as if intelligence were a substance poured evenly into society. Stage 3 already rejected that: AI futures depend on systems, scaffolds, verification, infrastructure, governance and deployment environments. Stage 4 adds the human layer: those systems meet creatures who are embodied, finite, social, status-aware, meaning-seeking and unevenly situated.

So the question becomes: under which conditions can people still direct attention, form intentions, test claims, sustain relationships, be recognised without domination, contribute without coerced sacrifice and keep open a future they have reason to value?

This is not yet a statement about education’s purpose. It is a map of the pressures education will later have to respect.

## 7. Life Within Futures Models

These are analytic condition models, not predictions. They combine Stage 3 technical bands with Stage 1R and Stage 2 human constraints.

### 7.1 Model A — Feedback-rich software acceleration

In this condition, AI is most powerful where work is digital, testable, reversible and cheaply evaluated. Software, documents, administrative processes, design drafts, analysis pipelines and many knowledge workflows become faster. The lived pressure is not simply unemployment or productivity. It is the collapse of some old signals.

When output is easier to produce, output alone says less. A polished essay, prototype, plan, image, spreadsheet or codebase no longer carries the same evidence about the person behind it. This matters for work, trust, identity and learning. Stage 1R already warned that a single performance is incomplete evidence of ability, motive, maturity or character. Stage 4 extends the caution: AI assistance may make that incompleteness visible everywhere. [Sources: S1R-PF-10, S2-PF-04, S3-F-002, S3-F-003, S4-CLM-005]

Opportunity: more people may be able to attempt complex work, explore ideas and recover from errors.

Pressure: institutions may demand new forms of verification, process evidence, trust and responsibility.

What must not be overclaimed: this does not mean AI replaces software engineers, writers, analysts or teachers. It means visible outputs become less reliable as standalone evidence.

### 7.2 Model B — Agentic tool-use expansion

Here AI systems do more than answer. They schedule, purchase, search, draft, message, operate interfaces, call tools, execute workflows and coordinate across systems. This can expand agency for people who have clear goals, adequate support, reliable oversight and the power to refuse.

But agency is not the same as having more buttons. Stage 1R distinguishes control beliefs, available means, opportunity, habits, planning and motivational qualities. Stage 2 treats credible agency as more than formal choice. Stage 3 warns that tool-using AI raises verification, permissions, liability and error-recovery problems. Combined, these imply a central Stage 4 pressure: people may increasingly act through systems they do not fully understand, cannot easily inspect and may not be able to refuse. [Sources: S1R-PF-03, S2-PF-02, S2-NRM-003, S3-F-005, S4-CLM-002]

Opportunity: delegated action can reduce friction, expand access and make complex systems navigable.

Pressure: the person may become responsible for outputs produced through systems whose defaults, incentives and failure modes are opaque.

What must not be overclaimed: more automation is not automatically more autonomy.

### 7.3 Model C — Generative interface transition

In this condition, software becomes less like a set of fixed screens and more like an adaptive conversation between intention and generated interface. People ask systems to assemble, operate or modify workflows. The boundary between using software and having software made around one’s intent becomes blurrier.

The lived effect is double-edged. For some people, especially those blocked by interface complexity, this could be liberating. For others, the interface becomes less stable and less inspectable. If the system changes the tool on the fly, the user must understand not only the task but the mediation.

This pressure matters for young people because learning often depends on making process visible. If a system hides too much of the route from intention to result, competence may grow in some places and weaken in others. That is not a curriculum claim. It is a caution for later stages: ease can expand access while also hiding structure. [Sources: S1R-PF-02, S2-PF-04, S3-F-005, S4-CLM-005]

Opportunity: complex tasks can become accessible to non-specialists.

Pressure: understanding, verification and responsibility may become harder to locate.

What must not be overclaimed: instant interface generation does not eliminate software complexity.

### 7.4 Model D — Embodied automation under physical constraints

Robotics and embodied AI alter life differently from software. Bodies are expensive. Environments vary. Safety, liability, maintenance, dexterity, uptime and trust matter. Stage 3 therefore separates embodied automation from software acceleration.

In lived terms, this produces patchy futures. A warehouse, hospital corridor, farm, kitchen, home, school and street are not the same automation problem. Physical automation may arrive strongly in structured environments while remaining fragile or costly in ordinary domestic and community spaces.

The human pressure is practical and symbolic. If physical labour changes unevenly, status and contribution may shift unevenly too. Some work may become safer or less exhausting. Other work may become monitored, deskilled or squeezed. Care work is especially important: the fact that a task can be assisted does not mean recognition, responsibility or relational care has been solved. [Sources: S1R-PF-05, S2-PF-03, S2-PF-06, S3-F-006, S4-CLM-007]

Opportunity: reduction of dangerous, repetitive or inaccessible physical tasks.

Pressure: new hierarchies around who is assisted, replaced, supervised or made responsible for machine failure.

What must not be overclaimed: robotics should not be placed on software timelines.

### 7.5 Model E — Discovery acceleration with validation bottlenecks

AI may accelerate candidate generation in science, medicine and engineering. But Stage 3 is explicit: candidates are not validated outcomes. Experiments, trials, replication, manufacturing, regulation and deployment remain expensive.

For ordinary life, this matters because hope and proof may separate. People may live amid more announcements, more possibilities, more experimental treatments, more designed materials and more claims of imminent breakthrough. Some will benefit. Others may face hype, unequal access, medical uncertainty or new forms of institutional gatekeeping.

The flourishing pressure is meaningfully different from software acceleration. It concerns trust in expertise, patience under uncertainty, access to validated benefit and protection from premature claims. [Sources: S2-PF-01, S2-NRM-001, S3-F-007, S3-U-004]

Opportunity: faster discovery pipelines in selected domains.

Pressure: more candidate possibilities can increase the burden of validation, access and public trust.

What must not be overclaimed: AI-for-science does not guarantee cures, validated discoveries or social benefit.

### 7.6 Model F — Infrastructure-constrained and access-divergent AI

AI futures are built from chips, compute, data centres, energy, water, devices, networks, capital, language coverage, procurement and governance. Stage 3 treats these as first-order conditions. Stage 4 adds that people do not experience “AI” equally; they experience the version available to their household, school, workplace, institution, language, jurisdiction and budget.

This is one of the most important constraints on easy futurism. Two young people in the same city may inhabit different AI futures: one surrounded by reliable tools, adult support and institutional permission; another by low-quality systems, surveillance, restricted access or none at all. The same technical era can produce abundance, dependence and exclusion simultaneously. [Sources: S1R-PF-08, S2-PF-01, S3-F-008, S3-F-009, S3-U-008, S4-CLM-008]

Opportunity: open, edge and local AI may broaden access and local control.

Pressure: access divergence may deepen inequality while distributing risk and accountability.

What must not be overclaimed: open or edge AI is not simply democratic, safe, dangerous or uncontrollable.

### 7.7 Model G — Ambient trust-pressure environment

In this condition, AI becomes part of the atmosphere: companions, generated media, automated moderation, surveillance, impersonation, recommendation, judgement, provenance systems and invisible filtering. The human problem is not only misinformation. It is the cost of ordinary trust.

Humans rely on social signals, categories, reputation, belonging and institutional cues. If those signals become easier to simulate or automate, people need new ways to decide what and whom to trust. Yet trust cannot become total suspicion without damaging social life. This is a delicate pressure: too much trust invites manipulation; too little trust corrodes recognition, belonging and public coordination. [Sources: S1R-PF-04, S1R-PF-06, S2-PF-03, S3-F-010, S4-CLM-004, S4-CLM-006]

Opportunity: support, companionship, translation, access and coordination may become more available.

Pressure: authenticity, accountability and credible care become harder to verify.

What must not be overclaimed: synthetic media and ambient AI do not automatically collapse truth or democracy.

## 10. Access and Inequality Analysis

Stage 4 should not imagine one universal AI future. Access is not only whether a person has a chatbot. It includes quality, language, reliability, cost, institutional permission, adult support, privacy, legal rights, cultural fit and ability to contest decisions.

| Access layer | Lived effect if strong | Lived effect if weak | Stage 4 caution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Device and connectivity | AI tools become routine supports. | AI remains intermittent, low-quality or absent. | Do not universalise high-resource users. |
| Language and cultural fit | Systems understand local meanings and communication. | Systems misread, exclude or flatten context. | Preserve construct-equivalence cautions. |
| Institutional permission | People can use tools openly and safely. | People use tools secretly or are blocked by blanket rules. | Adoption is social, not just technical. |
| Verification support | Outputs can be checked and trusted. | Users carry risk they cannot evaluate. | Verification burden is unequal. |
| Family and adult support | Young people can interpret tools with guidance. | Young people face powerful systems alone. | Do not assume expert adults are present. |
| Privacy and rights | People can refuse, contest or limit data use. | Participation becomes coercive or surveilled. | Agency requires credible exit. |
| Disability and support needs | AI may extend access and distributed capability. | Poor design may intensify exclusion or misclassification. | Do not reduce disability to difference or deficit alone. |
| Institutional accountability | Errors can be appealed and repaired. | Automated decisions become sticky. | Governance presence is not safety proof. |

## 11. Adolescent and adult modifiers

### 11.1 Adolescents

Stage 4 should treat adolescents neither as helpless nor as miniature adults. Stage 1R authorises a careful modifier: adolescents possess meaningful control, but deployment and goal priorities may vary with development, task, emotion and social context. In AI-rich futures, this modifier matters because identity, peer status, experimentation and institutional dependency interact.

Likely adolescent-specific pressures:

- output visibility and peer comparison may intensify status concerns;
- AI assistance may make performance harder to interpret;
- identity exploration may become easier but more surveilled or persistent;
- persuasive or companion systems may meet real social needs without guaranteeing care;
- credible agency depends heavily on adults, institutions and platform rules;
- disconnected or absent learners may be least visible in the evidence base.

Claim status: adolescent modifier, moderate confidence with strong caution. [Sources: S1R-PF-09, S1R-PF-10, S2-NRM-005, S3-F-010, S4-CLM-009]

### 11.2 Adults

Adults encounter AI conditions through work, family responsibility, civic life, care, money, health, status and institutional obligations. The adult modifier is less about development and more about responsibility under uncertainty.

Likely adult-specific pressures:

- being accountable for AI-assisted decisions without full transparency;
- needing to help children navigate systems adults themselves do not fully understand;
- work contribution and status being recomposed unevenly;
- care burdens changing without necessarily being recognised;
- civic trust and information verification becoming more expensive;
- material access shaping who benefits from automation.

Claim status: synthesis, moderate confidence where grounded in Stage 2 and Stage 3; lower confidence for specific institutional outcomes.

## 12. Cross-domain synthesis

### 12.1 The recurring mechanism: mediated agency

The strongest cross-domain mechanism is mediated agency. AI systems may sit between intention and action, person and institution, learner and evidence, worker and output, citizen and public information, friend and communication, patient and medical knowledge.

Mediation is not inherently good or bad. It can expand reach, reduce friction and open possibilities. It can also obscure responsibility, concentrate power and make refusal less credible.

This mechanism connects multiple prior-stage findings:

- Stage 1R: agency is not a single trait; compliance does not disclose endorsement.
- Stage 2: credible agency and recognition without domination are core commitments.
- Stage 3: agentic systems, generative interfaces and governance conditions make verification and permission central.

Therefore Stage 4’s most important warning is: do not confuse AI-enabled action with human agency unless the person can understand enough, refuse enough, contest enough and be supported enough to make the action meaningfully theirs. [Sources: S1R-PF-03, S2-PF-02, S2-NRM-003, S3-F-005, S4-CLM-002]

### 12.2 The strongest tension: ease versus formation

AI may make many actions easier. But human development often depends on forming attention, judgement, competence, responsibility, relationship and meaning over time. Ease can support formation when it removes pointless friction. It can undermine formation when it hides structure, bypasses practice, simulates recognition or makes outcomes look detached from process.

This is not a curriculum claim. It is a Stage 4 human-life tension that later stages must handle carefully.

Sources: S1R-PF-02, S1R-PF-10, S2-PF-04, S3-F-002, S3-F-005.

### 12.3 The deepest inequality: not access to AI, but access to trustworthy action

The common access question is “who has AI?” Stage 4 suggests a better question: who has access to AI-mediated action that is reliable, explainable, contestable, affordable, culturally legible and institutionally accepted?

This reframing matters because low-quality access can be a burden. A person may have a tool but not the power to verify it, appeal its effects or prevent its misuse.

Sources: S1R-PF-08, S2-NRM-001, S2-NRM-003, S3-F-008, S3-F-009, S4-CLM-008.

## 14. What this stage does not establish

- which AI future will happen;

- that technical capability becomes social adoption;
- that recurrent pressures are invariants;
- that education’s purpose follows directly from future conditions;
- that curriculum should teach particular capabilities;
- that AI should or should not be used in schools;

- that adolescents are uniformly vulnerable or uniformly empowered;
- that work will disappear or become meaningless;
- that synthetic media collapses truth;

## 17. Conclusion

Stage 4’s report-phase synthesis suggests that AI-shaped futures should be understood as changes in the conditions of human agency, trust, status, competence, recognition, contribution and access. The recurring question is not whether humans will live with more intelligence around them. It is whether human beings can still act, understand, refuse, belong, contribute and become capable inside systems that increasingly mediate action.

That is the strongest bridge this stage can currently offer to Stage 5. It is also exactly where the work must pause before becoming either a pass-forward or a book.
