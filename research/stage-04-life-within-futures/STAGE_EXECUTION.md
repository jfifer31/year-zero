## 2. Stage objective

Stage 4 models what lived human life may feel and function like under plausible AI-shaped future conditions by combining the authorised Stage 1R human baseline, Stage 2 flourishing model and Stage 3 technical condition bands. It identifies recurring pressures, opportunities, risks, inequalities and human needs that Stage 5 may later inspect for invariance.

This stage does **not** design curriculum, derive capabilities, choose a preferred future, predict a single scenario, prescribe policy or produce fictional world-building as authority.

## 5. Research question tree

### 5.1 Primary question

> How might human life be experienced and organised inside plausible AI-shaped future conditions, and which recurring pressures, opportunities and needs should Stage 5 examine as possible human invariants?

### 5.2 Secondary questions

1. How do Stage 3 technical condition bands alter the practical conditions for Stage 1R humans?
2. How do those altered conditions support, strain or complicate Stage 2 flourishing dimensions?
3. Which lived pressures recur across multiple AI futures rather than depending on one scenario?
4. Which pressures are adolescent- or education-context modifiers rather than general human conditions?
5. Which access, infrastructure, institutional and cultural differences prevent universalising the models?
6. Which claims are too speculative to pass forward?

### 5.3 Tertiary questions

| Secondary question | Sub-questions | Expected evidence or reasoning needed |
|---|---|---|
| Technical conditions | Which Stage 3 bands most affect agency, trust, work, learning and social life? | Stage 3 condition bands plus systems mapping. |
| Human baseline | Which human mechanisms are likely to be activated or strained? | Stage 1R authorised findings. |
| Flourishing | Which dimensions become easier, harder or more contested? | Stage 2 authorised flourishing model. |
| Variation | Who is helped, exposed, excluded or misread? | Stage 1R variation cautions plus Stage 3 access/infrastructure cautions. |
| Pass-forward | What should Stage 5 inspect for invariance? | Claims surviving synthesis and preliminary review. |

### 5.4 Questions deliberately excluded

| Excluded question | Reason for exclusion | Where it should go instead |
|---|---|---|
| What should the curriculum teach? | Premature curriculum design. | Stage 10. |
| What capabilities matter most? | Requires Stage 5 and Stage 6 first. | Stage 7. |
| Which AI future will happen? | Stage 4 models conditions, not predictions. | Not a project objective. |
| What should schools do with AI tools? | Pedagogy and implementation are out of scope. | Later curriculum and implementation stages. |
| Is flourishing measurable as a score? | Stage 2 explicitly prohibits this. | Only if a later stage authorises a separate measurement review. |

## 6. Method selection

| Method | Why this method fits | What it can answer | What it cannot answer | Output produced |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Authorised-input synthesis | Stage 4 depends on Stage 1R, Stage 2 and Stage 3 pass-forwards. | How authorised findings interact. | New empirical truth outside the authorised record. | Core synthesis claims. |
| Technical-band lived modelling | Stage 3 passes condition bands, not stories. | How different technical conditions affect lived contexts. | Which future will occur. | Life Within Futures Models. |
| Systems mapping | Stage 4 needs feedback loops between humans, institutions and technology. | Dependencies, mediators and second-order pressures. | Precise causal estimates. | Pressure and Opportunity Map. |
| Flourishing stress test | Stage 2 defines dimensions and trade-off rules. | Which conditions support or strain flourishing. | Individual diagnosis or scoring. | Recurring Human Need Map. |
| Access and inequality audit | Stage 1R and Stage 3 warn against universalising high-resource contexts. | Who may benefit or be exposed differently. | Full global distribution forecast. | Access and Inequality Analysis. |
| Adolescent modifier check | Stage 1R warns against age determinism but identifies modifiers. | How youth life differs from adult life. | A universal maturity timetable. | KS3–KS4 developmental cautions for later stages. |

### 6.1 Method risk

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scenario seduction | High | The report may read as prediction or fiction. | Keep models analytic and condition-based; label projections. |
| Curriculum drift | High | Stage 4 could smuggle in capability or curriculum conclusions. | Explicit exclusions; Claim Steward review. |
| Elite technology saturation | High | Models may overrepresent high-resource AI access. | Access and inequality analysis; Bias Auditor review. |
| Excessive abstraction | Moderate | Lived life may remain too conceptual. | Day-in-the-life stress testing as analytic method. |
| False convergence | Moderate | Recurring pressures may be overstated as invariants. | Pass forward candidates only; Stage 5 must test. |
| Under-citing synthesis | Moderate | Major claims may appear unsupported. | Ledger links and source-ID bundles from pass-forwards. |

### 6.2 Rejected methods

| Method rejected | Reason rejected | Risk if this rejection is wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Full new literature review | Stage 4’s main job is synthesis of authorised stages, not reopening foundations. | Some lived-experience evidence may be underdeveloped. |
| Fictional future-world writing as authority | Stage 4 must not produce predictive sci-fi as its controlled output. | The report may be less vivid than a narrative manuscript. |
| Quantitative forecasting | The pass-forwards do not support precise probabilities. | Some trend magnitudes may remain vague. |
| Curriculum implication mapping | Explicitly out of scope before later stages. | Readers may want more practical guidance now. |
