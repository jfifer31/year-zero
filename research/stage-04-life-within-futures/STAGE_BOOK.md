# Three Lives After the Curve

## Part I — The Patchwork Work World

### 2029

Owen Price first noticed the new homework rule because Mr Hargreaves had stopped looking angry and started looking tired.

That was worse. Angry teachers could be funny, if you were fourteen and sitting three rows back with Jamal kicking your chair. Tired teachers made the room feel smaller. They made you feel, in a way you did not want to feel at school, that adults were not pretending as well as they used to.

The rule was printed on yellow paper and taped beside the whiteboard, where the corners curled from the radiator.

All submitted written work must include a process note.

Mr Hargreaves read it aloud as if somebody else had written it, which Owen assumed they had.

“A process note,” he said, “means two or three sentences explaining how you produced the work. What you planned. What you changed. Which tools you used. If you used any.”

He paused there. Everyone knew what “tools” meant. Nobody looked at anyone else.

Jamal leaned back and whispered, “I used my hands, sir.”

Owen bit the inside of his cheek because laughing would get him moved, and if he got moved he would end up near Kieran Bell, who always hummed under his breath and smelled of tropical deodorant. Mr Hargreaves heard anyway. He gave Jamal the look teachers gave when they wanted to laugh but had chosen disappointment as a career.

“You’ll need to do better than that.”

“I used my brain as well.”

“Miracles continue.”

That got a laugh. Even Mr Hargreaves smiled, but only for a second. Then he rubbed his thumb across the corner of the yellow paper.

“I know some of you think this is stupid,” he said.

Owen did not think it was stupid exactly. He thought it was one more thing adults had invented because they could not admit they had lost track. His mum had the same face when the dental booking system sent three people to the same chair and then told her, very politely, that the schedule had been optimised.

He opened his exercise book. The page had a grey smear from his hand where he had tried to rub out a sentence about Macbeth and ambition. The essay was due next period. He had written most of it himself, if you counted asking the school assistant to “make this sound less rubbish” three times as writing it himself. The assistant had produced sentences that sounded like they wore shoes indoors. Owen had changed them back until they sounded more like him, which meant worse but safer.

He wrote his process note.

I planned it by looking at the quote. I changed some bits. I used the school assistant but not for ideas.

That last part was a lie in the way most school lies were lies: not completely false, not completely useful.

Across the room Liv Bennett was writing quickly, her head bent so the blue-black ends of her hair fell against her cheek. She did not need the assistant. Or if she used it, she used it in some clever way that made the work look more like her, not less. Liv played drums with a seriousness that made Owen embarrassed by how much he wanted to play guitar near her. She wore old band shirts for bands Owen suspected she had actually listened to, unlike Jamal, who wore a Nirvana shirt because an image model had told him it made his face look “less boyish”.

Jamal was sketching a cartoon of Mr Hargreaves being arrested by a robot for excessive sarcasm.

“You coming later?” he muttered.

“Where?”

“Centre.”

“Can’t. Dad needs help.”

Jamal drew prison bars around the robot. “With what?”

“Heat pump on Bracken Road.”

“Tell him to ask the app.”

Owen did not answer. Jamal had never seen the app when it was wrong. He had never stood with Mark Price in somebody’s cold kitchen while a customer watched the little spinning diagnosis wheel on their wall screen and said, “It says the compressor is fine,” as if the app were a second tradesman and Owen’s dad were a man with dirty hands trying to cheat them.

The bell went. Chairs screamed back. Mr Hargreaves called for essays. Half the class joined the upload queue by the door, where the school tablets sat in a charging rack like thin black plates.

Owen waited because the queue was a place where people watched your screen.

Liv passed him. “You playing tonight?”

It took him half a second to understand she meant the rehearsal room.

“Maybe.”

“That means no.”

“It means maybe.”

She smiled like she knew better, which she usually did. “We need someone who can do the bit before the chorus.”

Owen tried to shrug as if this information had not gone through his chest like a match. “Jamal can do it.”

“Jamal can make a video of himself doing it.”

This was true. Jamal could make anything look like it had happened. Last week he had posted a clip of the three of them playing in the community centre, except the lights were better, the crowd was bigger, and Owen’s guitar solo had become something his fingers would never agree to in real life. It got eight thousand views before Liv made him take it down.

Jamal said she was allergic to success.

Liv said he was allergic to shame.

Owen thought they were both right.

He handed in his essay and kept the process note face down.

After school he took the bus with his guitar case between his knees and his dad’s tool bag on the seat beside him. The bus was one of the new council ones with a screen above the driver that translated announcements into six languages and occasionally, for reasons nobody understood, Welsh. A baby cried two seats back. A man in a hi-vis jacket watched a tutorial on fitting kitchen sensors. Two girls from Year 10 were laughing at a filter that put teacher faces onto dance videos.

Owen checked his messages.

Jamal had sent a clip from the rehearsal room. He was sitting at the drums, not playing them, while a generated version of Liv sang a song she had never agreed to sing.

Under it he had written: this version less scary.

Owen smiled despite himself, then looked around in case anyone saw.

His dad was already outside the Bracken Road house when Owen arrived, kneeling beside a heat-pump unit with the cover off. Mark Price had the permanently offended posture of a man listening to a machine lie.

“Hold this,” he said, handing Owen a torch.

No hello. No how was school. His dad did not do warm-up sentences when something was broken.

The customer stood at the back door in slippers, arms folded.

“The diagnostic says it’s not the compressor,” she said.

Mark did not look up. “It would say that.”

“It says the pressure pattern is consistent.”

“It would say that as well.”

Owen angled the torch. The inside of the unit was damp and smelled of metal and old leaves. His dad reached into a place Owen would not have put his hand and wiggled a cable.

“Hear that?”

Owen listened. The unit clicked, hummed, then made a soft, uneven rattle, like a coin in a washing machine.

“Yeah.”

“That’s not pressure.”

The customer looked unconvinced. She held her phone up and took a picture, maybe for the landlord, maybe to ask another system whether the man in her garden knew his job.

Mark said, quieter, “The app doesn’t hear embarrassment.”

Owen laughed. His dad did not.

They worked until the sky went purple behind the neighbour’s extension. Owen’s fingers got cold. His phone buzzed four times from the group chat. He did not check it because his dad hated phones during work unless they were showing wiring diagrams or invoices.

At half five, the unit came alive with a smoother sound. The kitchen window glowed. The customer’s shoulders dropped before her face changed.

“So it was the compressor?”

“Loose contactor,” Mark said.

“But the diagnostic—”

“Didn’t fancy crawling about in the wet.”

She laughed then, because the house was warming and people became easier when the thing they wanted had happened.

On the bus home, Mark transferred the invoice through his trade app. It suggested a discount because his completion time had exceeded the estimated repair window.

“Estimated by who?” he muttered.

Owen watched the amount drop by fourteen pounds.

“Can’t you appeal it?”

“I can. Then it’ll waste twenty minutes and tell me I’m valued.”

He accepted the lower payment.

That night, after tea, Owen plugged his guitar into the small amp in his room and tried the bit before the chorus. The first attempt sounded like a doorbell being electrocuted. The second was worse. He asked the music assistant to isolate the guitar line from Liv’s demo. It did it perfectly. Too perfectly. Every note clear, every string bright, the rhythm cleaned of all the tiny hesitations that made it human enough to copy.

Owen played along until his fingers hurt.

At 10:12, Jamal sent another clip. This one showed the community centre stage, except the ceiling tiles were gone, the walls were black, and a crowd held up phones while a version of Owen — taller, better hair, better shoulders — played the chorus under blue light.

Jamal wrote: future you.

Owen stared at it. His stomach did a stupid, pleased little flip before embarrassment stamped it flat. The fake Owen looked like someone who knew exactly where to put his feet.

He typed: delete that.

Jamal replied: not posted.

Then: yet.

Owen put the phone face down and played the riff again. This time he got halfway before messing it up.

Downstairs, his mum was arguing with the dental scheduler.

“No, I can’t put Mrs Cole at 9:20 because Mrs Cole needs wheelchair access and you’ve put her with Ajay upstairs again. No, don’t apologise to me. Move her.”

A polite synthetic voice murmured through the kitchen speaker. Owen could not make out the words, only the tone: calm, reasonable, useless.

He played louder.

Two weeks later, at the school showcase, everything went wrong in a way that made sense only afterwards.

The assembly hall had been dressed up with rented light strips and a sound desk controlled by an app the deputy head said would “take the pressure off students.” Jamal loved it immediately. Liv did not.

“It’s going to quantise everything,” she said.

Owen pretended to know what quantise meant. “Good?”

“Bad.”

Jamal was filming himself in the reflection of the trophy cabinet. “Everything is bad to drummers. You hit things for emotional release.”

“You fake things for attention.”

“And yet the camera loves me.”

Owen tuned his guitar and tried not to look at the rows of plastic chairs filling with parents. His mum had come straight from work, still wearing her dental badge. His dad was there too, which surprised him. Mark stood at the back with his arms folded, not because he was annoyed but because he did not know what to do with his hands indoors.

The first three acts went brilliantly and nobody trusted them. A Year 9 girl sang with a backing track that lifted her voice until every wobble became style. Two boys did a comedy sketch with timing too clean to be theirs. A dance group performed under lights that corrected their spacing in real time, little floor glows nudging them into formation.

People clapped, but the clapping had a strange delay. Everyone was trying to decide what they had watched.

Then Owen, Liv and Jamal went on.

Jamal had promised not to use the generated crowd filter on the live feed. He had promised twice, which was usually one promise too many.

Liv counted them in.

For thirty seconds, it worked. Owen hit the riff. Liv came in hard on the drums. Jamal, to his credit, actually played bass instead of performing the idea of bass for his camera. The sound desk cleaned the levels and threw a soft amber wash across the stage.

Then the hall lights flickered.

The sound app froze on a screen that said restoring optimum mix.

The backing click vanished. Jamal looked at Liv. Liv did not look at anyone. She hit the snare harder and kept going.

Owen could not hear himself properly. The guitar monitor spat a burst of feedback that made the front row flinch. He reached for the volume, missed, and knocked his pick onto the floor.

There was a terrible open second in which he saw everything: Kieran Bell laughing, Mr Hargreaves standing up, his mum’s face tight with wanting to help, Liv staring at him as if daring him to disappear.

He played with his thumb.

It sounded rough and too low and nothing like the assistant’s isolated track. It also sounded, unmistakably, like him.

Jamal moved closer so Owen could hear the bass. Liv pulled the tempo back without making it obvious. Someone in the back clapped on the beat, badly. Then more people joined. The hall became messy, and because it was messy, people began to believe it.

When they finished, the clapping was immediate.

Owen looked at the floor, hot-faced, pretending to sort his cable.

His dad found him afterwards by the vending machines.

“Bit loud.”

“Yeah.”

“Good though.”

Owen shrugged.

Mark nodded toward the hall, where the deputy head was apologising to parents for “a temporary service interruption.”

“Thing about systems,” he said. “Sooner or later, someone still has to stand there.”

Owen did not answer because Liv was walking over with two cans of Fanta, and his brain had turned useless.

She handed him one. “You messed up.”

“Cheers.”

“No, I mean you messed up and didn’t stop.”

She said it like that was the better thing.

For the rest of the night Owen carried those words around like a coin in his pocket, touching them when nobody was looking.

### 2037

The booking agent wanted to remove the sound check.

It did not say this rudely. Nothing said anything rudely anymore unless it was a person or a cheap customer-service bot pretending to be one. The agent wrote in pleasant grey text on the venue wall screen:

Recommended optimisation: remove human sound-check labour from package. Predicted audience satisfaction impact: negligible. Estimated saving: £184.

Owen Price stood in the empty room with a mug of tea cooling in his hand and stared at the word negligible.

At twenty-two, he had learned that certain words were dangerous because nobody felt responsible for them. Negligible was one. Streamlined was another. Flexible meant poor. Authentic meant expensive but not for the person being authentic.

The venue was called The Kiln because it had once been part of a ceramics workshop and because every other name suggested by the branding assistant sounded like a cocktail bar in Birmingham. The floor sloped slightly. The toilets needed retiling. In winter, the green room smelled of damp coats. Owen loved it with a loyalty that embarrassed him.

He tapped the wall screen.

Reject recommendation.

The agent asked for a reason.

Because the room feeds back when full.

Please specify acoustic variable.

“People,” Owen said.

The wall did not hear him, or pretended not to.

He typed: bodies alter sound.

Accepted. Manual override logged. This may affect margin score.

“Margin score can kiss my arse,” said Kelly Price from the doorway.

Owen turned. His mum was carrying a crate of glasses against one hip. She had left the dental group two years earlier after they replaced reception with a voice system called Pearl and then rehired her for three days a week as patient confidence support, which meant sitting beside elderly people while Pearl mispronounced their names.

“You working tonight?” Owen asked.

“Depends if you’re paying me.”

“I’m your son.”

“That’s why I asked.”

She set the glasses down and looked around the room. “Your dad says the left speaker’s buzzing.”

“Your dad says every speaker’s buzzing.”

“He’s usually right.”

He was. Mark had become more valuable and more irritated with every passing year. The repair apps diagnosed quicker than he did now, but customers had learned that diagnosis was not repair. Corporate plans sent sealed modules and remote technicians who spoke through appliances in calm voices. Mark turned up in person, cursed softly, fixed the thing that had actually broken, and charged less than he should.

Owen’s work was not so different.

He ran events, repaired equipment, managed agents for older venue owners, hired performers, negotiated with procurement systems, and occasionally played guitar when someone dropped out. He had no job title that made sense to relatives. At weddings he said “events” and watched people decide whether that was failure.

His school had sent him three reunion reminders that month. The platform wrote as if the school itself missed him.

Reconnect with your cohort. Share your pathway. Inspire current pupils.

Owen deleted each message. He had no pathway. He had a van with a cracked reversing camera, two regular clients who paid late, a venue he half-owned through an arrangement no bank would understand, and a reputation that worked only if enough people kept saying his name in the right rooms.

At six, Jamal arrived wearing mirrored glasses though the sun had gone in.

“This place looks smaller in real life,” he said.

“Everything does after you’ve filtered it.”

Jamal grinned and hugged him with one arm. He smelled expensive in a subscription way, like a hotel lobby. His hair was cropped close now, his beard sharper than Owen remembered. Online, Jamal had half a million followers across three accounts and no reliable income. He made reaction edits, local history reconstructions, fake adverts for real businesses, real adverts for fake businesses, and once, accidentally, a political clip that got him interviewed by a programme his nan watched.

“You got my rider?”

“Tap water and humility?”

“Sparkling humility.”

They walked the room. Jamal was filming already, not obviously, just with the little pendant camera at his throat. People did that now. Owen hated it and had one himself.

“Liv coming?” Jamal asked.

Owen pretended to check the lighting rig. “Think so.”

“You think so?”

“She said maybe.”

“That means yes from Liv.”

“Does it?”

“No. But it means she wanted you to think maybe meant yes.”

Owen threw a coil of cable at him. Jamal ducked, laughing.

Liv arrived at seven with a drum case, a child’s backpack and a tiredness around her eyes that made Owen forget the clever thing he had prepared. She was twenty-two and looked exactly like herself, which annoyed him. Some people improved by becoming more artificial. Liv had become more real in a way that made rooms rearrange around her.

“Milo’s with my mum,” she said before Owen asked.

“I wasn’t—”

“You were.”

Jamal hugged her, then held her at arm’s length. “You look aggressively offline.”

“You look like a sponsored headache.”

They smiled at each other with eight years of practice.

The event was a “human night,” though Owen hated the phrase. It sounded like a zoo sign. No generated music, no synthetic vocals, no adaptive crowd overlays, no emotional optimisation lighting. Just people on a stage, which had become enough of a novelty that people paid fifteen pounds for it and then complained the drinks were not subsidised.

The first act was a poet who forgot her lines and got the biggest cheer of the night. The second was a father and daughter playing folk songs with a tuning problem nobody minded. Jamal hosted between acts and made everyone laugh by reading fake sponsorship messages from imaginary AI lifestyle brands.

Owen stayed near the desk, listening for trouble.

At ten, the platform agent messaged again.

Audience sentiment high. Recommend capturing biometric applause pattern for future marketing.

Owen declined.

Potential revenue loss: £312.

He declined harder, jabbing the screen as if it had skin.

Then Liv played.

She did not sing. She had never sung. She played drums with two other women under low white light, no backing track, no correction, no click. The room changed. People stopped recording, not because they were told to but because their hands forgot. Owen stood by the desk and felt the old school hall open inside him: the frozen app, the feedback, Liv’s eyes saying don’t stop.

Afterwards, outside by the bins, she smoked a cigarette she claimed not to smoke anymore.

“You’ve done something good here,” she said.

“Don’t sound so surprised.”

“I’m always surprised when men follow through.”

He laughed. “Fair.”

She looked back at the venue. Through the fire door they could hear Jamal leading the crowd in a chant about the bar prices.

“You going to sell it?” she asked.

Owen tried to look blank. “What?”

“Come on.”

He had not told her about the offer. He had told no one except his mum, who told his dad, who told him not to be daft in a tone that did not reveal which choice was daft.

The offer came from a national experience platform called Hearth. They bought local venues and kept the bits that looked soulful. Their agent had produced a twelve-page plan showing how The Kiln could become a “trust-certified regional authenticity hub.” Owen would stay on as local face and operations lead. The money was good enough to frighten him.

“Maybe,” he said.

Liv smoked and watched him.

“Don’t do that.”

“Do what?”

“Make me say the thing so you can argue with it.”

He leaned against the wall. The bricks were cold through his shirt.

“I’m tired, Liv.”

That was not the whole truth, but it was the truth standing nearest the door.

She nodded. “Yeah.”

“Everything’s a fight. Booking systems. Insurance. Model-use declarations. People wanting human nights but paying machine prices. The council agent thinks our roof repair is low cultural priority because we don’t produce enough daytime footfall. I spend half my life proving the thing that happened happened.”

Liv dropped the cigarette and crushed it. “And if you sell?”

“I get paid.”

“And?”

He pictured the Hearth plan: cleaner toilets, better lights, dynamic pricing, branded local stories on the walls, his face in a launch video saying he believed in community. He pictured not worrying about the rent. He pictured someone else choosing what counted as real.

“And I become décor,” he said.

She smiled a little, not kindly but not unkindly either. “You’d hate being décor.”

Inside, the crowd roared. Jamal had probably done something stupid.

Owen looked at Liv. “What would you do?”

She took too long to answer.

“I left touring because Milo started clapping when adverts came on,” she said.

Owen waited.

“That’s not an answer.”

“It is the only answer I have.”

Later, after everyone left, Owen found his dad on a stepladder adjusting the left speaker.

“Buzzing?” Owen asked.

“Told you.”

Mark climbed down slowly. His knees were bad now, though he refused to give them the satisfaction of mentioning it.

“Good night?”

“Yeah.”

“Till goes alright?”

“Yeah.”

Mark looked at him. “Then don’t sell this week.”

“That your business advice?”

“That’s my life advice. Business advice costs extra.”

Owen laughed, but when he locked the door, he stood for a long time with the key in his hand.

Across the road, an automated delivery rover paused at the kerb, considered the empty street, then turned back the way it had come. Owen watched its little red tail light disappear into the wet dark. Something about it made him feel tender and annoyed, which was how most things made him feel now.

His phone buzzed.

Jamal had posted a clip of the crowd chanting. No filters. No fake lighting. No improved Owen.

Caption: place was alive tonight.

For once, Owen did not ask him to delete it.

### 2045

The morning the neighbourhood agent failed, everyone blamed the council until the council blamed the weather.

The storm had come in hard before dawn, throwing rain against the windows in handfuls and knocking a branch through the sensor mast above the old library. By seven, half of Fenton had lost coordination. Bins sat uncollected because the route system could not confirm access. Two care visits were delayed because the mobility gates would not authenticate. The pharmacy queue spilled onto the pavement. Every shared calendar in the district seemed to have suffered a small stroke.

Owen found out because a woman he barely knew knocked on The Kiln’s side door and asked if he could open the community room.

“Why?”

“Because nobody knows where to go.”

That was how the day began.

At thirty, Owen had grown into his face but not quite into peace. His hair had started thinning at the temples, which Jamal described as “heritage masculinity.” He had a small scar under his chin from dropping a lighting rig in 2041. He owned three decent guitars, one unreliable van, and 31 percent of a cooperative that repaired venues, ran events, installed human-overridable systems and taught teenagers how not to electrocute themselves.

The cooperative had a name, Kilnwork, that Owen still disliked less than all the alternatives. It occupied the venue, two railway arches and a former vape shop. It survived on repairs, events, local contracts and the stubborn fact that things in the physical world kept breaking in ways remote agents could only describe.

By eight, the community room was full.

Mrs Cole from Bracken Road sat near the radiator with a paper list of people who needed checking on. A delivery driver whose name Owen always forgot had brought a crate of oat milk because the corner shop shutters were stuck. Two teenagers were trying to reconnect the public mesh through an old router Owen had not seen since 2038. Mark Price, seventy-one and furious about it, had appeared with a toolbox and immediately told everyone the council mast had been installed by idiots.

“You installed that mast,” Kelly said.

“Under protest.”

Owen made tea.

There were better emergency systems in richer places. Evie lived in one. Her building in Manchester had private coordination, filtered water, backup batteries and an agent that knew every resident’s medical needs without anyone having to shout across a room. She was a compliance architect for a firm that made public systems less embarrassing after they had already embarrassed themselves. She earned more than Owen, slept less and called every Sunday while walking very fast somewhere expensive.

At nine, she appeared on the wall screen in The Kiln, wrapped in a coat, hair wet from rain.

“You’re open?”

“Apparently.”

“Do you need anything?”

Owen looked around. His dad was arguing with a teenager about whether soldering irons had been better before safety locks. His mum was organising prescriptions by memory because the pharmacy feed was down. Jamal, who had turned up claiming he was only there to film, was carrying chairs.

“A normal life?”

Evie smiled. “Can’t source one.”

“Then we’re fine.”

Her face changed in the tiny way that meant she was about to discuss the offer.

“Hearth sent the revised terms.”

“During a local outage?”

“Their timing engine is poor at tact.”

The offer had returned every year in a new shape. Hearth no longer called itself an experience platform. It was a Civic Memory Company now, which meant it bought places people loved and made them legible to investors, tourists and municipal dashboards. It wanted Kilnwork because Kilnwork was trusted in exactly the unscalable way Hearth liked to scale.

“They’ll fund the roof,” Evie said.

Rain hit the patched ceiling above the bar with comic emphasis.

“I know.”

“And the youth workshop.”

“I know.”

“And Dad’s knee replacement privately.”

Owen looked away. That was new.

Evie saw that it had landed and hated herself slightly. He could tell. They had been siblings too long to miss each other’s cheap shots.

“Sorry,” she said.

“No, you’re not.”

“No. But I’m sorry I’m not.”

He laughed despite himself.

The room screen flickered as the district feed tried and failed to restore. A synthetic voice began, “Residents are advised to remain—” then cut into static.

Mrs Cole shouted, “We are remaining!”

Everyone laughed. It was too loud, the way laughter gets when people are relieved not to be frightened for one second.

At midday, Liv arrived with her daughter, Ada, and a box of battery lamps. Ada was seven, serious, and unimpressed by most adults. She had Liv’s eyes and someone else’s gentleness.

“School’s closed,” Liv said.

“Because of the outage?”

“Because nobody could prove which children were there.”

Owen laughed, then realised she was not joking.

Ada had a backpack full of paper books. She sat under a table and read while adults moved around her like weather.

By afternoon, Kilnwork had become what official language would later call an informal resilience node. At the time it was just people using what worked. Mark and two apprentices fixed the pharmacy shutter. Kelly organised lifts. Jamal posted a plain message telling people The Kiln was open, then spent twenty minutes refusing to let the engagement optimiser rewrite it as a “community moment.” Liv took over the back room for children whose parents had shifts. Owen climbed onto the roof in the rain to adjust a relay because the teenager who actually knew what he was doing was scared of heights.

From up there, he could see the district in pieces. Smart windows dark. Delivery rovers stranded beside puddles. A drone circling uselessly above the library mast. People in bright coats walking from door to door.

His phone buzzed inside his jacket.

Hearth again.

He ignored it.

That evening, after the council systems limped back and the room began to empty, Owen found Jamal alone on the stage. He was watching old footage on the wall: the school showcase from 2029, upscaled by some memory tool into impossible clarity. There was fourteen-year-old Owen, all elbows and panic, playing with his thumb after dropping the pick. There was Liv at the drums. There was Jamal, thin-faced and grinning, looking at the camera even then.

“I didn’t ask you to put that on,” Owen said.

“I know.”

“Then why?”

Jamal paused the clip on the moment the lights failed. The room on screen was grainy despite the enhancement. The children looked unbearably young.

“Because I’ve got six million views on a fake advert for a pub that never existed,” he said. “And this is the only thing I’ve made this month that I believe.”

Owen sat beside him.

Jamal’s online fame had become large and strangely worthless. Synthetic nostalgia had swallowed his best tricks. Anyone could make any town look like any memory. His face still opened doors, but only doors he did not want to enter.

“Hearth would pay you,” Owen said.

“Hearth would copy me.”

“Same thing, lately.”

Jamal nodded. “You selling?”

Owen looked out over the empty floor. The lamps were still on. Someone had left a child’s drawing on the stage: a square building with yellow windows and rain coming down in straight blue lines. Above it, in careful letters, Ada had written THE KILM.

He thought of the roof. His dad’s knee. The workshop money. The exhaustion of being responsible for a place that could not love him back except by needing him.

Liv came in, carrying mugs.

“Ada wants to know if she can come here again when the world isn’t broken.”

“Tell her we can’t promise that.”

“Which bit?”

“The world.”

Liv handed him tea and sat on the edge of the stage.

For a while they watched the frozen school hall. Owen felt, as he often did now, both trapped by the life he had chosen and insulted by the idea of any other.

His phone buzzed again.

He took it out. Hearth’s revised offer waited in clean, patient language. Beneath it was a message from Evie.

Just read it before saying no.

Owen did read it. He read the money. He read the roof provision. He read the clause giving Hearth rights to Kilnwork’s archive, name, venue style, operating patterns, trust metrics and staff likenesses for “regional authenticity modelling.”

He pictured a hundred Kilns opening in places that had never smelled of damp coats. He pictured his dad as a charming old repair archetype. His mum as reception warmth. Jamal as local wit. Liv as human music. Himself as the man who stood there when systems failed, licensed and replicated until he was everywhere and nowhere.

He declined.

The wall did not flash. No music played. Nobody noticed except Jamal, who looked at his face and understood.

“Daft,” Jamal said softly.

“Probably.”

Liv smiled into her mug.

Owen put the phone away.

Outside, the district lights came back in patches, one street at a time.

## Part II — The Intelligence Dividend

### 2029

Noor Rahman hated the hospital at night because everything kind sounded false.

The lights were kind. The signs were kind. The voice from the check-in column was kind. Even the chairs had been shaped by someone trying to imagine fear and reduce it by eight percent.

At two in the morning, with Sami asleep against her shoulder and her mother speaking too quietly to the triage screen, Noor wanted something less kind and more certain.

Her brother’s hand was hot in hers. He was nine and small in the way children become small when adults watch them too carefully. A rash had risen along his neck after dinner, not dramatic enough for an ambulance, too familiar to ignore. Their dad had stayed home with Nani because nobody wanted Nani in A&E again after last time, when she told the waiting-room assistant it had no soul and then apologised to it when it found her a blanket.

The triage column pulsed blue.

“Based on submitted symptoms and history,” it said, “Sami Rahman should be seen within twelve minutes.”

Farah closed her eyes.

Noor felt the shape of the night change.

Twelve minutes meant the system had found something. Twelve minutes meant they would not sit until sunrise watching drunk men bleed into paper towels while Sami pretended not to be scared. Twelve minutes meant the new model had read his history properly, including the rare marker the old systems always buried under hay fever and parental anxiety.

“See?” Sami whispered, trying to grin. “VIP.”

“Shut up,” Noor said, which meant I love you and don’t die.

He leaned harder against her.

On the wall, a public information video explained the National Health Intelligence Pilot in calm animation. Little blue lines connected homes to clinics, pharmacies to hospitals, blood tests to models, models to doctors, doctors to families. The people in the animation had round heads and no bills. A subtitle said: faster care, safer decisions, human oversight.

Farah watched it with the expression she used when a medicine advert skipped the side effects.

“Mum?”

“Hmm?”

“Is it good?”

Farah looked at Sami, then at the corridor where a nurse was already coming toward them.

“Tonight,” she said, “yes.”

Noor remembered that answer because adults rarely gave clean ones anymore.

At school the next day, she fell asleep in history.

Not properly. Just enough for her head to dip and Priya to jab her with a pen. Mr Ellis, who still wrote dates on the board even though the board could write them itself, looked over.

“Noor?”

“I’m listening.”

“Excellent. Then perhaps you can tell us why the rail strikes of the 2020s mattered.”

Priya mouthed something unhelpful.

Noor stared at the board. Transport. Labour. Automation. Public trust. The answer glowed faintly in the corner because the classroom assistant had started suggesting response prompts after the school’s trial update. Mr Ellis had turned the feature off three times. It turned itself back on whenever the board rebooted.

She ignored it.

“Because people couldn’t get places,” she said.

The class laughed.

Mr Ellis smiled. “True, if not yet history.”

At break, Priya dragged her to the toilets, where everyone went to talk because the school had installed corridor audio monitors after a safeguarding audit and then insisted they were not listening unless something happened.

“You look dead,” Priya said.

“Sami.”

Priya’s face changed at once. “Bad?”

“Better now.”

“Good.”

They stood by the sinks while two Year 10 girls took pictures with a filter that made their uniforms look vintage and expensive. One of them said, “My tutor says GCSEs are basically dead anyway.”

Priya’s eyes flashed. “People with tutors always say exams don’t matter.”

“You have a tutor.”

“I have my cousin’s login until she notices.”

Priya cared about grades with a ferocity Noor admired and found exhausting. Her parents ran a pharmacy and a mortgage and a private family mythology in which effort always produced escape. Priya had three revision systems: school, cousin login, and one she had built herself with a model that bullied her in her own voice.

Noor had a school planner with flowers drawn around the maths homework she had not done.

“You coming Saturday?” Priya asked.

“Where?”

“Theo’s thing.”

Noor groaned. “Is he still doing buildings?”

“He calls them civic spaces.”

“He is fourteen.”

“You arrange birthday rotas.”

“That’s different. Birthdays exist.”

Priya checked herself in the mirror. “His model got selected by the borough youth panel.”

“His model?”

“Not that kind. Like, a plan.”

Noor pictured Theo Mercer, quiet, long-fingered, always drawing impossible libraries in the margins of science worksheets. He spoke so rarely that when he did, everyone treated it as an event. Last month he had told a supply teacher the classroom layout made “public shame too efficient” and then gone back to sharpening his pencil.

“Maybe,” Noor said.

“That means no.”

“It means maybe.”

Priya gave her the look.

At lunch, the school announced new rules for AI use. They were read by the deputy head during assembly while four hundred pupils sat on the floor and pretended not to message each other.

No private tutors during school hours. No generated coursework without declaration. No emotional companion apps for pupils under sixteen on school devices. No using prediction tools to estimate teacher behaviour. No synthetic images of staff, living or dead.

That last one caused a ripple.

Noor sat between Priya and a boy who smelled of cheese crisps. Her phone buzzed in her blazer pocket with a message from her dad.

Sami home. Eating toast. Says hospital socks elite.

Noor smiled down at her lap.

The deputy head kept talking.

“We know the world is changing. But your education is still about your effort, your knowledge, your character and your choices.”

Priya whispered, “And your cousin’s login.”

Noor pressed her lips together.

She wanted to believe the deputy head. Not because the speech was good. It was not. She wanted to believe her effort belonged to her. She wanted school to be a place where things added up: revise, remember, pass, proceed. She wanted hospitals to say twelve minutes and mean it. She wanted Sami’s body to stop being a puzzle adults had to keep solving.

That Saturday, she went to Theo’s thing because Sami was asleep, her mother was working, her father said she had to leave the house, and Priya sent twelve messages ending with a threat to tell everyone Noor still watched 2021 medical dramas for comfort.

The borough youth panel met in a library room above a shopping centre. There were biscuits in packets and a screen showing a rotating model of a building with a garden on the roof.

Theo stood beside it, looking as if he might apologise to the floor.

“It’s not a youth centre,” he said when someone called it one. “It’s more like a place where people can bring things that don’t fit anywhere else.”

Noor liked that immediately and resented liking it.

The plan had rooms for health appointments, study, cooking, repairs, arguments, performances and “quiet failure,” which made several adults nod as if they understood and several teenagers look up because they did.

An older councillor asked whether AI moderation would reduce staffing costs.

Theo’s ears went red.

“It shouldn’t be for reducing people,” he said.

The room went still in the way rooms did when a child accidentally said the adult thing too plainly.

Afterwards, Noor found him by the biscuit table.

“Quiet failure is a weird phrase.”

Theo looked alarmed until he realised she was not being cruel.

“It’s what school doesn’t have,” he said.

Noor took a custard cream. “School has loads of failure.”

“Not quiet.”

She thought of the classroom assistant glowing answers at the edge of the board. She thought of Sami trying to joke in A&E. She thought of Priya’s model bullying her into revision. She thought of herself falling asleep in history while the right answer hovered in public.

“Yeah,” she said. “Fair.”

That night, Sami had energy enough to sit at the kitchen table and rank hospital snacks. Farah cooked eggs at nearly midnight because everyone’s body clock had given up. Imran told a story about a man at the travel office who once tried to book a holiday to “somewhere with no weather.”

Nani fed everyone as if calories were a legal defence.

Noor watched them and felt the little lift that came after a crisis passed: guilt braided with relief, love sharpened by fear. The hospital model had worked. The school rules did not. Theo’s building might never be built. Priya would probably get a nine in everything and still feel hunted.

Sami held up a triangle of toast.

“To the socks,” he said.

They toasted with tea, water, eggs, toast.

For one evening, the future did not matter because everyone was home.

### 2037

Noor’s contribution interview took place in a room that used to be a bank.

The vault was still there, sealed behind glass and turned into a quiet booth for difficult conversations. Someone had planted herbs in the old cashiers’ counter. Above the entrance, where a bank name had once promised security, brushed metal letters now read: Civic House — Stratford East.

Noor arrived twelve minutes early and still felt late.

At twenty-two, she had become good at entering rooms where nobody knew which version of adulthood they were performing. The people waiting with her were dressed in every possible theory of usefulness. One man wore a suit with trainers and kept asking his wrist for market updates even though market salaries mattered less than they used to. A woman in paint-stained trousers slept with her head against a window. Two boys younger than Noor whispered about a climate restoration guild in Portugal. An older man stared at the contribution menu like it had personally insulted him.

The menu floated on the wall.

Care and presence.
Local food systems.
Dispute mediation.
Youth work.
Water and coast.
Public memory.
Repair.
Arts and ritual.
Open research support.
Unassigned.

Unassigned had the longest queue.

Noor’s phone buzzed.

Priya: if they offer you youth work say no. typecasting.

Noor: you’re literally in a prestige research guild.

Priya: exactly. escape the brand early.

Noor smiled.

Priya had become one of those people whose life looked inevitable backwards. At fourteen, cousin login. At sixteen, scholarship. At eighteen, national prize. At twenty-two, an associate place at a private-public biomedical guild with buildings nobody was allowed to photograph from the outside. She complained constantly about hierarchy while climbing it with both hands.

Noor loved her and sometimes wanted to mute her for a year.

The interview door opened. “Noor Rahman?”

The woman inside was human. That still surprised Noor in official settings, though it should not have. The compact had rules about human presence in life-direction decisions. The model could recommend. A person had to sit there and be looked at.

“I’m Mara,” the woman said. “Tea?”

“No, thanks.”

“Water?”

“No.”

“You can say yes to things here.”

Noor laughed because it was almost annoying.

They sat by a window overlooking the high street. A delivery bot waited patiently behind a man arguing with a pram. Across the road, an old betting shop had become a free legal clinic. Next to it, a bubble tea place was still a bubble tea place, which Noor found comforting.

Mara glanced at her notes, then turned the screen slightly so Noor could see. That was another rule now. If someone was going to summarise you, you got to watch.

“You’ve done hospital family support volunteering, school peer mediation, community meals, some translation work, and a short placement in health navigation.”

“Yeah.”

“You stopped the health placement early.”

“My brother got better.”

Mara waited.

Noor hated skilled waiting.

“That sounds bad,” she said. “I mean, I started it because of him. Then it felt weird when he didn’t need it.”

“Weird how?”

Noor looked out the window. A child was trying to make the delivery bot say please. The bot waited, beaten by manners.

“Like I was hanging around someone else’s emergency after mine had ended.”

Mara nodded and wrote nothing, which Noor appreciated.

Sami was seventeen now and irritatingly alive. He cycled too fast. He ate street food Farah considered microbial roulette. He had a girlfriend nobody was allowed to call his girlfriend. He had applied to a marine robotics programme in Hull without telling Noor, then accused her of wanting him preserved like jam when she reacted badly.

He was not wrong, which made it worse.

“What do you want?” Mara asked.

Noor almost answered with something acceptable. To help. To contribute. To work with people. To support community resilience. The phrases lay around every civic room like spare chairs.

Instead she said, “I don’t know what counts.”

Mara smiled a little. “Counts to who?”

That was the problem with human interviewers. They asked the question under the question.

By 2037, Noor had enough to live. Not luxuriously, not carelessly, but enough. The dividend came monthly. Housing was still complicated. Status was worse. Nobody knew whether to envy a person with a job, pity them, suspect them of ambition, or ask what they had done to need one. Some people wore busyness like jewellery. Others cultivated slowness. Her father said half the country had become poets and the other half had become committee members, and both were impossible to invoice.

Noor wanted to matter in a way that did not feel like performing mattering.

“To me,” she said at last.

Mara nodded as if that were a practical answer.

The placement she chose was called Water and Coast: Essex Renewal Cluster. Priya sent three crying emojis and one map.

Priya: babe that is basically mud.

Noor: it has people.

Priya: mud with people.

Her parents were supportive in different languages. Farah asked about accommodation, governance, healthcare access, the nearest hospital and whether Noor’s contribution credits would transfer if the project failed. Imran said the coast was good for the soul and then spent two hours reading about flood maps. Nani packed spices in jars and said people near the sea never salted properly.

Sami barely looked up from his wall game.

“Cool,” he said.

Noor threw a sock at him.

He caught it without looking. His reflexes were obscene now, trained by adaptive games that changed around him faster than Noor could watch.

“You’ll miss me when I’m gone,” she said.

“You’ll call every day and ask if I drank water.”

“Because you’re made of crisps.”

He paused the game. “I’m not sick anymore.”

The sentence landed badly because it was true and because he had saved it like a weapon.

Noor folded another jumper.

“I know.”

“Do you?”

Farah called from downstairs before Noor had to answer.

The coastal town was called Harwich Common now, though older residents still called it by whatever bit they meant and ignored the civic rebrand. It had wind, gulls, modular flood walls, empty arcades, new clinics, old pubs, temporary housing and a Civic House built in a former discount furniture store. Noor’s room was above a bakery run by three sisters and a scheduling model they bullied like a fourth.

Her first week, she learned that the intelligence commons could translate planning law into five languages, optimise drainage proposals, simulate flood response, generate meeting summaries, detect mould risk from council photos and still fail to stop two neighbours from arguing for forty minutes about a fence.

Theo Mercer was there.

She recognised him from behind first: long, narrow, head tilted as if listening to a building. He was standing in the Civic House kitchen, explaining to a contractor that a corridor was too clean.

“Too clean?” Noor said.

He turned. His face opened slowly.

“Noor Rahman.”

“Theo Civic Spaces Mercer.”

He laughed. At fourteen his laugh had seemed borrowed. At twenty-two it fit.

“You came for the mud.”

“Apparently.”

He showed her the building. Rooms for repair. Rooms for argument. A kitchen big enough to make people stay. A quiet failure room, actually labelled Rest Room because adults remained cowards. The walls could display summaries of public meetings, but Theo had insisted on corkboards as well because people trusted paper differently when angry.

“Did they build your library thing?” Noor asked.

“Parts of it. Badly. Then better.”

“That sounds like society.”

He looked pleased in a way he tried to hide.

The work was harder than Noor expected, and not because the systems were bad. They were often excellent. Too excellent, sometimes. They produced options faster than people could develop feelings about them. A flood plan could be simulated in minutes. Moving someone’s mother from a ground-floor flat took months of guilt, memory, suspicion and tea.

Noor learned names. She learned who would not speak if a screen was on. She learned which men pretended not to understand forms because forms made them feel stupid. She learned that dividend life had not made people lazy, despite what angry feeds said. It had made some people bloom and some people drift and some people furious that they had survived long enough to be asked what they wanted.

One Thursday, Priya visited.

She arrived in a self-driving guild car that looked embarrassed by the seafront. She wore a coat that adjusted its warmth in panels and complained about the smell of chips while eating half of Noor’s.

“So this is where ambition comes to nap,” Priya said.

“You’re horrible.”

“I’m jealous.”

“No, you’re not.”

Priya looked toward the Civic House, where a group of teenagers were painting chairs in colours the planning assistant had definitely not approved.

“Sometimes,” she said. “A bit.”

Noor waited because Priya only said true things sideways.

“The guild is amazing,” Priya said. “Like, actually amazing. We’ve got models catching interactions nobody saw. Trials are faster. My supervisor thinks we’ll crack two autoimmune pathways by Christmas.”

Noor thought of Sami.

“That’s good.”

“It is.” Priya kicked a stone. “And everyone is unbearable. If you take a weekend off, someone says rest is part of excellence in a way that means they logged six hours from bed.”

Noor laughed.

“You’re still competing.”

“Everyone is still competing. The prize just keeps changing outfit.”

That night, Noor called home. Sami answered from a train.

“Why are you on a train?”

“Marine programme visit.”

“In Hull?”

“Maybe.”

“Sami.”

He turned the camera to show dark glass and his reflection grinning.

“I told Mum.”

“You told Mum because Mum can’t chase a train.”

“Exactly.”

She wanted to say, You were sick. She wanted to say, I held your hand while machines decided how frightened we should be. She wanted to say, You don’t get to just become someone who leaves.

Instead she said, “Send me the address.”

He softened. “I will.”

“And drink water.”

He groaned and hung up.

Noor sat on her narrow bed above the bakery, listening to the night delivery bots roll over cobbles and the sea worrying the wall beyond the road.

For the first time in years, nobody urgently needed her.

She had thought that would feel like freedom.

It did, sometimes. Other times it felt like standing in a room after the music stopped, holding a plate nobody had asked her to bring.

### 2045

The old delivery driver came to Civic House every Tuesday to complain about the chairs.

His name was Alan Price, no relation to anyone Noor knew, and he had opinions about lumbar support that bordered on spiritual. He had driven vans for thirty-four years until the insurance markets made human driving expensive, then briefly supervised vehicle swarms, then retired into the dividend, which he treated as a clerical error he intended to correct through resentment.

“These chairs make a man soft,” he said.

Noor looked at him over her tea. “You’ve been sitting in that one for two hours.”

“Research.”

At thirty, Noor had become the kind of person people asked for without knowing her job title. The Civic House at Harwich Common had grown into a clinic, kitchen, assembly room, repair desk, youth hall, flood office, argument chamber and unofficial waiting room for lives in transition. Noor coordinated nothing and everything. The public systems handled scheduling, translation, benefit navigation, legal triage, health monitoring, meeting summaries, procurement and maintenance forecasts. Noor handled the moment when Mrs Bell refused a relocation plan because the model had not known her husband’s ashes were under the rose bush.

The world around her was better than the one she had grown up in. She believed that even on bad days. Sami was alive, healthy and insufferable from three time zones away. Farah’s hospital had treatments that would have sounded like miracles in 2029. Imran mediated community disputes with an assistant that could summarise forty years of property records while he made tea. Nani had died two winters earlier after accusing three separate care models of flirting with her. She had been ninety-one and correct about at least one of them.

People were safer. Not all people. Not everywhere. Not always. But enough that denial felt obscene.

Still, Tuesday chairs mattered.

“Alan,” Noor said, “you could join the port-history project.”

“I don’t want to tell stories to children who think 2020 was black and white.”

“You could help with driving simulations.”

“So fake vans can pretend to listen?”

“You could run deliveries for the kitchen.”

“I am not a charity case.”

Noor leaned back. “Then why are you here?”

He glared at her. His hands, broad and mottled, gripped the arms of the offending chair.

“Because at home the walls ask if I’m lonely.”

There it was.

Noor nodded, not too much.

“We’re cooking on Thursday,” she said. “Come peel potatoes badly.”

“I peel potatoes beautifully.”

“Prove it.”

He came Thursday.

That was most of the work: not solving people, just leaving a door open in the shape they could bear.

The intelligence commons had become so ordinary that children complained about it. They hated civic models the way Noor had hated school printers: as infrastructure that failed personally. The systems were good enough to be blamed. They could get a person legal advice in minutes, detect disease risk from a breath test, coordinate local energy, translate meetings in real time, tutor a child through calculus, simulate ten flood plans and produce a grief letter so moving that nobody admitted using it.

They could not make Alan feel proud to peel potatoes.

They could not make Sami stay on Earth.

He told Noor on a Sunday call, while she was folding laundry.

“It’s not off-world,” he said.

“If you begin with that, it’s off-world.”

“Low orbit first.”

Noor sat down on the bed.

Sami was twenty-five now, taller than their father, with Farah’s eyebrows and a habit of smiling before delivering bad news. He worked with marine robotics, then polar systems, then high-altitude infrastructure, each move described as temporary until the next one. The new project maintained orbital energy and compute platforms that supported climate modelling, materials research and, according to Sami, “boring infrastructure stuff.”

“Boring things don’t require low orbit,” Noor said.

“That’s not true. Most dangerous things are boring once engineers get involved.”

“Don’t flirt with me using engineering.”

He laughed. Then stopped because she did not.

“Noor.”

She hated how gentle he sounded.

“You nearly died because your immune system thought you were a group project,” she said. “I’m allowed to be annoying.”

“You are. You’ve excelled.”

She covered her face.

On her wall, the house assistant dimmed the light because it misread her posture as migraine onset. She told it no. It apologised and brightened the room. She wanted, irrationally, to throw a sock at it.

“Mum knows?” she asked.

“Mum knows.”

“Dad?”

“Dad sent me four articles and one poem.”

“Nani would have said space has no proper food.”

Sami smiled. “She did say that. To me. In 2038.”

Noor looked at him. “You remember?”

“I remember loads.”

Of course he did. He had been there too. Not just as the child in the hospital bed, not just as the reason everyone packed bags at midnight and knew which nurses were kind. He had been watching them arrange their lives around his body. Perhaps all this leaving was not recklessness. Perhaps it was revenge against being loved too carefully.

“I’m proud of you,” she said, and cried in the ugly way that made him look away to give her privacy through the screen.

The day before he left, the town held a contribution evening.

These events had begun as policy and become something closer to ritual. People brought work that did not fit old work: a repaired bench, a poem, a care rota, a flood-drill map, a meal, a game built for children with tremors, a legal guide translated into Somali, a chair design Alan insisted did not make men soft. The models archived everything. The people decided what mattered.

Priya came from London, late and glittering with exhaustion. Her guild had helped validate a treatment that changed three diseases and produced nine lawsuits about access priority. She had money now, real private money, though she still stole chips from Noor’s plate.

“Your town is sentimental,” Priya said.

“Your coat costs more than Alan’s first van.”

“And yet I’m cold.”

They stood at the back of the hall while Theo adjusted the lights. He and Noor had tried romance once, gently, like testing a bridge. It had held for a while and then become friendship again, which suited them better and annoyed everyone who wanted a cleaner story.

On stage, Alan presented his chair.

“This,” he said, “is a chair for people who have not given up.”

The hall applauded as if this were a reasonable design category.

Sami stood beside Noor, taller than her, warm-shouldered and alive. She leaned into him without meaning to.

“You’ll visit?” she said.

“From low orbit?”

“Don’t be clever.”

“Yes.”

“You’ll eat properly?”

“No.”

She pinched his arm.

He laughed, and for one second he was nine again with hospital socks and toast crumbs, and she loved him so fiercely it felt like anger.

Later, after the hall emptied, Noor carried mugs to the kitchen. Theo was washing up. The public model had already summarised the evening, tagged contributions, updated community records and drafted thank-you notes. Noor deleted the notes. She wrote her own badly, slowly, with too many commas.

Theo watched her.

“The model’s are better,” he said.

“I know.”

“So?”

She shrugged. “Mine take longer.”

He smiled because he understood that this was the point.

Outside, the sea wall lights blinked green along the edge of town. Above them, somewhere too high to see, machines moved around Earth carrying sunlight, heat, risk, ambition and Sami Rahman.

Noor stood in the kitchen doorway with wet hands and listened to Alan argue with a teenager about potato peel thickness. The world had become astonishing and still people needed somewhere to be ridiculous together.

She had spent half her life trying to be needed.

At thirty, she was beginning to understand that being needed was not the same as being loved, and being free was not the same as being finished.

## Part III — The Surplus World

### 2029

Tia Morgan learned to make her homework worse because perfect homework got questions.

Not from teachers at first. Teachers were tired and outnumbered and mostly grateful if words arrived in the right box before midnight. The questions came from the school system, which had opinions about sentence rhythm, vocabulary distance, household device history, pupil baseline, peer similarity and whether a girl who got a four in English last term was likely to produce a paragraph about dramatic irony with “sustained conceptual control.”

Tia did not know what sustained conceptual control meant. She knew it sounded like trouble.

So she wrote the first version with the assistant, then broke it. She put in two spelling mistakes, moved one comma to a stupid place, deleted a good sentence and replaced “Macbeth’s moral disintegration” with “Macbeth getting worse.” The system liked that better. It called the work plausible.

Plausible was a word adults used when they meant poor enough to be yours.

At school, the corridors smelled of wet coats and body spray. The building was the same one her mum had gone to, though now it had facial gates that worked unless it rained, which in Port Talbot meant they worked as a theory. On bad mornings half the pupils queued at reception while a secretary named Mrs Vale overrode the system with the expression of a woman personally betrayed by progress.

Tia liked Mrs Vale. Mrs Vale did not say things would be fine.

That Tuesday, Ellis was flagged at the gate.

He was eleven, all knees and fringe, with the quick, bright look of a child who could either win prizes or get recruited by criminals depending on who noticed first. He stood in the scanner box with his bag hanging off one shoulder while the gate blinked amber.

“Again?” Tia said.

“I didn’t do anything.”

“That’s never mattered.”

Mrs Vale looked at her screen. “Ellis Morgan. Behavioural review pending.”

Tia felt heat move up her neck. “For what?”

“It doesn’t say for me, love.”

Ellis stared at the floor.

Behind them a boy groaned, “Move.”

Tia turned. “Say it again.”

He looked at her face and decided not to.

Mrs Vale pressed override. “Go on. I’ll log him as present.”

“Will that fix it?”

Mrs Vale lowered her voice. “It will fix nine o’clock.”

That was another adult answer Tia remembered.

In maths, the board tutor explained simultaneous equations to nobody in particular. Mr Preece moved between desks trying to help, but the tutor was faster and less embarrassed when pupils ignored it. Seren Jones sat beside Tia, painting clear gloss over nails already too long for school rules.

“I made eighteen quid last night,” Seren whispered.

“Doing what?”

“Sleep stream.”

“You streamed yourself sleeping?”

“Not myself. My sleep face.”

“That’s not a thing.”

Seren grinned. “It is if men are lonely.”

Tia made a disgusted noise.

“Not like that,” Seren said. “It’s cosy. I trained it on my face and voice and it says goodnight to people. Barely me.”

“That’s worse.”

“Eighteen quid worse?”

Tia had no answer to that. Eighteen quid was electricity on prepay. Eighteen quid was Ellis’s trainers from the discount bin. Eighteen quid was her mum not doing the face she did when the fridge sounded empty.

At lunch, Ellis found her by the fence.

“I got a message,” he said.

He showed her his phone. The app was bright green and called FutureForge Junior. It said Ellis had been identified as having high adaptive-systems potential and could join a free challenge league with prizes, mentoring and “pathway visibility.”

“Delete it,” Tia said.

“Why?”

“Because it’s weird.”

“Everything’s weird.”

“Ellis.”

“It says I’m good.”

That stopped her.

The message did say that. Not in those words, but close enough. It had found him in whatever mess of school scores, game data, device behaviour and cheap public records floated around children now, and it had told him he might be special.

Tia hated it for knowing where to press.

“Show Mam,” she said.

“She’ll say no.”

“Then there’s your answer.”

He snatched the phone back. “You say no to everything.”

“Because you say yes to scams.”

“You’re not my mum.”

He regretted it at once. Tia saw it, which made her angrier.

Their mum, Rhiannon, worked twelve-hour shifts in a care home chain that had recently installed remote mood monitoring for residents and productivity prompts for staff. The system told her when Mrs Davies was likely lonely. Rhiannon said she did not need a graph for that. The system told her to spend seven minutes providing reassurance. Rhiannon said Mrs Davies had lost a husband, not a bus.

Their dad, Dean, had been home for six months.

Not unemployed, officially. In transition. Under review. Awaiting pathway. Appealing a missed retraining appointment he swore had never appeared. Some days he fixed things for cash. Some days he sat in the kitchen watching job-matching clips with his jaw tight enough to crack teeth.

Tia looked at Ellis’s bright, stubborn face and wanted to put him somewhere nobody could price him.

“Just don’t sign up yet,” she said.

He walked away without promising.

After school she went to boxing.

The gym was above a carpet warehouse and smelled of sweat, rubber and rain in the walls. Coach Delyth still used paper sign-in sheets because she said anything important deserved handwriting. Tia wrapped her hands badly and hit pads until the day stopped buzzing under her skin.

“You’re angry,” Delyth said.

“No.”

“Then you’re punching stupid for fun?”

Tia hit harder.

“Feet.”

She fixed her feet.

The gym had girls whose families owned houses and girls whose families moved when rents changed and girls who arrived in cars that drove themselves to the better side of town afterwards. In the ring, for three rounds, none of that vanished. It just had to wait outside the ropes.

When Tia got home, Dean was at the kitchen table with his appeal open on the wall.

The system said: Claimant declined reskilling appointment, 14 March 2029.

Dean said, “I was at your nan’s funeral.”

The system said: Upload evidence.

He had uploaded the funeral notice, the travel receipt, a photo of himself carrying the coffin, and a message from his sister calling him useless for crying in the car park. The system had accepted the documents and maintained the decision.

Rhiannon stood behind him in her uniform, too tired to take off her shoes.

“There’s a help line,” she said.

Dean laughed once. “There’s a voice line.”

Tia put her bag down.

“I can try.”

Her dad looked at her, and the shame in his face made her wish she had not spoken. He was not ashamed because she offered. He was ashamed because he wanted to say yes.

She sat beside him. The appeal form had fourteen sections and a tone like a locked door. Tia asked an assistant to make the explanation clearer. It produced something clean and hopeless. She deleted it and wrote:

My father could not attend because he was burying his mother. The system has made a mistake.

It sounded childish. It sounded true.

That night, Ellis signed up for FutureForge Junior without telling anyone.

Tia found out because he won the first challenge and the app sent a congratulations animation to the household display at breakfast.

Dean smiled for the first time in days.

“That’s my boy.”

Rhiannon looked uncertain. “What is it?”

Ellis glowed.

Tia watched the green light move over his face and felt, horribly, that she might be the only person in the room who could see the hook.

### 2037

By twenty-two, Tia had learned that queues were where the country hid its violence.

Not the dramatic kind. Nobody was bleeding in the municipal access centre. Nobody was shouting yet. There were plastic chairs, wall screens, a vending machine, two security guards and a children’s corner with wipe-clean books. The violence was in the numbering system that kept moving people backwards. In the way the eligibility assistant asked for documents people had already given. In the way every rejected appeal arrived in language so clean it left no fingerprints.

Tia held ticket B-184.

The screen was on B-067.

Her mum sat beside her in care-home shoes, rubbing her thumb over a split in the fake leather. Rhiannon’s chain had cut staff again after installing overnight companion units that could sing, monitor, alert, reminisce and call a human if a resident tried to die outside expected parameters. The residents hated them until they did not. The families liked the reports. The staff were now “exception handlers,” which meant they handled everything the machines could not handle and got blamed for the exceptions.

“I can come back tomorrow,” Rhiannon said.

“You said that yesterday.”

“I’ve got Mrs Davies’s daughter visiting at four.”

“Mrs Davies’s daughter can wait.”

“She comes from Neath.”

Tia stared at her.

Rhiannon smiled weakly. “Fine.”

They were there because their household support had been reduced after Ellis moved into a residential pathway run by Verity Shield, a private security-intelligence contractor with government accreditation, glossy adverts and contracts written like traps. The system had classified him as no longer dependent. It had not classified the food he still ate when he came home, the money Rhiannon sent him, the panic attacks he pretended were fitness alerts, or the fact that Tia could feel him slipping from the family one approved access badge at a time.

Her phone buzzed.

Mal: got a slot if you want it.

Tia looked across the room. Mal was not there, but Mal was everywhere if you were desperate enough. He was twenty-four, soft-spoken, neat, with a barbered beard and a talent for finding the side doors in systems built to have none. Device repairs, access tokens, score reviews, appointment swaps, identity clean-ups. Nothing violent. Nothing free.

Tia typed: no.

Mal: you like chairs that much?

She put the phone away.

Seren would have said yes. Seren said yes to everything and somehow landed on her feet, though the ground under her kept changing. At twenty-two she made money through presence markets: wake-up voices, sleep loops, grief cameos, argument partners, flirt feeds, memory skins. Some of it was her. Some of it was licensed versions of her. Some of it was stolen versions she chased through takedown agents that cost more than the theft lost her.

“Men are lonely,” she still said, but she said it less like a joke now.

B-068.

Rhiannon sighed.

Tia opened the Verity Shield contract again. She had read it until the words became furniture. Ellis had signed for five years. Housing, food, advanced systems access, training, medical cover, family visit credits, pathway status. In exchange: behavioural compliance, movement restrictions, biometric training rights, data continuity, non-disparagement, emergency redeployment.

“He’s safe there,” Rhiannon said quietly.

“You haven’t seen the dorms.”

“You have?”

Tia did not answer.

She had visited once. The facility sat outside Cardiff behind trees planted too recently to hide anything. Everything was clean. The food was good. The beds were better than his bed at home. Ellis wore black training clothes and a wrist band that opened doors. He looked taller, calmer, proud in a way that made Tia feel cruel for hating it.

He showed her a simulation room where he and other recruits learned to manage swarms of defensive agents across synthetic networks. His scores were high. Of course they were. The app had found him at eleven and kept finding him until he belonged to it.

“This is a chance,” he told her.

“It’s a contract.”

“Everything’s a contract.”

“Not family.”

He looked away then, and she knew she had lost that round because she had made family sound like a debt.

B-069.

A man two rows ahead began shouting. A guard approached with the calm of someone whose body camera had already decided the first draft of the story.

The man shouted that his daughter’s work record was wrong. The assistant said it could not discuss third-party claims. He shouted that she was dead. The assistant expressed sympathy and repeated that it could not discuss third-party claims. The guard said, “Sir.”

Tia felt Rhiannon’s hand close around her wrist.

The man sat down. Not because he was less angry. Because anger had nowhere to go.

At three, they left without being seen because Rhiannon had to get to work. Outside, rain blew sideways across the pavement. A delivery crawler rolled past carrying premium groceries behind a locked panel. Someone had sprayed HUMAN SERVICE HUMAN PRICE on the wall opposite the centre. Under it, a smaller tag said U WISH.

Rhiannon stood under the bus shelter and looked suddenly old.

“I can ask Ellis,” she said. “Maybe his case officer can—”

“No.”

“Tia.”

“No.”

They rode home in silence.

That evening, Tia took a care shift for cash.

The old man’s name was Mr Iqbal, though he told her to call him Harun because “Mr Iqbal is my father and he is dead, thank God.” His family paid for human evening visits because he unplugged companion units and told the care chain he would haunt their shareholders. Tia cooked soup, checked the floor sensors, changed a dressing on his ankle and listened while he complained about three governments, two sons and the decline of mango quality.

“You are wasted in this work,” he said.

“I’m not in this work. I’m near it.”

“Near work is the only work now.”

She laughed.

He pointed his spoon at her. “You laugh because I am right.”

After the shift, Mal was waiting by the bus stop.

“That’s creepy.”

“That’s efficient.”

He handed her chips wrapped in paper.

“I didn’t ask.”

“You never ask.”

She took them anyway. Hunger had a way of humiliating principles.

They walked toward the seafront. The steelworks lights were fewer now. Warehouses glowed inland where logistics systems moved things no one local could afford. Above them, drones blinked red in the low clouds.

“I can get Ellis’s contract reviewed,” Mal said.

“By who?”

“Someone who knows what words mean.”

“Cost?”

“For you? Less.”

“That means still too much.”

He shrugged. “Everything costs. Even doing nothing.”

Tia stopped walking.

“Do you ever get tired of sounding like a villain in a children’s film?”

Mal smiled. “Villains have stable income.”

She wanted not to like him. She managed it most days.

Her phone rang. Ellis.

She answered too fast.

His face appeared in low light. He looked thinner. Older. Behind him, a dorm wall displayed a moving Verity Shield logo, blue lines folding into a shield, over and over.

“You okay?” she asked.

“Yeah. Why wouldn’t I be?”

“Because you call when you’re not.”

He looked off-screen. “I need you not to freak out.”

Tia’s hand tightened around the chips.

“They offered me accelerated placement.”

Mal, beside her, went still.

“Where?”

“External operations.”

“Ellis.”

“It’s good.”

“You’re eighteen.”

“That’s legal.”

“Legal is not the same as good.”

His face hardened. She had seen that expression on Dean when systems made him feel small. It frightened her more on Ellis.

“You don’t get it,” he said. “There’s nothing outside. You want me queueing with you forever?”

She flinched.

He saw it and looked sorry, then angry at the sorrow.

“I’m good at this,” he said. Softer. “They see that.”

There it was again. The hook, deeper now, dressed as recognition.

“I see it,” Tia said.

“You see danger.”

“Because there is danger.”

“There’s danger at home.”

The call ended badly, without goodbye.

Tia stood under the orange streetlight, chips cooling in her hand. Mal said nothing, which was the first decent thing he had done all night.

At home, Dean was asleep on the sofa with an appeal screen still open. Rhiannon had not returned from work. The kitchen smelled faintly of damp and toast.

Tia sat at the table and opened the Verity contract again.

This time, when Mal sent the review slot, she did not say no.

### 2045

The protected zone had grass too green to trust.

Tia noticed that first. Not the gates, not the drones, not the clean white clinic towers or the quiet roads where no one parked badly. The grass. It lay on either side of the arrival path in perfect spring, each blade bright after a week of rain that had turned the streets outside into grey runnels. A child could fall on that grass and not cut herself on glass. An old person could walk without checking for broken paving. Nobody had sprayed anything on the walls because there were no walls low enough to reach.

At thirty, Tia had learned to mistrust places that removed consequences from view.

She wore black trousers, a cream blouse Seren had once said made her look “appeal-hearing expensive,” and shoes that hurt by the time she reached the visitor pavilion. She had dressed carefully because she did not want Ellis’s colleagues to think he came from nowhere. She hated herself for that and kept walking.

The gate read her face, gait, token, temperature, compliance history and invitation. It opened without drama.

Inside the pavilion, a woman in a soft blue jacket smiled.

“Welcome, Ms Morgan. Ellis is finishing a debrief. He’ll be with you in seven minutes.”

Seven minutes meant they wanted her to know they were precise.

“Tea? Coffee? Water?”

“Water.”

The water came in a glass thin enough to make her nervous.

She sat by a window overlooking a courtyard where young recruits crossed between buildings. They looked healthy. That was the worst part. Not rich exactly. Not soft. Healthy in a maintained way, skin clear, posture good, clothes fitted to bodies measured by systems that noticed everything. They laughed like people under pressure but not under threat.

Outside the zone, the support credits had tightened again. Rhiannon’s care chain had collapsed after a companion unit failed to escalate a resident fall and the family published the footage. The public investigation found layered responsibility, which meant no person with a face. Residents were redistributed. Staff were reassessed. Rhiannon, fifty-six and limping, had been offered a remote reassurance role supervising synthetic carers for less money than she used to make touching actual hands.

Dean was gone now. Heart attack in 2041, three days after an appeal finally restored benefits for a period so old the back payment arrived with condolences.

Seren had vanished from feeds last winter after an identity clone dispute. For six weeks there were hundreds of her: sleepy Seren, cruel Seren, young Seren, grieving Seren, Seren selling men the feeling of being forgiven by someone who had never met them. She messaged Tia once — still here babes, don’t believe any premium me — then disappeared again.

Mal had become respectable in the way gatekeepers become respectable when enough people need gates. He ran an access office beside a church, charging less than criminals and more than justice. People brought him letters, scores, bans, licences, missing records, dead relatives still being billed. He fixed some. He owned pieces of others.

Tia worked with him when she had to and hated how often that was.

Ellis entered wearing a grey uniform with no visible logo. He was twenty-seven, taller than Dean had been, with Rhiannon’s mouth and a stillness that made Tia feel loud before she spoke.

For one second, he was her little brother at the school gate, amber-flagged and furious.

Then he smiled.

“T.”

She stood. He hugged her carefully, as if she might break or accuse him of it.

“You look fancy,” he said.

“You look institutional.”

He laughed. Good. She had needed him to laugh.

They walked through the courtyard. He showed her a clinic where models caught disease early enough that people spoke of cancer in the tone Tia’s childhood had reserved for fillings. He showed her a training hall, gardens, family housing, a memorial wall for operatives lost in external incidents. The names were etched without causes.

“You happy?” she asked.

He looked at her sideways. “You starting early?”

“Answer.”

They passed a group of children in matching green jumpers being led by a teacher and two quiet machines.

“I’m safe,” he said.

“That’s not what I asked.”

“It’s what you wanted.”

She could not argue because once, yes, safety had seemed like enough. Food, bed, medicine, doors that opened, people who saw his brightness and did not use it against him. She had wanted those things with such force that she had missed the small print life puts under rescue.

They ate lunch in a visitor dining room. The food was excellent and slightly under-seasoned. Ellis told her about infrastructure defence in words she understood individually and not together. He asked about Mam. He asked about Port Talbot with the careful nostalgia of someone who had escaped and did not know whether he was allowed to miss the cage.

“Mam won’t take the remote role,” Tia said.

“Can she afford not to?”

“No.”

He nodded and looked down.

“I can help.”

“With what money? They pay you in housing and moral superiority.”

“I have allocation rights.”

She put her fork down.

He winced. “Don’t do that.”

“Do what?”

“Hear everything as control.”

“Stop saying controlling things.”

For a moment they were not thirty and twenty-seven in a protected zone. They were in the kitchen again, him glowing green from an app, her furious because nobody else saw the hook.

He leaned back.

“There’s a pathway for family stabilisation,” he said. “It could get Mam medical cover, housing priority, proper support.”

Tia waited.

“There’s a community liaison role attached.”

“For who?”

He did not answer.

She laughed once. “There it is.”

“It’s not like that.”

“It is always like that.”

“You don’t know what I’m offering.”

“You’re offering to buy the family with me.”

His face closed.

“I’m trying to keep Mam alive.”

The sentence did what he meant it to do. It went straight to the place she had no armour.

After lunch, he took her to the edge of the zone where a glass walkway overlooked the outer road. Beyond the perimeter, people queued beside a mobile clinic. Some had appointments. Some hoped being near the door would become a kind of appointment if they waited long enough.

“We can’t take everyone,” Ellis said quietly.

Tia looked at him. “Do you hear yourself?”

“Every day.”

That stopped her.

Below, a woman shifted a sleeping child from one hip to the other. A man argued with a gate assistant. A teenager in a red hoodie stared up at the glass and saw Tia seeing him.

She stepped back.

On the train home, her shoes blistered both heels. She took them off and sat in stocking feet while the carriage screens played public safety notices about synthetic extortion, heat risk, household credit updates and a minister promising a review of access fairness. Across the aisle, a boy no older than sixteen edited his own face into a luxury-zone background and posted it with a caption about grinding.

Tia wanted to slap the phone out of his hand. She wanted to tell him the grass was too green. She wanted to tell him Ellis had looked well and trapped and powerful and used.

Instead she leaned her head against the window and watched wet fields slide past.

When she got home, Rhiannon was asleep in the chair. The flat was too warm because the cheap climate unit had two settings: damp and fever. On the table lay a letter about the remote reassurance role. Rhiannon had written pros and cons on the envelope.

Pros: money. home. knees.

Cons: not real.

Tia stood there for a long time.

Her phone buzzed.

Mal: heard you visited paradise.

Tia: heard you’re a gossip.

Mal: heard they’re hiring liaisons.

She did not reply.

A minute later:

Mal: careful. they don’t buy what they can’t use.

She hated him for being right in sentences that sounded stolen from films.

At midnight, Seren’s old account came alive.

For a second Tia thought it was really her. The face was older than the stolen skins, tired in the right way, hair tied up badly, one earring missing. The clip showed Seren sitting on a bathroom floor, laughing silently before speaking.

“If this is me, T, I owe you twenty quid. If it’s not me, I owe you more.”

Then the account vanished again.

Tia watched the clip six times and still could not tell.

In the morning she went to Mal’s office.

It sat beside the church, under a sign that said ACCESS SUPPORT / DOCUMENT REVIEW / HUMAN WITNESSING. Inside, people waited with folders, devices, children, anger. Mal looked up from behind a desk that had once belonged to a solicitor.

“No,” Tia said before sitting.

He raised an eyebrow.

“No to what?”

“Whatever clever thing you’re about to offer.”

“Shame. It was very clever.”

She sat anyway.

“I need to know what the liaison role actually is.”

Mal’s face changed. Not much. Enough.

“You don’t want that.”

“I didn’t ask what I want.”

He opened a private screen between them. The role was worse and better than she expected. Community stabilisation. Household access support. Risk mapping. Informal network reporting. Escalation liaison. Protection priority for named dependents. Behavioural conditions. Data continuity.

There were salaries that would save her mother. There were clauses that would sell pieces of everyone who trusted her.

Tia read slowly.

Outside, church bells rang because some old systems had never been worth automating.

“They asked you to suggest candidates?” she said.

Mal did not answer quickly enough.

She smiled without warmth. “Of course they did.”

“I didn’t give them your name.”

“Why not?”

“Because you’d be good at it.”

That was the first honest warning he had ever given her.

She left without deciding.

For three days, she carried the choice through the flat, the clinic queue, the care appeal, the boxing gym, the bus, the shop with locked formula, the street where boys flew cheap surveillance toys and pretended they were pilots. She imagined Rhiannon in proper housing with medical cover. She imagined Ellis relieved. She imagined Mal’s clients looking at her with the expression people used when they realised a door had been listening.

On the fourth day, she took Rhiannon to the old seafront.

The tide was out. The air smelled of salt, rust and fried food from a van that had survived every economy by selling chips to people with no better plan.

Rhiannon walked slowly. Her knees were bad. She refused the bench until Tia refused to keep walking.

“Ellis can help,” Tia said.

Her mother looked at the water.

“I know.”

“There’s a role.”

“For you?”

Tia laughed softly. “You knew?”

“I’m old, not decorative.”

A gull screamed above them.

Rhiannon took Tia’s hand. Her fingers were swollen from years of work that systems had measured and misunderstood.

“When you were little,” she said, “you used to stand in front of Ellis whenever a dog came near.”

“He was scared.”

“Sometimes. Sometimes he wanted to pet the dog.”

Tia looked away.

“I can’t watch you sell yourself to keep us safe,” Rhiannon said.

“We’re not safe.”

“No.”

That was the whole cruelty of it. Her mother did not lie. Safety was available, somewhere behind green grass, but not cleanly. Not for free. Not without turning love into leverage.

They sat until the wind made their eyes water.

That evening Tia messaged Ellis.

Can’t take it.

He replied after eleven minutes.

I know.

Then:

I’m sorry.

She stared at the words. They were not enough. They were more than she had expected.

At the gym, Coach Delyth was older and slower but still used paper sign-in sheets. Tia helped with the younger girls now. Some came for sport. Some came because the gym was one of the few places where being watched meant someone might intervene.

A thirteen-year-old named Cerys hit pads with wild, ugly force.

“Feet,” Tia said.

The girl fixed her feet.

“Again.”

Outside, systems priced and sorted and forgot. Inside, for three minutes, Cerys learned where to put her weight.

Tia held the pads and took the force through her arms.

She did not know if this was enough.

It was not enough.

It was still something.
