## 1. Why the previous draft is being preserved but replaced

The current `Three Lives After the Curve` draft succeeds in one important respect: the language often stays close to ordinary human life. The young people worry about friends, family, embarrassment, small loyalties and the pressure of being seen. That texture should be preserved.

It fails in the more important structural respect: the three timelines are not different enough. They repeatedly return to similar pressures of care, verification, institutional ambiguity and personal competence. Those are valid Stage 4 themes, but the user’s intended book form is sharper: three different future worlds, each built from a different technological and social condition set, each following one person through life.

The next draft must therefore preserve the human-scale texture while rebuilding the scenario mechanics.

## 3. Forecast anchors for scenario years

The same three setting years remain useful, but they must be used more carefully.

### 2029 — early-transition pressure

2029 is not used because the project predicts AGI by then. It is used because several public forecasts and stress tests make the late 2020s a plausible near-term pressure window: Kurzweil continues to point to 2029 for human-level AI; Grace et al. report expert forecasts assigning notable probability to major milestones by 2028; METR’s long-task work shows rapidly increasing software-task horizons with important caveats; AI 2027 uses the late 2020s as an aggressive scenario-planning window.

Use in the book: the school chapters. The school system still looks recognisably like today: subjects, timetables, homework, GCSE pressure, teacher workload, parental expectations, patchy access. The surrounding world has started to change, but teenagers mostly experience it through cheating rumours, parental stress, social status, school rules, money and uneven access.

Primary support: S4-WORLD-SRC-001, S4-WORLD-SRC-002, S4-WORLD-SRC-003, S4-WORLD-SRC-004.

### 2037 — system-reorganisation pressure

2037 is useful because Grace et al. report 10 percent forecast probability for all human occupations becoming fully automatable by that date, while other sources suggest major uncertainty about whether AI exposure becomes augmentation, substitution or organisational redesign. It is the right year for modelling young adulthood under changed labour markets without forcing a settled end-state.

Use in the book: the young adult chapters. The characters are old enough to experience the labour market directly. The worlds should now strongly diverge.

Primary support: S4-WORLD-SRC-002, S4-WORLD-SRC-005, S4-WORLD-SRC-006, S4-WORLD-SRC-008.

### 2045 — mature-divergence pressure

2045 is useful because it is Kurzweil’s singularity horizon and sits close to Grace et al.’s 2047 median forecast for unaided machines outperforming humans at every task. It is far enough out to model large divergence in social settlement, compute infrastructure and economic role while preserving uncertainty. Orbital compute may be used only as a radical boundary condition with strong caveats: papers describe possible architectures, but economic viability remains heavily constrained by launch cost, communications, replacement cadence, heat, radiation and utilisation.

Use in the book: the adult chapters. The question is no longer “is AI present?” It is “what kind of world did humans build around intelligence abundance or intelligence concentration?”

Primary support: S4-WORLD-SRC-001, S4-WORLD-SRC-002, S4-WORLD-SRC-007, S4-WORLD-SRC-009.

## 4. Worldspace matrix

The final book should explore three timelines that are intentionally different along the same axes.

| Axis | Timeline A — Linear AI | Timeline B — Exponential Good | Timeline C — Exponential Bad |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capability curve | Compounding but recognisable improvement | Rapid acceleration | Rapid acceleration |
| Institutional response | Patchwork adaptation | Partial social compact | Institutional failure |
| Human economic role | Humans remain economically useful but repositioned | Paid work recedes for many; contribution is rebuilt | Humans become economically marginal in many domains |
| Access model | Commercial and local-institutional inequality | Public intelligence commons plus regulated private systems | Concentrated capability; dependency tiers and informal workarounds |
| Trust model | Managed mistrust; credentials weakened but not dead | Stronger provenance and civic verification systems | Synthetic persuasion, scams and institutional unreliability |
| Work model | Fragmented platform work, local trust work, repair, supervision, taste, physical presence | Social dividend, civic contribution, project guilds, care, science and local governance | Labour collapse for many, access brokerage, informal economies, protection networks |
| Status system | Reputation for taste, reliability, human presence and local usefulness | Recognition for contribution, care, excellence, civic trust and chosen difficulty | Access status, protection, affiliation, human authenticity markets and survival reputation |
| School pressure | School lags but still gives routine and credential scaffolding | School lags more visibly as society rebuilds outside it | School becomes a brittle holding pen while the world stops believing its signals |
| Flourishing pressure | Agency depends on navigating systems and preserving local value | Meaning and recognition become difficult when survival no longer requires work | Dignity, safety and belonging fracture when usefulness is no longer economically rewarded |

The matrix deliberately avoids a fourth “AI stalls” world because Stage 4’s current book task is not to exhaust all futures. It is to make three strong contrasting worlds from the user’s requested linear / exponential-good / exponential-bad structure.

## 5. Timeline A world bible — Linear AI: The Patchwork Work World

### Core world idea

AI has not ended work. It has made work stranger.

By 2045, everyday life is full of agents, translation layers, adaptive interfaces, generated media, automated admin, tool-using software, cheap legal/accounting support, better diagnostics, semi-robotic logistics and predictive services. But deployment is uneven. Robotics improves physical work but does not erase bodies, buildings, weather, tools, liability or local trust. Many institutions adopt AI cautiously because mistakes are expensive and blame still needs a human address.

The result is not a post-work society. It is a patchwork work society. People still need income. They still argue about rent. Parents still worry about credentials. Teenagers still care who laughed at them in class. But many jobs are thinner, more mediated and more reputational. The valuable person is often not the one who can produce the output; the machine can produce outputs. The valuable person is the one trusted to choose, repair, perform, host, persuade, notice, show up, take responsibility or make something feel worth paying for.

This world should feel noticeably more transformed than today without becoming exponential. The reader should feel that AI has seeped into everything, like electricity with opinions.

### Technology condition card

- Foundation models are strong, cheap and embedded but still unreliable in high-stakes edge cases.
- Agents can handle bounded workflows, admin, consumer tasks, local business operations and much software work.
- Generative software has made small businesses more capable, but integration, liability, taste and maintenance still matter.
- Robotics is common in warehouses, kitchens, farms, hospitals and maintenance fleets, but human hands remain valuable in messy local environments.
- AI-for-science improves medicine, materials and logistics incrementally; validation and regulation remain slow.
- Compute is abundant enough for most commercial use but stratified by quality, privacy and institutional support.
- Trust systems exist but are annoying: signatures, provenance marks, model-use declarations, audit trails.

### Economy and work

Work persists, but many jobs become bundles of local responsibility and AI-mediated coordination. “A job” is less often a stable role and more often a reputation attached to a person who can operate within a mesh of systems.

Examples of valued work:

- human repair and installation in homes full of semi-smart devices;
- local event production where the difference is atmosphere, trust and taste;
- care and supervision where families want a known human;
- small manufacturing and craft with AI-generated design but human finishing;
- school-adjacent tutoring, coaching and youth support;
- compliance roles where someone must be accountable when an automated system acts.

Who flourishes: people with social reliability, practical judgement, taste, confidence using tools, local reputation and the ability to move between physical and digital contexts.

Who does not: people whose work was mostly routine output, people without trusted networks, people unable to afford good tools, people trapped in platform ratings and people whose school credentials no longer signal enough.

### Protagonist dossier

**Name:** Owen Price  
**Birth year:** 2015  
**Location:** Stoke-on-Trent and later the wider Midlands.  
**Age points:** 14 in 2029, 22 in 2037, 30 in 2045.

**Family:** Owen lives with his mum, Kelly, who works reception and scheduling for a dental group that keeps adding AI admin tools but never reduces the number of angry patients. His dad, Mark, is a self-employed appliance and heat-pump repairer who trusts machines only after he has heard the noise they make when they are failing. Owen has a younger sister, Evie, who is sharper at school than he is and knows it.

**Friends:** His best friend is Jamal, funny and quick with edits, who wants internet fame but hates being seen trying. Owen also knows Liv, who plays drums and is better than him at hiding disappointment. Their group spends time around a music rehearsal room attached to a community centre that survives on grants, favours and someone’s uncle fixing the boiler.

**Hobbies and texture:** Owen loves old electric guitars, five-a-side football, battered bikes, cheap noodles, wiring things badly and then fixing them. He dislikes polished school presentations and people who say “just use AI” when they do not understand what they are asking for.

**Private contradiction:** He wants recognition but mistrusts anything that looks like showing off. He mocks “creator kids” while secretly wanting strangers to like something he made.

**Relationship to technology:** Practical and suspicious. He uses AI constantly but treats it like a clever mate who lies under pressure.

**What he is good at:** Hearing when something is off; making an event feel alive; getting adults to trust him because he turns up; turning AI-generated plans into things that survive contact with rooms, cables, rain and people.

**What he misunderstands:** He thinks school has failed him because it does not reward what he is good at. Later he realises school gave him some of the routine, social exposure and adult witnesses that let him be trusted.

### Personal story engine

Owen wants to make something real enough that people remember being there. At 14 this means a school performance that goes wrong. At 22 it means keeping a collapsing local venue alive under AI-generated competition. At 30 it means deciding whether to sell his reputation into a national experience platform or stay local and smaller.

### Chapter A1 — 2029: Secondary school in the assistant economy

Owen is not worried about “AI.” He is worried about whether Liv heard him mess up a chord in music, whether his English homework looks too clean, whether his dad will make enough this month, and why Jamal’s generated videos get more attention than anything they actually play.

World details should emerge through:

- teachers trying to spot AI homework with rules nobody believes;
- parents still treating grades as the safest ladder;
- richer pupils using subscription tutors that produce better revision plans;
- Owen’s dad using cheap diagnostic agents that help but also send customers to corporate repair subscriptions;
- a school showcase where generated backing tracks make everyone sound competent until the power flickers and the human musicians matter again.

School friction should be subtle: school rewards neat written output, but Owen’s valuable skill is making messy real things work with people watching.

### Chapter A2 — 2037: Young adulthood in managed automation

Owen is 22. He has no clean career. He repairs event equipment, runs local shows, manages AI booking agents for older venue owners and occasionally installs home systems with his dad. His school friends have scattered: Jamal has a following but not much money; Liv has joined a touring human-only music collective that sells “no generated audio” nights.

Work is everywhere and nowhere. Everyone is busy. Nobody feels secure.

Scenes should include:

- Owen negotiating with an AI procurement agent that keeps downgrading human sound checks as unnecessary cost;
- a friend with a degree working under a platform manager that scores “human warmth” from customer recordings;
- a local event where people pay extra because they believe Owen’s crew actually played;
- his mum’s dental group replacing reception staff but asking her back as “patient confidence lead” after complaints rise;
- Owen realising his reputation is worth more than his qualifications but also more fragile.

### Chapter A3 — 2045: Adult life in uneven intelligence infrastructure

Owen is 30. AI is ordinary infrastructure. Some streets have municipal agents that coordinate maintenance, energy use and appointments. Other places live with broken systems and cheap models that misread accents, housing conditions and informal arrangements.

Owen runs a small venue-repair-performance cooperative. The cooperative survives because people trust it to make things happen in the physical world. A national platform offers to buy them out and make Owen the face of “authentic local experience.”

Scenes should include:

- a local blackout or model outage where old habits and human networks matter;
- Owen’s sister Evie, now professionally successful, urging him to scale up;
- Jamal’s online fame becoming worthless after synthetic nostalgia floods the market;
- Liv returning with a child and asking whether the venue can host something not optimised for audience capture;
- Owen choosing between money, autonomy, local belonging and the fear of becoming small.

The ending should not preach. It should leave Owen inside a real trade-off: work has not disappeared, but it has become a fight over whose systems define value.

## 6. Timeline B world bible — Exponential Good: The Intelligence Dividend

### Core world idea

AI accelerates sharply, but the world does not simply fall apart. Enough institutions respond seriously enough that the gains become partly public.

This is not utopia. It is a world where survival is less tied to paid employment for many people, medicine and local services improve, civic intelligence exists, public models are strong, and some scientific progress arrives faster than expected. But humans do not stop being humans. They still compare themselves. They still want to matter. They still form cliques. They still resent people who get more recognition. They still need love, dignity, challenge, status and a reason to get up.

The central pressure is not starvation. It is mattering.

### Technology condition card

- Powerful AI arrives early enough to reorganise institutions by the mid-2030s.
- Public intelligence infrastructure is created after political struggle: civic models, health agents, legal support, local planning systems and open education support.
- AI-for-science has real gains in diagnostics, drug discovery, materials, energy management and public health, but validation remains a bottleneck.
- Robotics improves logistics, construction, eldercare support and agriculture, but human presence remains valued in care, ritual, sport, art, local politics and chosen difficulty.
- Compute is treated partly as public infrastructure, though private systems remain stronger in some domains.
- Provenance and audit systems are built into institutions early enough to preserve some trust.
- Social dividend or guaranteed income structures exist by the late 2030s, not as magic abundance but as a political settlement.

### Economy and work

Paid work no longer carries the same universal adult meaning. Some people still work for money, especially in scarce physical roles, elite research, governance, care, embodied craft and high-status culture. Many others live from dividend, public provision, cooperative contribution systems or mixed local projects.

The economy has not abolished scarcity. Housing, attention, status, beautiful places, embodied care, political voice, romantic love, excellence and trusted belonging remain scarce.

Who flourishes: people who can choose hard projects, care for others, accept non-market meaning, live inside community, tolerate comparison and find status without needing economic indispensability.

Who does not: people whose identity depended on being needed, people without recognised contribution routes, people excluded from high-trust civic systems, people whose communities treat dividend life as shameful or childish.

### Protagonist dossier

**Name:** Noor Rahman  
**Birth year:** 2015  
**Location:** East London at 14, then a coastal civic-renewal town in Essex by adulthood.  
**Age points:** 14 in 2029, 22 in 2037, 30 in 2045.

**Family:** Noor lives with her mother, Farah, a hospital pharmacist, her father, Imran, who once ran a small travel business and now does community mediation, and her little brother Sami, whose rare autoimmune condition shapes the family’s routines. Her grandmother lives nearby and believes usefulness is shown by feeding people.

**Friends:** Her closest friend is Priya, competitive, funny, and good at making teachers like her. Noor also knows Theo, a quiet boy who draws buildings and later becomes important because he takes civic design seriously when everyone else treats it as admin.

**Hobbies and texture:** Noor likes swimming, spicy crisps, medical dramas, making playlists for bus rides, and arranging other people’s birthdays. She dislikes being called “mature,” because adults usually say it before asking her to cope with something.

**Private contradiction:** She wants relief from responsibility but feels hurt when she is not needed.

**Relationship to technology:** Trusting when it helps Sami, sceptical when it flatters people, impatient with adults who either worship AI or ban it without understanding it.

**What she is good at:** Noticing who has been left out; organising people; making a room feel less frightening.

**What she misunderstands:** She thinks a better society will remove the ache of comparison. It does not.

### Personal story engine

Noor grows up partly defined by her brother’s illness. As AI-enabled medicine and public systems improve, the family’s emergency atmosphere lifts. Noor gets what she thought she wanted: fewer crises, less necessity, more freedom. The story is about what happens after relief — when the world no longer forces her to be useful in the same way, and she has to choose what kind of contribution is actually hers.

### Chapter B1 — 2029: Secondary school during the acceleration compact

Noor is 14. Adults are tense in ways they do not explain. The school still runs on lessons, homework, behaviour points, assemblies and exams. Outside school, the NHS has new triage systems, public debate about AI rights and safety is constant, and parents talk about “the compact” as if teenagers should know what it means.

Scenes should include:

- Noor using a public health assistant at 2 a.m. during one of Sami’s flare-ups;
- school banning private AI tutors on paper while richer pupils use them anyway;
- Farah attending hospital retraining sessions because pharmacy work is changing;
- Priya caring more about predicted grades than the news;
- Noor feeling guilty because the new system helps Sami and scares adults around her.

School friction: school still rewards individual output and exam discipline, while Noor’s world is beginning to reward navigation of systems, care, trust and collective decision-making.

### Chapter B2 — 2037: Young adulthood in the intelligence commons

Noor is 22. The intelligence commons is real but uneven. Everyone has access to strong public models for health, law, learning and local administration. A social dividend covers basics. Many of Noor’s peers delay or reject traditional careers. Some call this freedom; others call it being made decorative.

Sami is healthier than anyone expected. That changes the family. Noor has been quietly organised around his fragility; now the centre of gravity shifts.

Scenes should include:

- Noor attending a contribution placement where young adults choose civic projects rather than jobs;
- Priya joining a high-status private research guild and pretending not to care that it is still elite;
- a public assembly where AI summaries are excellent but people still fight over whose lived experience counts;
- Sami taking risks Noor cannot bear because he is no longer the sick child;
- Noor choosing a coastal renewal project because she wants to be useful somewhere nobody already knows her role.

### Chapter B3 — 2045: Adult life after abundance arrives unevenly but constructively

Noor is 30. The world is materially better in many ways. Healthcare is faster. Energy systems are cleaner in many regions. People live with strong public support. Some dangerous work has vanished. A great deal of administration is handled by civic intelligence.

But adult life has not become simple. Recognition has become more intimate and more political. If everyone can be supported, what counts as a life well lived? If almost anyone can create beautiful things with help, what makes a gift feel like yours? If public systems can model need, who gets to refuse being modelled?

Noor works in a civic house by the sea. It is part clinic, part assembly room, part youth centre, part kitchen, part argument. She is not economically necessary in the old sense. She is necessary because people trust her to hold the room when the model cannot.

Scenes should include:

- Noor helping a retired delivery driver who feels humiliated by dividend life even though he is materially safer;
- Priya visiting with access to private systems Noor’s town does not have;
- Sami announcing he wants to join an off-world infrastructure project or high-risk research habitat, forcing Noor to release him again;
- a community ritual where people bring evidence of contribution that is not measured by income;
- Noor realising she does not miss crisis, but she does miss being unquestionably needed.

The ending should be hopeful but not smooth: the good world still asks humans to invent dignity after work.

## 7. Timeline C world bible — Exponential Bad: The Surplus World

### Core world idea

AI accelerates sharply, but institutions fail to build a dignified settlement. Capability exists. Abundance exists for some purposes and some people. But access is concentrated, governance lags, work collapses unevenly, synthetic persuasion corrodes trust, and many humans become economically marginal before society has a convincing answer to the question: what are people for when they are no longer needed by the economy?

This world must not be cartoon dystopia. Shops still open. People still flirt, joke, cook, dance, gossip, watch sport, help siblings and care what others think. The horror is not that everything is dark. The horror is that life goes on after society quietly reprices many people toward zero.

### Technology condition card

- Agentic systems become powerful quickly and spread through markets, administration, media, finance, software and persuasion.
- AI systems can perform or coordinate most digital work and much organisational work better and cheaper than humans.
- Robotics improves but remains concentrated where capital can deploy it: logistics, security, warehouses, eldercare chains, ports, agriculture, construction modules.
- AI-for-science produces valuable advances, but benefits flow through private access, geopolitical competition and insurance-like rationing.
- Compute and high-quality models are concentrated among states, frontier firms and protected enclaves.
- Synthetic media and personalised persuasion make reputation unstable.
- Governance is reactive, fragmented and often captured.
- Public institutions still exist but are overwhelmed, automated badly or distrusted.

### Economy and work

Formal employment disappears for large groups faster than social settlement appears. Some people receive minimal support credits tied to behaviour, location, data-sharing or compliance. Others attach themselves to firms, local bosses, religious groups, criminal networks, mutual aid circles or political movements.

Human work does not vanish completely. It moves into:

- high-control access management;
- embodied care for those who can pay or who distrust machines;
- human-authenticity markets;
- local protection and favour networks;
- black-market repair, identity laundering, tutoring, escorting through systems;
- dangerous physical work where robots are too expensive or liability is disposable.

Who flourishes: owners of compute, protected technical elites, people inside trusted enclaves, people with strong kin networks, people who can broker access, people whose embodied presence is scarce to the wealthy.

Who does not: people without access, people whose skills were legible only through credentials, young people entering a collapsed labour ladder, families dependent on behaviour-scored support, people misclassified by automated systems and people without trusted witnesses.

### Protagonist dossier

**Name:** Tia Morgan  
**Birth year:** 2015  
**Location:** Port Talbot and later the South Wales corridor.  
**Age points:** 14 in 2029, 22 in 2037, 30 in 2045.

**Family:** Tia lives with her mum, Rhiannon, who works shifts in a care home chain increasingly run by remote systems, and her younger brother, Ellis, who is bright, impulsive and good at games in a way adults keep calling “promising.” Her father, Dean, once worked in logistics coordination and now does irregular cash work after his role was automated and then his retraining pathway disappeared into appeals.

**Friends:** Her best friend is Seren, who is glamorous in a cheap way and always knows which apps are paying for attention this week. Tia also knows Mal, older by two years, who can get people documents, device repairs and access tokens, though never for free.

**Hobbies and texture:** Tia boxes at a community gym, draws tattoos in the margins of school books, loves chips with too much vinegar, hates being pitied and likes clothes that look more expensive than they are.

**Private contradiction:** She distrusts systems but wants desperately to be recognised by one that matters.

**Relationship to technology:** Opportunistic and wary. She uses anything that helps her family but assumes every system is trying to trap or score her.

**What she is good at:** Reading danger in people; staying calm in humiliating situations; protecting Ellis; making quick social calculations.

**What she misunderstands:** She thinks the right proof will make institutions treat her fairly. Later she learns that proof only matters inside systems that still care to receive it.

### Personal story engine

Tia wants Ellis to have a way out. At 14 that means keeping him in school and away from scams. At 22 it means stopping him from signing a predatory access contract with a private AI-security firm. At 30 it means deciding whether to trade her own freedom for his place inside a protected zone.

### Chapter C1 — 2029: Secondary school during the agentic shock

Tia is 14. School looks normal but feels less believed. Teachers still talk about coursework, attendance and revision. Students know half the work is generated, half the punishments are random, and adults are scared of something they cannot name clearly.

Scenes should include:

- Dean losing an appeal because an employment system says he declined training he never saw;
- Tia using AI to write homework badly on purpose so it looks human;
- Seren earning money from synthetic-personality streams and hiding who is really watching;
- Ellis being targeted by game-like recruitment prompts for “youth optimisation” schemes;
- a teacher trying hard to keep the classroom human while the school’s behaviour system flags the wrong pupils.

School friction: school still pretends effort leads to credentials and credentials lead to work. Tia’s family is already living in a world where that ladder is cracking.

### Chapter C2 — 2037: Young adulthood in the trust fracture

Tia is 22. Formal work exists mostly as rumour, elite credential, family inheritance or surveillance-heavy contract. People survive through credits, local networks, scams, micro-contracts, care shifts, access brokerage and favours.

Ellis is 18 and talented with systems. A private security-intelligence contractor offers him a residential pathway: food, housing, models, training and status in exchange for data rights, behavioural control and long service terms. Rhiannon sees safety. Tia sees ownership.

Scenes should include:

- Tia queueing at a municipal office where an automated eligibility system misreads her household;
- Mal offering a way to alter Ellis’s risk score;
- Seren thriving in synthetic intimacy markets while becoming less sure who actually likes her;
- Tia doing a human-only care shift for an old man whose family pays because they do not trust robot carers;
- Ellis accusing Tia of wanting him trapped with her because she cannot imagine another life.

### Chapter C3 — 2045: Adult life in runaway asymmetry

Tia is 30. Intelligence abundance exists, but not for everyone as freedom. Protected zones have extraordinary medicine, security, education and personal AI. Outside, people live with cheap agents, rationed compute, behavioural credits, decaying public systems and constant persuasion.

Work has not exactly disappeared; it has lost its dignity as a general route to adulthood. People do tasks, favours, performances, risk trades and human-authenticity labour. But the economy no longer needs most of them in the old way.

Scenes should include:

- Tia entering a protected zone to visit Ellis and finding him healthier, calmer and less reachable;
- Rhiannon’s care chain collapsing after a model scandal and leaving residents dependent on families;
- Seren vanishing from feeds after an identity-clone dispute;
- Mal becoming a local gatekeeper whose help feels like both protection and capture;
- Tia being offered a compliance role that would give Ellis permanent protection if she becomes an informant against her own neighbourhood.

The ending should not become apocalypse. It should be worse in a more human way: Tia must decide what kind of loyalty can survive when the formal world has stopped offering dignity.

## 9. Human-perspective discipline for the final rewrite

The next `STAGE_BOOK.md` must not contain analytic omniscient lines that explain the project’s concepts from above. The narrator may know more than the character, but must not break the spell by translating the character’s life into research categories.

### Banned movement

Do not write:

- “Owen does not think in terms of agency or verification.”
- “In this world, trust has become expensive.”
- “Noor’s society has solved material scarcity but not recognition.”
- “Tia lives in a condition of mediated agency.”

### Required movement

Write the life instead:

- Owen notices that the venue’s AI contract marks his sound check as optional until the room fills with feedback and everyone looks at him.
- Noor watches her brother sprint into the sea for the first time without anyone checking his pulse monitor.
- Tia stands outside a protected-zone clinic with shoes that hurt because she wanted to look like someone who belonged.

The reader should infer the Stage 4 pressure. The character should not name it.
