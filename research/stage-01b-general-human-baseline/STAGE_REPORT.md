# Stage 1B – General Human Baseline Report

**Evidence base:** 185 accepted, deduplicated source records across eight workstreams
**Date:** 21 June 2026

## Executive Summary

For integration, this report adopts a provisional analytical frame: the reviewed literatures show recurring patterns concerning bodily regulation, directed action under limited information and control, affiliation and boundaries, differential esteem and influence, joint action, and meaningful commitment. Treating these as six problem domains is a **Working Assumption**, not an empirical discovery or exhaustive map. It is moderate in confidence, open on transportability, and revisable if broader sampling, construct non-equivalence or finer-grained evidence shows that the grouping obscures distinct mechanisms.

Human action is therefore neither treated as the output of an isolated will nor reduced to biology. Bodily regulation is associated with changes in salience and readiness; attention samples only part of the world; habits connect recurring cues to action; goals and plans can alter those connections under specified conditions. The report treats agency as action exercised through available means, learned expectations and relationships, while keeping the integrated causal structure open. [Sources: S1B-W1-SRC-001–S1B-W1-SRC-012; S1B-W2-SRC-001–S1B-W2-SRC-004; S1B-W2-SRC-018–S1B-W2-SRC-024]

Affiliation appears repeatedly in the sampled evidence without implying inevitable hostility. Care, belonging and cooperation occur through overlapping kin and non-kin relationships. Categories may make expectations and obligations legible, but categorisation alone does not dictate favouritism or aggression. The significance and permeability of boundaries are associated with norms, histories, resources and institutions. [Sources: S1B-W1-SRC-013–S1B-W1-SRC-018; S1B-W4-SRC-001–S1B-W4-SRC-006; S1B-W4-SRC-008–S1B-W4-SRC-016; S1B-W4-SRC-020–S1B-W4-SRC-024]

Status is not one ladder. Prestige, dominance, formal authority, inherited position and control of resources are distinct routes to influence. Institutions help make some performances visible; reputation can convert visibility into deference; deference can increase access to attention and resources; those advantages may then be mistaken for merit. Yet rank is also resisted through refusal, ridicule, mobility, sharing rules and coalitions. Recurring rank allocation does not prove an innate hierarchy drive or make an existing order necessary or legitimate. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-001–S1B-W3-SRC-014; S1B-W3-SRC-016–S1B-W3-SRC-024]

Observed cooperation varies with expectations, reciprocity, monitoring, group boundaries and rules rather than appearing as kindness enlarged. These conditions are associated with both sustained contribution and exclusion, retaliation or extraction. Punishment has conditional collective effects: it can deter some free-riding, but it can also target contributors, invite revenge and consume collective gains. Whether punishment is just is a separate normative question. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-001–S1B-W5-SRC-024]

Meaning-related judgements and moral commitments are associated in the reviewed evidence with duties, identities, protected values and relationships that resist ordinary exchange. Selected ritual studies report public commitment and coordinated action; contribution is associated in some studies with mattering and connection. Ritual may also accompany rank or conflict, and duty may be coerced. These distinct evidence bases do not establish one commitment mechanism or moral worth. [Sources: S1B-W6-SRC-001–S1B-W6-SRC-011; S1B-W6-SRC-014–S1B-W6-SRC-024]

Culture and institutions are not treated here as a thin surface laid over fixed nature. They are associated with variation in triggers, categories, status currencies and obligations, and they affect what actions and differences become visible to researchers. Cross-cultural variation is structured rather than residual noise. Imported measures may also shape which differences become visible or comparable. Similar observed forms need not carry the same meaning or reflect the same mechanism. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-001–S1B-W8-SRC-015; S1B-W8-SRC-017, S1B-W8-SRC-018, S1B-W8-SRC-020–S1B-W8-SRC-024]

The resulting baseline is deliberately provisional: the sampled evidence portrays humans as embodied, bounded and relational, with recurrent patterns involving regulation, action, affiliation, rank, cooperation and commitment. Each pattern has more than one possible direction. Care can accompany control; affiliation can accompany exclusion; prestige can convert into domination; cooperation can conceal extraction; meaning can be used manipulatively. Stage 1B does not settle origins or proper forms, and it passes candidate material only to Stage 1R.

## Prologue

Accounts of human nature often begin by naming what is supposedly inside the person: fear, self-interest, attachment, curiosity, status-seeking, fairness, purpose. The list feels explanatory because the words are familiar. It can also conceal the hardest question. When a behaviour appears repeatedly, what exactly has recurred?

Take hierarchy. Differential influence appears in many settings. But prestige gained through valued knowledge is not the same mechanism as dominance secured through threat, authority granted by office, inherited position, or control of material resources. Calling all of them a hierarchy drive compresses distinct routes to power into one presumed cause. It also overlooks resistance: coalitions, ridicule, refusal, mobility and sharing obligations can prevent would-be dominators from converting standing into command. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-001–S1B-W3-SRC-014]

Or take belonging. Human lives are densely relational, but the units of relation are not given in advance. Genealogy matters, yet lived kinship can be constituted through feeding, co-residence and recognised obligation. Networks in the sampled populations often extend beyond close kin. Group labels are associated with trust and cooperation under some conditions, but they do not contain a fixed programme of hostility. [Sources: S1B-W4-SRC-005, S1B-W4-SRC-013; S1B-W4-SRC-020–S1B-W4-SRC-024]

As a provisional integration device, the recurrent object may be framed as a pattern concerning care, scarce attention, trust, contribution, influence, commitment or resistance rather than as a discrete motive. This is not evidence that each pattern has one function or solution. Bodies constrain action; developmental histories, ecologies and institutions are associated with differences in visible form and consequence. Pattern is not destiny.

This report follows that distinction from the body outward. It begins with regulation because no action occurs outside material limits. It then examines agency under scarcity, relationships and boundaries, rank and power, cooperation and enforcement, and meaning and moral commitment. The final chapters turn the lens back on the evidence itself. The result is not a total portrait of the species. It is a disciplined baseline: a map of recurrent problems, variable forms, double-edged mechanisms and unresolved origins.

## Chapter 1 — Regulation Can Be Anticipatory

Some regulation can precede an observed demand rather than occurring only after deviation. Allostatic accounts propose anticipatory adjustment, while comparative models relate adjustment to energy budgets, life history and ecological pressure. These are theoretical frameworks with partly overlapping evidence, not a settled universal architecture. The reactive-scope model provides a boundary by distinguishing proposed predictive and reactive ranges from overload and by allowing thresholds to vary across mediators, histories and environments. [Sources: S1B-W1-SRC-001–S1B-W1-SRC-004]

This changes the meaning of stress. Stress mediators are not simply poisons. Short-term mobilisation can be protective; repeated or prolonged activation may carry costs. Nor are biomarkers transparent narrators of experience. Physiological activity cannot by itself disclose what happened, what it meant, or what will follow. Social evaluation can recruit bodily stress systems even in the absence of immediate physical danger, showing how institutional meaning can become biologically consequential without becoming biologically predetermined. [Sources: S1B-W1-SRC-004–S1B-W1-SRC-006]

Threat and reward also fragment under scrutiny. Defensive action changes with imminence and controllability, but defensive circuitry, behaviour and conscious fear are not identical. Reward involves partially separable processes: wanting, liking and learning can diverge, while dopamine prediction error concerns updating rather than serving as a meter of pleasure. Measurement studies caution that tasks bearing these labels do not always isolate the constructs they claim to test. [Sources: S1B-W1-SRC-007–S1B-W1-SRC-012]

The bounded inference is that bodily states may change readiness and salience without issuing simple behavioural commands. Arousal is associated with changes in attention, and repeated exposure may alter readiness; neither observation establishes a universal sequence preceding deliberation. Physiology can respond to social-evaluative conditions, but it cannot explain or justify the rank order involved.

Care provides evidence of social dependence. Attachment-relevant patterns appear in the sampled cross-cultural evidence, but classifications vary within and across societies and depend partly on a Western-designed procedure. Reviews and ethnography describe protection and responsive care across different bodily practices and caregiving networks. Evidence from Efe and Aka communities documents multiple caregivers and ecological variation; comparative cooperative-care accounts remain plausible historical hypotheses, not proof of one original or natural family arrangement. [Sources: S1B-W1-SRC-013–S1B-W1-SRC-018]

Affect follows the same function–form distinction. Facial movement is contextual and variable rather than a universal one-to-one code for inner states. Languages divide emotional experience differently, and cultural goals shape valued feelings, eliciting situations and display rules. This does not make affect unreal or unconstrained. It means embodied processes become socially legible through categories and relationships that vary. [Sources: S1B-W1-SRC-019–S1B-W1-SRC-024]

The reviewed evidence therefore supports a narrower finding: regulation includes anticipatory and reactive patterns, while defence, reward, care and affect are non-unitary and context-sensitive. It does not establish a finite drive inventory, universal triggers, invariant feelings, one social form or a general claim that bodily priorities are always anticipatory.

## Chapter 2 — Agency Is the Management of Limited Control

The evidence distinguishes beliefs about means and ends, beliefs about executing those means, and whether relevant opportunities are available. Confidence, persistence and passivity are therefore ambiguous observations rather than direct measures of one inner quantity. Treating agency as a relation among these components is a moderate-confidence Working Assumption for integration, not a directly tested general model. [Sources: S1B-W2-SRC-001–S1B-W2-SRC-004]

Under uncertainty, attention and computation are limited. People sample, frame, recognise cues and stop searching. Satisficing and ecological-rationality research proposes that ignoring some information can increase robustness in specified environments. Strategy success does not transport automatically: framing effects, inattentional blindness and differences between described and experienced risk show that bounded strategies can also fail. [Sources: S1B-W7-SRC-001–S1B-W7-SRC-007]

Automaticity is part of this system, not its opposite. Repeated behaviour in stable contexts can become cue-responsive and less dependent on active deliberation. Habits can preserve valued action or perpetuate conduct after its value has disappeared. Deliberate if-then plans work partly by recruiting cue sensitivity, while goal effects depend on commitment, identifiable opportunities and executable responses. Narrow or excessive goals can produce tunnel vision, risk-taking and distorted attention. More intention is not always more control. [Sources: S1B-W2-SRC-018–S1B-W2-SRC-024]

Exploration is selective for the same reason. Curiosity allocates attention toward uncertainty, expected information value and perceived knowledge gaps; it is not an indiscriminate hunger for novelty. Play can create spaces for flexibility and negotiation, yet comparative evidence does not establish one function, universal benefit or necessary developmental route. Exploration always has opportunity costs, and unequal power helps shape who can bear failure. [Sources: S1B-W2-SRC-013–S1B-W2-SRC-017]

Social learning extends the management of scarcity across minds. Copying can reduce discovery costs, but indiscriminate copying preserves error. People use cues such as uncertainty, success and frequency, with substantial variation by livelihood, experience and setting. Automatic imitation need not reveal a dedicated inherited module; learned association and attention remain viable explanations. [Sources: S1B-W7-SRC-012–S1B-W7-SRC-017]

Autonomy is especially vulnerable to cultural overreach. The reviewed evidence distinguishes interest, endorsed value and controlled action, and repeatedly associates competence and autonomy-related experience with more self-endorsed conduct. Yet autonomy need not mean independence, rejection of authority or maximal choice. It can coexist with obligation and interdependence. Personal preference predicts choice differently across sampled settings, and the underlying constructs were largely developed within Western traditions. [Sources: S1B-W2-SRC-005–S1B-W2-SRC-012]

Institutional and material conditions may affect which options and cues are visible, whose testimony is treated as credible and who bears the cost of experimentation. This can alter observed action without erasing the actor. The report therefore uses agency as an analytical relation among action, revision and constraint, while leaving its integrated causal structure open.

## Chapter 3 — Relationship Comes Before the Bounded Group

An isolated agent is an analytical convenience, not a plausible human baseline. Dependence on care begins the story; affiliation extends it. Across the reviewed evidence, people form overlapping relationships of care, exchange, identity and obligation. What does not follow is one natural social unit.

Belonging is partly subjective and relational. People can identify with groups while differentiating themselves within them, and distinct identities may become salient in different settings. Measures often compress acceptance, attachment, identity and safety into a single construct, while much of the literature rests on Western-developed self-report. The safe conclusion is recurrent affiliation, not one universal experience or intensity of belonging. [Sources: S1B-W4-SRC-001–S1B-W4-SRC-004]

Categories are associated with differences in expectations and allocation in some sampled settings. Minimal-group experiments show that arbitrary labels can sometimes alter allocation, but effects vary with norms, identification, interdependence and task meaning. Ingroup preference is not the same as outgroup hostility. The mere existence of a category does not contain a behavioural programme. [Sources: S1B-W4-SRC-005, S1B-W4-SRC-006; S1B-W4-SRC-008, S1B-W4-SRC-009; S1B-W4-SRC-013]

This is why “tribalism” explains too much too quickly. Contact is generally associated with lower prejudice, although causal evidence is narrower and effects are conditional. Cross-cutting identities can reduce some biases but can also leave dominant boundaries intact. National favouritism appears across numerous sampled countries, yet it varies in strength, and the nation is itself a historically organised institution rather than a primordial unit. [Sources: S1B-W4-SRC-010–S1B-W4-SRC-012; S1B-W4-SRC-014–S1B-W4-SRC-016]

Kinship reveals the deeper mechanism. Genealogy has material consequences, but lived relatedness is also made through recognition and practice. Forager network studies show ties extending beyond close genetic kin. Ethnographic work describes feeding and living together as constitutive of relatedness, while other field evidence finds that genealogical and culturally recognised kinship both predict coordination. Contemporary small-scale communities are not fossils, and these studies do not identify an ancestral arrangement. They show that biology and culture are not competing total explanations: institutions make some relations legible, enforceable and meaningful. [Sources: S1B-W4-SRC-020–S1B-W4-SRC-024]

Boundaries are associated with patterns of care, testimony, expected contribution and institutional access. They also mark vulnerability. A boundary may protect mutual obligation or support exclusion, while its permeability varies with resources, alternatives, history and power. These findings are population- and measure-bounded; they do not establish a universal boundary-management function, inevitable hostility or a fixed coalition module.

This chapter adds a social field to the control loop. People do not merely select actions; they act among audiences, obligations and categories. But affiliation alone does not explain which voices become authoritative. For that, the baseline needs rank.

## Chapter 4 — Status Is a Political System of Attention

Status is often described as a universal ladder, as though every group placed everyone on one shared scale. The evidence supports something more plural and more political. Prestige, dominance, authority, inherited position, popularity and resource control are different routes to influence. They can reinforce one another, but they are not interchangeable. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-001–S1B-W3-SRC-008; S1B-W3-SRC-024]

In the reviewed theories and studies, prestige refers to comparatively freely conferred deference toward valued skill or knowledge, while dominance refers more directly to influence backed by threatened cost. Formal authority and resource control are further distinguishable bases of influence. These distinctions are analytically useful but not proven to exhaust rank systems or retain identical meaning across settings. Any claim about hierarchy must specify the currency, audience, population and measure.

One cross-domain hypothesis is a feedback loop: institutional settings may make some performances more visible; visibility can affect reputation; reputation may be associated with deference and access to further opportunities; accumulated advantage may then be read as merit. Evidence for parts of this sequence is stronger than evidence for the full causal loop, which remains a moderate Working Assumption. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-002, S1B-W3-SRC-007, S1B-W3-SRC-008; S1B-W3-SRC-020–S1B-W3-SRC-023]

Rank is associated with differences in attention, credibility and influence, but the evidence does not establish one causal sequence from rank to cognition or morality. Social-information use varies with uncertainty, livelihood, experience and institutional setting rather than following one universal prestige rule. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-001–S1B-W3-SRC-008; S1B-W7-SRC-012–S1B-W7-SRC-017]

Counterevidence prevents this account becoming hierarchy naturalisation. Ethnographic and comparative work describes levelling practices including ridicule, refusal, mobility, demand sharing and coalitions against domination. Such practices do not prove the absence of rank. They show that influence can be prevented from hardening into command. Nor should apparently egalitarian arrangements be romanticised: gendered, domestic or ritual inequalities may remain hidden from the most visible public hierarchy. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-009–S1B-W3-SRC-014]

Across the populations and records reviewed, differential attention, esteem and influence appear repeatedly, while rank's form, rigidity, domain and convertibility vary. Public leadership and readily documented reputation may overrepresent adult men and visible production while missing domestic, spiritual, informal and resistance-based influence. No evidence here establishes a universal desire for maximum rank, an inherited hierarchy architecture, or the necessity of inequality. Existing rank cannot serve as evidence of merit or legitimacy.

The political question is unavoidable: who benefits from the currency of standing, who is rendered invisible, who can move between ranks, and who can resist? Those questions connect status to collective action because whose expectations count and who may enforce them can affect observed cooperation.

## Chapter 5 — Cooperation Has Machinery, and Machinery Has Owners

Observed cooperation varies with heterogeneous motives, expectations, boundaries and rules rather than appearing as a stable personality trait multiplied across a group. Some participants contribute conditionally; visible participation is associated in experimental settings with further contribution, while anticipated defection can accompany withdrawal. Transport from short games to durable, unequal institutions remains uncertain. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-002, S1B-W5-SRC-004; S1B-W5-SRC-009–S1B-W5-SRC-013; S1B-W5-SRC-021]

Studies of durable commons associate outcomes with boundaries, participation, monitoring, graduated sanctions and conflict resolution, while broader frameworks relate outcomes to resources, users and governance. These are diagnostic patterns, not universal recipes or isolated causal effects. Case evidence cannot establish that local rule is always successful, and laboratory games cannot reproduce durable relationships, contested legitimacy or unequal power. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-001–S1B-W5-SRC-003; S1B-W5-SRC-015–S1B-W5-SRC-018]

Punishment exposes the moral ambiguity. Sanctions can raise contribution or deter free-riding. They can also target cooperators, invite retaliation, lack proportionality and consume the gains they protect. Punishment amplifies an enforced norm; it does not make the norm just. Nor does recurrent punishment demonstrate an innate punitive instinct. Cross-cultural experiments show recurrence and variation, while thin tasks and uncertain equivalence leave origin and real-world legitimacy unresolved. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-005, S1B-W5-SRC-007, S1B-W5-SRC-008; S1B-W5-SRC-013, S1B-W5-SRC-014; S1B-W5-SRC-019, S1B-W5-SRC-020]

The machinery therefore has a distribution. Who monitors? Who may sanction? Whose conduct is defined as defection? Who can appeal? An institution can coordinate mutual benefit or stabilise extraction. Unequal sanctioning power may protect a dominant norm and transfer burdens to people with fewer credible options. Cooperation must be analysed together with power, beneficiaries, casualties and resistance.

Competition does not sit neatly on the other side. Between-group competition can increase contribution within groups, but matched thresholds without a rival can generate similar effects, weakening a unique competition explanation. Ingroup cooperation may exceed outgroup cooperation under some conditions, yet favouring insiders is not identical to harming outsiders. Cooperation and competition can be nested: people may coordinate intensely to defeat, exclude or exploit others. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-022–S1B-W5-SRC-024]

Cross-cultural evidence rejects a uniform economic actor. Behaviour in games varies substantially across societies and is associated with features such as market integration and cooperative production, but these associations do not establish causation or historical origin. No population supplies the species template. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-004–S1B-W5-SRC-006]

The baseline that survives is conditional. Reciprocity, trust, monitoring, free-riding, enforcement and institutional coordination recur. Their combination does not have a fixed direction. The question is never only whether cooperation exists. It is cooperation among whom, toward what end, under whose rules, with which burdens, and with what possibility of refusal.

## Chapter 6 — Meaning and Commitment Are Related but Not Morally Unidirectional

Predominantly Western self-report research distinguishes related dimensions labelled coherence, direction or purpose, and mattering. Narrative and contribution are proposed sources, but the evidence is shaped by an individualist ideal of the authored life and does not establish a universal meaning taxonomy. Other settings may emphasise ordinary role fulfilment, recognised obligation or concepts not captured by these measures. [Sources: S1B-W6-SRC-001–S1B-W6-SRC-006; S1B-W6-SRC-024]

Some commitments resist ordinary exchange. Protected values can make material offers feel insulting because the offer changes the social meaning of the transaction. Conflict studies suggest that symbolic concessions may matter where material inducements fail, but selected populations and contested causal pathways prevent a universal model. A refusal to trade may defend dignity, preserve identity, signal loyalty or entrench conflict. [Sources: S1B-W6-SRC-021, S1B-W6-SRC-022]

Ritual participation is associated in selected field studies with support networks or ingroup giving, while experiments examine synchrony, cost and symbolism under narrower conditions. Selection, local meaning, gender, caste, role and constraint are difficult to separate from influence. The same practices may accompany rank, exclusion, fusion or costly refusal. Neither coordination nor moral direction can be inferred from ritual recurrence. [Sources: S1B-W6-SRC-007–S1B-W6-SRC-011]

Moral concern also recurs without one universal content or weighting. Cross-cultural experiments reject both uniform selfishness and a uniform fairness preference. Equality, proportionality, need, loyalty, authority and duty can compete. Imported moral taxonomies do not establish equivalent constructs everywhere, and behavioural games impose researcher-defined options. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-004; S1B-W6-SRC-014–S1B-W6-SRC-020]

Contribution brings the tension into focus. The same act may be experienced as mattering, reciprocal duty, reputational investment, coerced labour or extraction. Voluntariness, recognition, resources, dependence and the credible possibility of refusal alter its meaning. Associations between helping and reported meaning do not prove that contribution causes well-being, nor do they legitimise demands for sacrifice. [Sources: S1B-W6-SRC-001, S1B-W6-SRC-006; S1B-W6-SRC-023, S1B-W6-SRC-024]

Meaning is therefore neither treated as private decoration nor assumed to be virtuous. Meaning-related judgements, ritual, protected values and contribution belong to distinct evidence bases and should not be collapsed into one commitment mechanism. Power and dependence remain relevant to whose values become collective obligations, whose suffering is interpreted as sacrifice, and who may refuse, but the reviewed evidence does not establish a general causal model.

This chapter completes the outward movement from regulation to commitment. The reviewed literatures associate bodily state with readiness, bounded action with cues and opportunities, relationships with affiliation, rank with differential attention, institutions with coordination, and some meaning systems with non-substitutable commitments. These associations do not establish one causal sequence, and none is culturally neutral. The next chapter examines how culture, institutions and measurement affect what becomes visible.

## Chapter 7 — Culture, Institutions and Measurement Affect What Becomes Visible

The phrase “human factors” can imply a stable internal nature expressed through local decoration. The cross-cultural evidence does not support that inference. Psychological research remains concentrated in Western, affluent and formally schooled populations. Adding countries does not by itself provide linguistic, regional, class, gender or institutional balance: nations contain different languages, livelihoods, ecologies and histories, while cultural distance varies within as well as between borders. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-001–S1B-W8-SRC-007]

Variation is patterned. Economic-game behaviour differs across small-scale societies. Within-country studies report psychological differences associated with subsistence-linked ecologies. Historical kinship institutions, market integration and religious systems are associated with present measures. These studies cannot isolate cause: migration, state formation, selection, diffusion and measurement remain alternatives. Their contribution is to show that context is not random noise around a universal mean. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-004; S1B-W8-SRC-012–S1B-W8-SRC-015; S1B-W8-SRC-017]

Measurement affects what becomes visible. Translation does not guarantee construct equivalence. Configural, metric and scalar equivalence support different kinds of inference, and acceptable statistical fit cannot establish identical local meaning. Imported personality instruments have shown weak factor recovery and substantial method effects in low- and middle-income settings; a familiar five-factor structure did not emerge robustly among Tsimane adults. This does not show an absence of stable differences. It shows that one taxonomy and procedure cannot be presumed to reveal the same structure everywhere. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-006; S1B-W8-SRC-008–S1B-W8-SRC-012]

Developmental evidence adds another layer. Some early social-cognitive capacities appear across sampled settings, while timing and performance vary. Later prosocial and fairness patterns show both recurrence and divergence toward local adult forms. These studies are cross-sectional and task-bound, but they demonstrate that labels such as fairness and prosociality contain separable mechanisms. Early appearance does not establish innateness: biological preparedness, common caregiving problems, convergent learning and task affordances remain possible explanations. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-018, S1B-W8-SRC-020–S1B-W8-SRC-022; S1B-W6-SRC-015]

Comparative evidence raises species-level hypotheses without settling them. Human children have outperformed other apes on selected social-cognitive tasks, a pattern consistent with species-typical specialisation. Captive rearing, motivation, motor demands, task equivalence and culturally narrow human samples leave alternatives open. Comparative difference does not identify genetic specification or innate content. [Source: S1B-W8-SRC-023]

A transactional account is compatible with the evidence: observed capacities emerge amid relations among organism, caregivers, practices, ecology and institutions. This remains a Working Assumption rather than a uniquely established causal account. Current evidence often cannot partition biological preparedness, developmental calibration, cultural transmission and institutional feedback cleanly. [Source: S1B-W8-SRC-024]

Cultural and institutional conditions are associated with differences in attention, emotion terms, interpretations of authorship, recognised kin, status currencies, obligations and protected commitments. They also affect which options, consequences and differences become visible. The evidence does not establish that culture independently causes each difference, but it does rule out treating local form as a superficial costume on a fully specified universal mechanism.

## Chapter 8 — A Baseline Without an Essence

The cumulative synthesis can now be stated cautiously. Bodies regulate, but no biomarker narrates experience. Control-related action is associated with intentions, habits, cues and opportunities. Affiliation recurs in sampled populations, but no natural group unit follows. Rank has multiple observed currencies and can be resisted. Cooperation varies with institutional conditions, while enforcement has conditional effects. Meaning-related judgements and commitment practices are related without sharing one moral direction. Cultural and institutional conditions affect what becomes visible without making human action arbitrary.

This is not a compromise between biological and cultural explanations. It is a distinction among levels of claim. A recurring pattern may be compatible with multiple mechanisms, and a similar observed form may have different meanings across settings. Present recurrence may reflect biological preparedness, common developmental demands, diffusion, convergence, institutional reproduction, measurement artefact or combinations of these. Evidence at one level cannot silently answer questions at another.

The distinction matters most where naturalisation serves power. A physiological response to rank does not justify rank. Recurrent punishment does not establish justice. Widespread ritual does not prove religious necessity. Kin cooperation does not define one natural family. Cross-site inequality does not make inequality inevitable. Successful coordination does not reveal who paid its costs.

Nor should variation be romanticised. Historically contingent categories can have durable material effects. Institutions can narrow available action so thoroughly that an arrangement reproduces without universal endorsement. Culture is not a free choice made by isolated individuals, and resistance is not equally safe or available. A baseline must therefore hold agency and constraint together.

The report's **Working Assumption for integration** is a six-domain problem–solution architecture. It is analytical, non-exhaustive, moderate in present usefulness and open on transportability. It groups recurring patterns concerning the six domains without claiming one shared function or independent modules. Stage 1R should revise or reject it if broader sampling, construct non-equivalence or finer-grained evidence shows that the grouping obscures rather than clarifies mechanisms.

That is enough for a baseline. It is not enough for a total theory, an origin story or a moral settlement. Stage 1R must compare this general account with the parallel component evidence, preserving disagreements and checking whether apparent convergence is independent. Stage 1B supplies candidate findings and boundaries to that reconciliation, and nowhere else.

## Method and Evidence Boundary

Stage 1B used a deep rapid evidence review across eight workstreams. The final ledger contains 185 accepted, deduplicated source records: W1 24, W2 24, W3 23, W4 22, W5 24, W6 22, W7 24 and W8 22. The corpus combines reviews, meta-analyses, conceptual frameworks, laboratory and field studies, cross-cultural comparisons, ethnography, comparative research and institutional cases. Similar workstream counts are coverage controls only: they do not imply linguistic, regional, class, gender or institutional balance, and they were not treated as votes.

Each workstream included material intended to anchor cross-cultural or anthropological interpretation and material intended to limit stronger claims. Specialists produced bounded syntheses and candidate findings. Cross-disciplinary challenge then tested biological reduction, cultural determinism, status blindness, cooperation romanticism, moral functionalism and measurement overreach. This report integrates those inputs rather than reproducing them serially.

The method has material limits. It is not eight systematic reviews and cannot estimate prevalence or exhaust every literature. English-language publication systems remain overrepresented. Public, formal and readily documented activity may be more visible than domestic, informal or hidden activity; this creates particular uncertainty around gendered power and status. Laboratory tasks simplify histories, meanings and unequal power. Ethnography adds context but does not establish prevalence. Cross-sectional and observational associations cannot identify causation. Comparative studies face task-equivalence, rearing and motivation problems. Historical associations leave multiple causal pathways open. Neural, hormonal and behavioural measures do not transparently disclose subjective states.

Four evidential distinctions govern every conclusion:

1. **Recurrence** means that a pattern or problem appears in more than one reviewed setting. It is always bounded by sampling and measurement.
2. **Universality** requires broad cross-cultural coverage, construct equivalence and serious boundary testing. This report establishes no strong universal inventory.
3. **Developmental emergence** concerns when and how a capacity appears. Early appearance can be compatible with several biological and cultural pathways.
4. **Innateness** requires convergent developmental, cross-cultural and comparative evidence that excludes common learning, diffusion, convergent solutions and artefact. No major Stage 1B finding meets that threshold.

The report uses canonical claim classes. Empirical Findings describe evidence-bounded patterns. Working Assumptions make the integration possible but remain revisable. Normative Judgements are explicit project constraints on interpretation; they are not presented as discoveries. No Speculative Projections are made.

## Minimum General Human Baseline

**Claim class:** Working Assumption for integration
**Confidence:** Moderate
**Transportability and completeness:** Open

The provisional baseline groups the reviewed evidence into six recurring pattern domains. The grouping is non-exhaustive, does not imply independent modules, and is falsifiable by broader sampling, construct non-equivalence or evidence that it conceals materially different mechanisms:

1. **Regulation:** The reviewed evidence includes anticipatory and reactive regulation; defence, reward learning, care and affect are non-unitary and context-sensitive. [Sources: S1B-W1-SRC-001–S1B-W1-SRC-024]
2. **Directed action:** Humans allocate scarce attention and act through interacting beliefs, plans, cues, habits, feedback and opportunities. Agency cannot be reduced to either sovereign intention or automatic response. [Sources: S1B-W2-SRC-001–S1B-W2-SRC-024; S1B-W7-SRC-001–S1B-W7-SRC-024]
3. **Affiliation and boundaries:** Sampled populations show overlapping relationships and category-linked differences in care, identity and obligation; no single natural group unit or inevitable hostility follows. [Sources: S1B-W4-SRC-001–S1B-W4-SRC-006; S1B-W4-SRC-008–S1B-W4-SRC-016; S1B-W4-SRC-018–S1B-W4-SRC-024]
4. **Rank and power:** Groups recurrently distribute attention, esteem and influence through multiple currencies, while institutions can reproduce advantage and people can resist domination. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-001–S1B-W3-SRC-014; S1B-W3-SRC-016–S1B-W3-SRC-024]
5. **Collective action:** Cooperation varies with competition, reciprocity, monitoring, enforcement and institutional conditions; observed collective effects do not establish justice. [Sources: S1B-W5-SRC-001–S1B-W5-SRC-024]
6. **Meaning and commitment:** Humans interpret conduct through coherence, purpose, mattering, identity, ritual and duty; these mechanisms can support mutual commitment or coercive burden. [Sources: S1B-W6-SRC-001–S1B-W6-SRC-011; S1B-W6-SRC-014–S1B-W6-SRC-024]

**Revision rule:** These domains may overlap, decompose or require replacement during Stage 1R. Central traceability: S1B-EVD-001–S1B-EVD-008; S1B-UNC-001–S1B-UNC-003.

## Status, Power and Coalition Analysis

Status must be disaggregated into prestige, dominance, authority, inherited position, visibility and resource control. In some documented settings these currencies converted into one another in ways associated with institutions and audiences. Visibility, reputation, deference and resource access could become mutually reinforcing, but the reviewed evidence does not establish one general causal sequence; prior resources, selection and institutional appointment remain alternatives. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-001–S1B-W3-SRC-008; S1B-W3-SRC-020–S1B-W3-SRC-024]

Coalitions are central both to power and resistance. They can coordinate exclusion or collective protection; they can support a dominant order or prevent one person from consolidating command. Levelling practices demonstrate that recurrent rank does not entail fixed hierarchy. At the same time, visible equality can coexist with concealed gendered, domestic or ritual inequalities. [Sources: S1B-W3-SRC-009–S1B-W3-SRC-014; S1B-W4-SRC-008–S1B-W4-SRC-016]

Every status claim should therefore answer five questions: What is the currency? Who recognises it? What converts it into power? Who benefits or is obscured? What forms of resistance remain credible? Stage 1B does not authorise rank as a proxy for merit, competence, legitimacy or necessity.

## Culture and Variation Boundaries

- Country count is not cultural coverage; national averages can conceal major within-country variation. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-002–S1B-W8-SRC-007]
- Translation does not establish construct equivalence; the required level of equivalence depends on the claim. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-006; S1B-W8-SRC-008–S1B-W8-SRC-011]
- Western-developed constructs of autonomy, belonging, personality, morality and meaning may not preserve the same structure or local significance elsewhere. [Sources: S1B-W2-SRC-007–S1B-W2-SRC-012; S1B-W4-SRC-001–S1B-W4-SRC-004; S1B-W6-SRC-001–S1B-W6-SRC-006; S1B-W8-SRC-008–S1B-W8-SRC-012]
- Ecological and institutional associations are not causal proof; migration, selection, diffusion and historical co-development remain alternatives. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-012–S1B-W8-SRC-015; S1B-W8-SRC-017]
- Variation can falsify claims of uniformity but cannot by itself identify whether a pattern reflects calibration, preparedness, measurement or their interaction. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-018, S1B-W8-SRC-020–S1B-W8-SRC-024]
- Small-scale societies are contemporary communities, not windows onto a frozen ancestral past.

## Claims of Recurrence versus Claims of Innateness

| Claim level | Evidence needed | Stage 1B judgement |
|---|---|---|
| Observed recurrence | Pattern appears in multiple adequately described settings | Supported for several broad problems and functions |
| Broad cross-cultural recurrence | Diverse settings with credible construct equivalence and boundary tests | Partly supported, with uneven coverage |
| Universality | Near-species-wide coverage or a strong theory of missing cases, equivalent measures and no material counterexample | Not established for a fixed drive or institution inventory |
| Developmental emergence | Repeated age-sensitive evidence across settings with suitable tasks | Suggestive for selected capacities; pathways remain mixed |
| Biological preparedness | Convergent developmental, comparative and mechanistic evidence | Plausible for some broad capacities, not cleanly partitioned |
| Innateness | Evidence excluding common learning, diffusion, convergence and artefact while specifying innate content | Not established for any major report claim |
| Evolutionary origin or adaptation | Comparative and historical logic plus evidence discriminating alternatives | Not established by recurrence or present usefulness |

The governing inference is: recurrence can identify a candidate function worth carrying into reconciliation; it cannot settle origin, necessity, legitimacy or value. [Sources: S1B-W8-SRC-001, S1B-W8-SRC-006; S1B-W8-SRC-008, S1B-W8-SRC-009; S1B-W8-SRC-018, S1B-W8-SRC-020–S1B-W8-SRC-024]

## What Stage 1B Does Not Establish

- It does not establish a complete or fixed list of human drives.
- It does not establish a strong human universal, innate psychological content or a single evolutionary origin for any major synthesis.
- It does not show that hierarchy, inequality, tribal hostility, punishment, religion or sacrifice is inevitable.
- It does not make recurring institutions legitimate, beneficial or necessary.
- It does not show that culture is unconstrained, that biology is irrelevant, or that all differences are socially constructed.
- It does not establish one natural family, kinship, authority, cooperation or moral system.
- It does not allow neural, hormonal, behavioural or psychometric measures to serve as transparent readings of subjective state or character.
- It does not treat existing rank as merit, successful coordination as justice, or contribution as voluntariness.
- It does not resolve whether particular recurrences arise from preparedness, common developmental demands, convergent learning, diffusion, ecology or institutional reproduction.
- It does not authorise use beyond Stage 1R.

## Claim Classification Summary

**Empirical Findings:** Narrow component patterns recur within the reviewed samples, with strong evidence for several distinctions—fear versus defence, wanting versus liking, prestige versus dominance, ingroup preference versus hostility, and punishment effects versus justice—and for contextual variability. The six-domain grouping is not itself an Empirical Finding.

**Working Assumptions:** The six-domain baseline and problem–solution architecture are analytical compressions, not a modular ontology or exhaustive account. Their usefulness is moderate, their transportability is open, and Stage 1R must revise or reject them if broader sampling, non-equivalence or finer-grained evidence exposes hidden mechanisms.

**Normative Judgements:** Recurrence is not treated as legitimacy. Reports of rank, duty, punishment, sacrifice or cooperation must identify power, beneficiaries, casualties and resistance. This is an explicit project value and interpretive discipline, not an empirical discovery.

**Speculative Projections:** None.

## Uncertainties and Open Questions

1. Which candidate functions survive broader linguistic, ecological and institutional sampling with construct-equivalent measures?
2. How should evidence distinguish biological preparedness from common caregiving problems, convergent learning, diffusion and task affordance?
3. At what level are threat, reward, attachment, autonomy, belonging, fairness and meaning comparable across settings?
4. When does cue-responsive action extend agency, and when does it preserve obsolete or imposed conduct?
5. What determines whether affiliation produces mutual care, indifference, exclusion or hostility?
6. Under what conditions does prestige remain freely conferred rather than convert into dominance or inherited authority?
7. How do gendered, domestic, ritual and informal inequalities interact with visible public rank?
8. When do monitoring and sanctions stabilise mutually beneficial action rather than protect extraction?
9. What evidence can distinguish endorsed contribution from obligation under dependence or coercion?
10. When do ritual and protected values support dignity, and when do they make manipulation or conflict more resistant to challenge?
11. How much observed cross-cultural variation reflects lived difference versus translation, unfamiliar tasks, response conventions and researcher power?
12. Which apparent convergences with the parallel component are genuinely independent rather than products of shared sources or constructs?

## Conclusion

The evidence does not reveal a hidden catalogue of universal drives waiting beneath culture. It shows recurring patterns concerning regulation, bounded attention, control-related action, affiliation, rank, cooperation and commitment across the sampled literatures. The six-domain architecture used to connect them is a Working Assumption, not a discovered system.

Observed form and moral direction remain variable. Regulation may protect or exhaust. Habits may extend action or preserve constraint. Affiliation may accompany care or exclusion. Prestige may reward knowledge or convert into domination. Sanctions have conditional collective effects and do not establish justice. Ritual may accompany solidarity or costly dissent. Cultural and institutional conditions are associated with differences in triggers, meanings, options and consequences and affect what researchers can see.

The most defensible conclusion is therefore narrower than a model of human essence. The sampled evidence portrays humans as embodied, bounded and relational, with meaning-related practices and recurrent patterns concerning coordination. This wording is analytical and population-bounded. Recurrence supports candidates for reconciliation; it does not establish completeness, innateness, inevitability, legitimacy or value.

Stage 1B passes its component gate with cautions after evidence, bias, cultural-generalisability and adversarial review. Its reviewed findings pass only to Stage 1R, where they must be compared with Stage 1A without strengthening claims or hiding disagreement.
