# Stage 2 Public Summary — What We Mean by Flourishing

> **Status:** Passed with cautions after evidence, bias and adversarial review.

## The question

Stage 2 asked what “flourishing” should mean for this project. Evidence can show what tends to accompany well-being and healthy development, but it cannot decide by itself what a good life ought to be. The report therefore separates research findings from explicit value choices.

## The central idea

Flourishing is not just happiness, achievement, fitting in or being useful. A person can score well on any one of those while lacking safety, freedom, care or meaning.

The proposed model defines flourishing as a growing ability to live a life one has reason to value. That requires enough bodily and material security to act; real rather than token agency; developing competence; relationships that recognise without controlling; purposes that can be questioned; and ways to contribute without being forced to sacrifice one’s worth or voice.

## What the evidence supports

Research repeatedly links experiences of volition, effectiveness and supportive connection with motivation and well-being. Relationship quality matters more than simply counting contacts. Meaning and helping others can matter, but their effects vary.

The project also adopts a bounded structural premise: material resources, health, rights, safety and institutions condition which options are genuinely available. Conceptual, official and cross-national frameworks inform this Working Assumption, but this review does not establish one causal structural account.

These are population-level patterns, not diagnoses. Much evidence is observational, culturally uneven and based on Western measures. It does not establish one universal list or weighting.

## What the project chooses to value

The project adopts a “floor and freedom” approach. Everyone should have a basic floor of safety, bodily integrity, material security, equal standing and real opportunity. Above that floor, people should have substantial room for different identities, relationships, commitments and ways of living.

It also values responsibility: recognising other people’s equal standing, understanding consequences, repairing harm and contributing where burdens are fair. Responsibility does not mean obedience or compulsory self-sacrifice.

## Special care with young people

Young people have lives that matter now, not only future potential. Their voice, joy, safety, privacy and relationships matter in the present. They are also still developing capacities and future options. The model therefore rejects both ignoring adolescent agency and treating every immediate preference as decisive. Protection should be proportionate, explainable and open to review.

## What readers should not conclude

This stage does not create a happiness test, a learner profile or a curriculum. It does not say everyone needs the same relationships, purpose or degree of independence. It does not treat hardship as necessary for growth or usefulness as the source of human worth.

The seven dimensions are a decision framework, not a score. Later stages must preserve their tensions, uncertainties and protections against coercion.
