## 2. Stage objective

Determine the minimum defensible empirical findings and clearly labelled working assumptions about human beings that an educational framework for KS3–KS4 learners must respect. The stage will examine motivation, agency, cognition, attention, memory, emotion, embodiment, adolescence, identity, social belonging, cooperation, competition and individual variation without attempting to define flourishing, predict AI futures, derive capabilities or design curriculum.

## 4. Stage boundaries

### 4.1 In scope

- motivation, incentives, agency and self-direction;
- attention, cognition, memory and learning constraints;
- adolescent development, identity, executive function and moral development;
- emotion, embodiment and self-regulation;
- status, belonging, peer dynamics, cooperation and competition;
- individual variation, neurodiversity and cross-cultural generalisability;
- tensions and constraints that later stages must preserve;
- classification of empirical findings, working assumptions and uncertainties.

### 4.2 Out of scope

- a universal or exhaustive theory of human nature;
- a model of flourishing or the good life;

## 5. Research question tree

### 5.1 Primary question

> What minimum defensible human baseline must a serious KS3–KS4 educational framework respect?

### 5.2 Secondary questions

1. Which features of human motivation, agency and behaviour are sufficiently evidenced to constrain later educational reasoning?
2. Which developmental realities of adolescence matter for KS3–KS4 learners?
3. How do cognition, attention, memory, emotion and embodiment shape learning and action?
4. Which forms of individual, neurodevelopmental and cultural variation prevent a single standard-learner assumption?
5. Which tensions among autonomy, belonging, status, cooperation, competition and meaning must remain visible?
6. Which conclusions are empirical findings, working assumptions, normative judgements or unresolved uncertainties?

### 5.3 Tertiary questions and workstreams

| Workstream | Sub-questions | Evidence needed | Target included sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| W1 — Motivation and agency | What drives engagement and persistence? How do autonomy, incentives and perceived control interact? Where are effects context-dependent? | Reviews and strong studies in motivation, self-determination, agency and incentive effects | 15–22 |
| W2 — Cognition and learning | What constraints arise from attention, memory, prior knowledge, cognitive load and metacognition? | Cognitive and learning-science reviews, meta-analyses and consensus sources | 18–25 |
| W3 — Adolescent development | What changes in executive function, identity, moral reasoning, risk, autonomy and future orientation are educationally relevant? | Developmental reviews with explicit adolescent and KS3–KS4 relevance | 20–28 |
| W4 — Emotion and embodiment | How do emotion, stress, sleep, physical development and embodied experience affect learning and agency? | Developmental, affective and embodied-cognition reviews | 14–20 |
| W5 — Social life and status | How do peers, belonging, status, cooperation and competition affect behaviour and learning? | Social, developmental, educational and anthropological evidence | 15–22 |
| W6 — Variation and generalisability | Which findings vary by culture, class, context, neurotype, disability, language or resources? | Cross-cultural, neurodiversity, inclusion and individual-difference evidence | 18–25 |

The final included corpus must contain 100–150 unique sources after deduplication. Source targets guide coverage; they are not quotas that justify weak evidence.

### 5.4 Questions deliberately excluded

| Excluded question | Reason for exclusion | Where it should go instead |
|---|---|---|
| What is the ideal human life? | Normative rather than baseline empirical question | Stage 2 |
| Which jobs will remain valuable? | Future-facing and speculative | Stage 3 onward |
| Which capabilities should learners develop? | Requires later derivation | Stage 7 |
| Which subjects should be retained? | Risks curriculum-first anchoring | Stage 8 and Stage 10 |
| Which intervention works best? | Stage 1 identifies constraints rather than selecting programmes | Later learning-design and implementation stages |

## 6. Method selection

| Method | Why this method fits | What it can answer | What it cannot answer | Output produced |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Extended rapid evidence review | Covers a broad multidisciplinary question while remaining bounded and reproducible | What high-quality literature supports, contests or leaves unresolved | Exhaustive coverage of every discipline or causal certainty | Logged source and evidence corpus |
| Developmental constraint mapping | Connects adolescent evidence to limits later stages must respect | Developmental cautions and age-sensitive constraints | Curriculum sequence or pedagogy | Adolescent Development Constraints |
| Cross-domain convergence synthesis | Tests whether conclusions recur across independent evidence areas | Robust baseline candidates and disagreements | Value-neutral resolution of normative disputes | Minimum Human Baseline |
| Learner variation mapping | Prevents the standard-learner assumption | Important variation, access and inclusion cautions | Individual diagnosis or universal accommodation rules | Learner Variation Map |
| Human tension mapping | Preserves relationships that cannot be reduced to one-variable prescriptions | Tensions among agency, status, belonging, cooperation and competition | A final flourishing model | Human Tension Map |
| Claim classification and traceability | Separates evidence, assumptions, values and uncertainty | Permitted downstream use and limits | Whether a value judgement should ultimately be adopted | Ledger and pass-forward candidates |
| Evidence and bias audits | Challenges claim inflation and narrow generalisation | Weak support, missing counterevidence and bias risks | New empirical findings without sources | `STAGE_REVIEW.md` and revisions |

### 6.1 Method risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Breadth produces shallow synthesis | High | High | Use workstream boundaries, review-level sources and explicit evidence weights |
| English-language or WEIRD evidence dominates | High | High | Dedicated W6 search, bias fields and final generalisability audit |
| Review evidence hides population differences | Moderate | High | Extract population, setting, age range and heterogeneity into scope/limitations |
| Foundational theories become untested assumptions | Moderate | High | Require current reviews or convergent evidence; otherwise classify as assumption |
| Educational relevance is inferred too freely | Moderate | High | Separate evidence finding from downstream implication and prohibit curriculum decisions |
| Source-count pressure rewards weak material | Moderate | Moderate | Treat 100–150 as an inclusion range after quality screening, not a discovery quota |
| Agent consensus erases disagreement | Moderate | High | Preserve contested findings and independent audit objections in ledger/report |

### 6.2 Rejected methods

| Method rejected | Reason rejected | Risk if this rejection is wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Direct learner research | Not authorised, unnecessary for this evidence-review stage and creates ethics/privacy obligations | Some lived-experience nuance may be underrepresented; carry as uncertainty |
| Curriculum comparison | Risks importing inherited structures before derivation | Some implementation context is deferred appropriately |
| Single grand theory of human nature | Exceeds the stage purpose and encourages ideological overreach | Synthesis may be less elegant but more defensible |
| Quantitative meta-analysis conducted by the project | Evidence areas are too heterogeneous for a valid new pooled estimate | The stage relies on published syntheses instead |
| Informal expert opinion as evidence | Expertise can guide searches but cannot substitute for logged sources | Emerging issues may remain open rather than falsely settled |

## 8. Source discovery plan

### 8.1 Source types to prioritise

1. Systematic reviews, meta-analyses and consensus reports.
2. Strong recent narrative or umbrella reviews where systematic evidence is unavailable.
3. Strong primary studies for gaps, contested findings or underrepresented populations.
4. Official datasets and institutional reports for population context, not causal proof.
5. Cross-cultural, qualitative and lived-experience research where context and generalisability are central.
6. Foundational theoretical work only when continuing relevance is justified by later evidence.

Discovery will use ERIC, PubMed, APA/PsycNet where accessible, scholarly discovery indexes and authoritative institutional repositories. Searches will be current through the recorded execution date.

### 8.2 Search themes

| Theme | Search terms / source targets | Reason needed |
|---|---|---|
| Motivation and agency | adolescent motivation, autonomy support, self-determination, incentives, self-efficacy, learned helplessness, agency reviews | W1 baseline |
| Cognition and learning | adolescent attention, working memory, prior knowledge, cognitive load, metacognition, transfer, learning science reviews | W2 constraints |
| Adolescent development | executive function, identity development, moral reasoning, puberty, risk, future orientation, developmental reviews | W3 constraints |
| Emotion and embodiment | emotion regulation, stress, sleep, physical development, embodied cognition, adolescent learning | W4 constraints |
| Social life | peer influence, belonging, status, cooperation, competition, social identity, school connectedness | W5 tensions |
| Variation and generalisability | neurodiversity, disability, cross-cultural adolescence, socioeconomic context, multilingual learners, gender, resource inequality | W6 audit |

Each workstream must seek at least two credible limiting, null, contradictory or boundary-setting sources where available.

### 8.3 Inclusion rules

- directly answers a Stage 1 question;
- source identity and citation are verifiable;
- population, context and method can be described;
- evidence strength and limitations can be assessed;
- reviews are preferably recent, while older sources require a continuing-relevance justification;
- adult-only evidence is contextual unless adolescent relevance is separately supported;
- duplicate reports of the same evidence base are identified and not treated as independent convergence.

### 8.4 Exclusion rules

- unsourced commentary, marketing, popular summaries or prompt-generated claims;
- curriculum prescriptions presented as evidence about humans;
- AI-futures, labour-market or policy material belonging to later stages;
- sources whose claims cannot be verified or whose relevance is merely rhetorical;
- individual anecdotes used as universal evidence;
- studies used outside their population, setting or measured construct without explicit limitation;
- low-quality sources added solely to meet a numerical target.

### 8.5 Verified source-selection outcome

Source discovery and metadata verification were completed on 2026-06-17. The
included corpus contains 120 unique sources: 20 in each of W1–W6. Selection
prioritised review-level evidence while retaining strong primary studies,
cross-cultural evidence and explicit boundary-setting or contrary findings.

Candidate records were deduplicated by DOI or authoritative source URL. A
duplicate account of the age boundaries of adolescence was retained only in W3,
not counted again in W6. Citations that did not resolve to an authoritative
record were corrected where the publication could be established or excluded
and replaced. Inclusion in the source corpus does not by itself authorise a
Stage 1 finding; evidence extraction, synthesis and Gate 2 review remain
required.

## 9. Stage ledger setup

`STAGE_LEDGER.csv` is the only structured register. It begins with the canonical 32-column template header.

### 9.1 Record IDs

| Record type | ID pattern |
|---|---|

| `evidence` | `S1-EVD-###` |
| `claim` | `S1-CLM-###` |
| `assumption` | `S1-ASM-###` |
| `uncertainty` | `S1-UNC-###` |
| `decision` | `S1-DEC-###` |
| `risk` | `S1-RSK-###` |
| `traceability` | `S1-TRC-###` |
| `challenge` | `S1-CHL-###` |
| `revision_trigger` | `S1-REV-###` |

### 9.2 Ledger rules

- One source record per unique included or transparently rejected source.
- Evidence records must link to source records.
- Claim records must link to supporting and limiting evidence records.
- Every main report finding must link to at least one claim record.
- Every pass-forward finding must link to a reviewed report finding and ledger claim.
- Related IDs use semicolon-separated values.
- Canonical claim classes, confidence labels and evidence weights must be used exactly.
- Empty fields are permitted only when genuinely not applicable; material omissions must be explained in `notes`.
- Agent analysis is never entered as a source or evidence record.

### 9.3 Minimum corpus and coverage checks

- 100–150 unique included source records after deduplication;
- visible review-level evidence in every workstream;
- explicit cross-cultural and neurodiversity coverage;
- limiting or dissenting evidence sought in every workstream;
- all source limitations and population scope recorded;
- all high-impact claims have counterevidence or a documented search result stating none was found.
