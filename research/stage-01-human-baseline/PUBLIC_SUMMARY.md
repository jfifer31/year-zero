# Stage 1 Public Summary — The Person Is More Than the Performance

> **Component status (20 June 2026):** This summary describes Stage 1A. The findings remain useful but await reconciliation with the general human baseline before downstream use.

## What question did this stage ask?

What is the minimum defensible account of how human beings learn, act and develop that an educational framework for KS3–KS4 must respect?

Stage 1 did not ask what the curriculum should contain or what flourishing should mean. It asked what later stages must not forget about the people education is for.

## The central finding

Education sees performances: an answer, a silence, an effort, a refusal, a score, a rule followed or broken. The evidence says these performances matter—but they do not reveal the whole person.

Observed performance reflects interactions among what a learner already knows, the demands of the task, motivation, bodily and emotional state, relationships, development, opportunity and context. These conditions do not erase agency, and individual differences are real. They do mean that one performance should not be treated as a complete reading of ability, motive, maturity or character.

## What the stage found

**Knowledge changes how difficult a task is.** Human attention and working memory are limited, but prior knowledge can organise many details into meaningful patterns. The same task may therefore place very different demands on different learners. Remembering knowledge and transferring it into an unfamiliar situation are also distinct achievements. [Ledger: `S1-CLM-004`, `S1-CLM-005`]

**Motivation has different qualities.** Interest, personally accepted value, confidence, social pressure and reward can all produce visible effort, but they do not mean the same thing. A learner can willingly persist with work that is not enjoyable because its purpose matters to them. Compliance alone does not show personal commitment. [Ledger: `S1-CLM-001`–`S1-CLM-003`]

**Capability and its momentary use are not identical.** Sleep, stress and emotion can alter readiness and observed functioning. That does not mean every weak performance has a hidden bodily cause, or that improving readiness will automatically raise grades. It means a temporary state or one result does not diagnose fixed potential. [Ledger: `S1-CLM-010`; uncertainty: `S1-UNC-005`]

**Adolescents have meaningful capacities, not an absence of reason.** Their control can be less consistently deployed when tasks are unfamiliar, emotional or socially exposed. Social information and changing priorities can matter greatly, but adolescents are not uniformly reckless or controlled by peers. Age and puberty do not provide one maturity timetable. [Ledger: `S1-CLM-006`–`S1-CLM-009`]

**Peers can amplify good or harmful local norms.** Belonging is more than being present. In the mainly school-based evidence reviewed, it involves recognition, accepted identity, safety and supportive relationships. Popularity is not the same as acceptance, and membership can support people while also excluding them. Belonging is associated with important outcomes, but the evidence does not show that it independently causes them. [Ledger: `S1-CLM-009`, `S1-CLM-011`, `S1-CLM-012`]

**There is no single standard learner.** Language, culture, disability, neurotype, gender, material conditions, prior opportunity and institutional access are associated with what learners can show and what systems can see. Group patterns can be useful, but no diagnosis or demographic category supplies a complete individual profile. [Ledger: `S1-CLM-014`, `S1-CLM-015`]

## Why this matters

If education mistakes performance for person, it can turn unfamiliarity into low ability, compliance into agency, temporary strain into weak character, popularity into belonging, or a group average into an individual future.

The opposite mistake is also possible. Context does not explain everything, and it does not remove agency, knowledge gaps, impairment or responsibility. The defensible position is the harder one: interpret behaviour carefully while preserving both real human constraints and the person acting within them.

## What remains uncertain?

Important questions remain open:

- what causes associations involving autonomy support;
- how far learning transfers into complex real situations;
- whether adolescent sensitive periods exist in particular domains;
- how identity and mental health influence one another;
- how causal and age-specific sleep, stress, regulation and movement effects are;
- what produces outcomes associated with belonging;
- which findings travel across cultures, languages and institutions;
- how neurotype, disability, language, gender, poverty and access interact.

The evidence base is broad but not exhaustive. English-language and Western research is overrepresented. Several areas rely on self-report, correlational studies or laboratory tasks. These limits are part of the result.

## What readers should not conclude

Stage 1 does not show that:

- adolescents are irrational;
- rewards are always harmful or choice is always beneficial;
- belonging, sleep or confidence automatically raises attainment;
- one diagnosis, score, age or behaviour reveals potential;
- context excuses every action;
- one culture's model of autonomy, identity or morality is universal;
- any particular curriculum, pedagogy or assessment should now be adopted.

## What passes to the next stage?

Stage 1 passes with cautions. Stage 2 may use seven controlled findings: bounded and condition-sensitive performance; knowledge and transfer; motivational quality; embodied deployment; adolescent developmental variability; social influence and belonging; and structured learner variation.

Stage 2 must still decide what flourishing should mean. That is partly a value judgement, not something Stage 1 evidence can settle on its own. The full authority and cautions are recorded in `PASS_FORWARD.md`.

## Plain-language takeaway

A learner is never only the performance in front of us. Education should take performance seriously without pretending it is the whole person.
