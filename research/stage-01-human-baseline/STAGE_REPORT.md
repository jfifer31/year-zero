> **Stage status:** `pass_with_cautions` — revised after evidence, bias, generalisability and adversarial audit. Only claims reproduced in `PASS_FORWARD.md` are authorised downstream.
>
> **Evidence cut-off:** 17 June 2026
> **Scope:** Stage 1 only. This report establishes constraints for later reasoning; it does not define flourishing or design a curriculum.

## Executive Summary

Education observes surfaces. It sees an answer given or withheld, attention sustained or lost, effort continued or abandoned, a rule followed or broken. Its most consequential mistake is to assume that these performances reveal the learner directly.

The Stage 1 evidence supports a more demanding view. Human beings are bounded but active interpreters. What a person can do at a particular moment emerges from an interaction among accumulated knowledge, cognitive demands, motivation, bodily and emotional state, relationships, social meaning, development, opportunity and the way performance is measured. None of these conditions wholly determines action. Learners retain agency; cognitive constraints and relatively stable differences are real. But observable behaviour is evidence requiring interpretation, not a transparent reading of fixed ability, motive or character.

Six empirical conclusions and one normative boundary organise the report:

1. **Knowledge changes the effective limits of thought.** Attention and working memory are bounded, but prior knowledge can compress a complex task into meaningful units. Identical tasks are therefore not psychologically identical. Retention can be strengthened; transfer into unfamiliar situations is a separate achievement. [Sources: S1-SRC-025, S1-SRC-026, S1-SRC-028–S1-SRC-040]
2. **Motivation has quality as well as quantity.** Interest, personally endorsed value, competence belief, social pressure and reward can produce similar visible effort while organising persistence, performance quality and well-being differently. Identified value can sustain work that is not enjoyable; compliance does not demonstrate endorsement. [Sources: S1-SRC-001–S1-SRC-004, S1-SRC-009–S1-SRC-017]
3. **Capability and deployment are different.** Sleep, stress and emotion can alter readiness and the use of existing capacities. Better readiness need not register as higher grades, and a temporary state does not diagnose potential. [Sources: S1-SRC-061–S1-SRC-069, S1-SRC-071–S1-SRC-074]
4. **Adolescence changes priorities and consistency, not the possession of reason.** Control capacities are meaningful while their flexible deployment continues to develop. Social evaluation, identity and emotionally significant goals can change the problem an adolescent believes they are solving. There is no single maturity timetable. [Sources: S1-SRC-041–S1-SRC-052]
5. **Peers and institutions alter the meaning of action.** Peer sensitivity can amplify prosocial conduct as well as risk. Belonging involves recognition, accepted identity and safety, not mere proximity; popularity is not acceptance. [Sources: S1-SRC-081–S1-SRC-097]
6. **Compliance does not disclose motive; responsibility remains a normative question.** Moral development and future-directed action are associated with motivation, emotion, identity, relationships, opportunity and culturally situated norms. These findings do not define responsibility or settle accountability; Stage 2 must do that work explicitly. [Sources: S1-SRC-002, S1-SRC-053–S1-SRC-060, S1-SRC-089, S1-SRC-096, S1-SRC-097]
7. **No single standard-learner profile is defensible.** Culture, language, socioeconomic conditions, disability, neurotype, gender, prior opportunity and institutional access are associated with performance, can shape it, and affect what measurement makes visible. Group patterns can matter without determining an individual. [Sources: S1-SRC-101–S1-SRC-120]

**Classification:** Items 1–5 and 7 are Empirical Findings integrated through cross-source synthesis; confidence is moderate overall and high for several bounded component findings. Item 6 combines an empirical caution about inference from compliance with a Normative Judgement explicitly reserved for Stage 2. Confidence is lower for a unified theory of action, precise causal mechanisms and cross-cultural transportability.

## Central Thesis

**Across the reviewed evidence, observed educational performance reflects interactions among person, task, development and context. Attention, effort, judgement, recall and conduct should therefore be treated as evidence about a bounded, developing person—not as a complete or context-free reading of fixed ability, motive or character. The precise mechanisms and their transportability vary.**

This thesis rejects two tempting simplifications. The first is learner essentialism: the idea that a score or behaviour cleanly exposes an inner quantity of intelligence, motivation or character. The second is context determinism: the idea that people are merely products of circumstances and bear no agency. The evidence supports neither. Human action is constrained without being mechanical, situated without being wholly caused by the situation, and patterned without making individual lives predictable.

## Prologue: The Person Behind the Performance

Imagine two learners looking at the same page. One begins quickly. The other waits, looks away and eventually stops. The visible contrast is small enough to name in a sentence and large enough to change a life. One may be called able, motivated or ready. The other may be called weak, distracted or unwilling.

But the page is not the same psychological event for both learners. One may recognise familiar structures where the other sees many unrelated elements. One may expect success because previous success has made that expectation credible. The other may be working in a less familiar language, carrying a night of poor sleep, anticipating public embarrassment, or judging that the promised future reward is not realistically available. The quick learner may be genuinely engaged—or merely skilled at producing the expected surface. The hesitant learner may lack relevant knowledge—or understand the social stakes more clearly than the observer does.

The point is not that any compassionate explanation must be true. It is that the behaviour does not tell us which explanation is true. The same outward act can arise through different mechanisms; different outward acts can conceal similar capacities. Educational observation is therefore an inference problem.

Stage 1 asks what minimum account of human beings later stages must respect. It cannot offer a total theory of human nature. Its evidence comes from heterogeneous literatures with different measures, populations and limits. Yet across motivation, cognition, adolescent development, embodiment, social life and human variation, one pattern recurs: the learner cannot be understood apart from the conditions in which learning and action become possible, meaningful and observable.

That pattern has consequences. Before education asks what young people should become, it must become more careful about what present behaviour means. Before it interprets failure, it must distinguish knowledge from processing burden, motivation from compliance, capacity from deployment, belonging from attendance, and a population average from an individual future. The chapters that follow build that interpretive model step by step.

## Chapter 1 – Knowledge Changes the Size of the Problem

The prologue established that a performance is not self-explanatory. The first reason is cognitive: a task has no fixed psychological size.

Human attention and working memory are limited. When material is unfamiliar, its elements must be selected, held and coordinated under those limits. Prior knowledge changes this burden. It allows separate details to be organised into meaningful structures, so an experienced learner can process what is physically the same material as fewer functional units. Expertise need not mean possessing a larger general-purpose mental container; it can mean that knowledge has changed what must be contained. [Sources: S1-SRC-025, S1-SRC-026, S1-SRC-030–S1-SRC-032]

This is why an identical task can be unequal without anyone altering the instructions. For one learner, the task calls up a structure; for another, it demands search. For one, guidance removes unnecessary burden; for another, the same guidance has become redundant or obstructive. A smooth performance may reflect an accumulated history of opportunity as much as an abstract capacity, while hesitation may reveal unfamiliarity rather than a global limitation. Prior-knowledge measures themselves can encode differences in language, culture and access, so they cannot be treated as neutral measures of potential. [Sources: S1-SRC-027, S1-SRC-030–S1-SRC-032]

Knowledge also changes subsequent learning. What is already organised can receive new material; what can be retrieved becomes available for combination. Retrieval practice and spacing generally support delayed retention, although effects vary with timing, feedback, material, stakes and outcome. These are important findings, but they define one part of learning rather than the whole. [Sources: S1-SRC-034–S1-SRC-038]

The boundary appears at transfer. A learner can recall information and still fail to recognise when it applies. Gains on a practised task, a trained strategy or a researcher-aligned measure do not automatically become competence in a distant, messy situation. Transfer depends partly on relevant knowledge, scaffolding and noticing structural similarity under changed conditions. Strong claims for broad cognitive training or minimally guided discovery exceed what this evidence can establish; equally, the evidence does not prove that unaided exploration is always ineffective or that instruction must be passive. [Sources: S1-SRC-028–S1-SRC-033, S1-SRC-039, S1-SRC-040; uncertainty: S1-UNC-002]

This distinction changes how educational success should be read. Immediate fluency can be produced by familiarity or heavy support without proving portable understanding. Failure in an unfamiliar setting can reflect difficulty recognising relevance rather than complete absence of learning. Retention, understanding and transfer are related, but they are not synonyms.

**Claim type:** Empirical Finding; **method:** cross-source synthesis; **confidence:** high for bounded processing, knowledge effects and the retention–transfer distinction; moderate when extending laboratory-supported findings into long-duration, collaborative and culturally varied KS3–KS4 activity.

The implication at this stage is a constraint, not a pedagogy. Later reasoning must treat prior knowledge as part of the learning condition, distinguish immediate performance from durable retention and authentic transfer, and resist interpreting a single processing demand as an equal test of all learners. Cognition is genuinely limited. But what those limits permit depends partly on what learning has already made possible.

## Chapter 2 – Effort Has More Than One Engine

Knowledge changes the size of a task. It does not explain why a learner takes the task up, persists with it or decides that it matters. For that, the model needs motivation—but not motivation understood as a fuel gauge.

Two learners can complete the same work through different relations to it. One may find it interesting. Another may not enjoy it but may personally endorse its purpose. A third may act to gain a reward, avoid shame, satisfy an authority or protect a reputation. These motives can produce similar visible effort while showing different associations with persistence, performance quality and well-being. [Sources: S1-SRC-001, S1-SRC-002, S1-SRC-010, S1-SRC-013–S1-SRC-017]

One of the most useful findings is also one of the least sentimental: willing effort does not require constant enjoyment. Identified regulation—acting because the person understands and endorses the significance of an activity—is strongly associated with persistence. Humans can willingly do difficult or uninteresting things when the reason has become their own. Intrinsic motivation is associated with success and well-being, while externally controlled motives show fewer adaptive associations; nevertheless, controlled motives can produce short-term action. The important distinction is not between pleasant and unpleasant work, but among the different meanings under which work is undertaken. [Sources: S1-SRC-001, S1-SRC-002]

Competence enters this system twice. Believing success is possible can make effort appear worthwhile, but successful performance can also make later confidence credible. Longitudinal synthesis found performance predicting later self-efficacy more strongly than the reverse, with limited child evidence failing to support a confidence-to-performance effect. This does not make belief irrelevant. It warns against trying to install agency as a belief while leaving inaccessible tasks, absent knowledge or repeated failure untouched. [Source: S1-SRC-016]

The intervention evidence gives the same warning. Motivation interventions have positive average educational effects, but results vary substantially by intervention, outcome, age and design; randomised estimates are smaller than quasi-experimental ones. Growth-mindset effects are weak on average, with small context-conditioned benefits in some lower-achieving groups. A belief may become actionable only where the surrounding environment makes it plausible. Context is not background noise around agency; it can be part of the mechanism. [Sources: S1-SRC-012, S1-SRC-018, S1-SRC-019]

Autonomy is similarly easy to caricature. It is not the absence of expectations or the multiplication of choices. Autonomy-supportive conditions are more strongly associated with autonomous motivation, engagement and self-beliefs than with attainment. Choice has average benefits in some experiments, but effects vary with what is chosen, how meaningful the choice is and who is choosing. Learner reports of support also show stronger associations than observations or teacher reports, leaving causality and common-method effects unresolved. The reviewed evidence supports understanding autonomy as structured and relational: action can be owned while still involving guidance, limits and responsibility. These constructs were largely developed in Western research and may not capture culturally different forms of relational obligation or agency. [Sources: S1-SRC-003, S1-SRC-004, S1-SRC-011, S1-SRC-058]

Nor do rewards permit a slogan. Expected tangible rewards have sometimes reduced later free-choice engagement, but that literature is old and laboratory-heavy. Across broader domains, intrinsic motivation relates more strongly to performance quality and incentives to quantity. Field trials suggest that some input-linked incentives can alter behaviour while output-linked rewards do not reliably improve achievement. Rewards are neither universally corrupting nor a dependable route to learning. [Sources: S1-SRC-009, S1-SRC-010, S1-SRC-020]

**Claim type:** Empirical Finding; **method:** cross-source synthesis; **confidence:** high that motivational quality is multidimensional; moderate about causal pathways, cultural transportability and context-specific interventions.

The educational constraint is interpretive. Visible effort cannot disclose whether a learner feels interested, capable, pressured, safe or personally committed. Low effort does not identify one defect; high compliance does not prove agency. Later stages may consider how meaning, competence and ownership matter, but Stage 1 does not authorise a motivational recipe. It establishes only that action has more than one engine—and that the engine matters.

## Chapter 3 – The Mind Arrives in a Body

The first two chapters described a learner whose performance depends on knowledge and motive. That learner is still too abstract. The person who thinks and chooses also slept, became stressed, experienced emotion and entered the task in a bodily state.

Sleep makes the capacity–deployment distinction visible. Sleep duration, quality and temporal alignment are associated with school functioning, mood and emotion regulation. Experimental sleep-loss evidence indicates effects on positive affect, anxiety symptoms and emotional reactivity, although the studies span broad ages and do not establish one adolescent response. These findings support the claim that bodily state can alter readiness. They do not support the claim that a sleep measure reveals fixed capacity or that improving sleep will necessarily raise an individual learner’s attainment. [Sources: S1-SRC-061–S1-SRC-064]

Later school starts provide a particularly useful boundary. They are associated with longer sleep, less sleepiness, improved mood and lower social jetlag. Yet review evidence does not show a generalisable improvement in grades or test scores, although achievement is generally preserved. Readiness, mood and attainment sit at different levels of the system. A condition can matter without moving a coarse institutional outcome in a uniform way. [Sources: S1-SRC-065–S1-SRC-067]

Stress gives the same lesson in a different form. Acute stress tends to disrupt working memory and cognitive flexibility, while effects on inhibition vary. Threat and deprivation are associated with weaker executive functioning in childhood and adolescence. But much acute-stress evidence is adult-heavy and laboratory-based, while adversity evidence is correlational. It cannot establish fixed deficits, individual prognosis or one causal pathway. [Sources: S1-SRC-068, S1-SRC-069]

Emotion regulation is not an internal switch that a learner either possesses or lacks. It coordinates cognitive, motivational, social and physiological processes. Strategies differ in their associations with internalising and externalising symptoms, but constructs overlap and much evidence is cross-sectional. Even reappraisal varies with development, emotion, task and context. Physiological associations cannot be converted into a readable biological score of motivation, readiness or potential. [Sources: S1-SRC-071–S1-SRC-074]

The deeper point is not that the body overrides the mind. It is that the distinction is misleading. Population evidence shows that state can affect functioning; therefore one performance may be insufficient to distinguish a knowledge gap from disrupted deployment. Bodily state cannot become an all-purpose explanation for weak performance. The evidence does not tell an observer how much any particular result reflects sleep, stress, knowledge, effort, task structure or another difference. English-language and Western evidence is overrepresented, and the causal and cultural transportability of adversity and regulation findings remains limited.

**Claim type:** Empirical Finding; **method:** cross-source synthesis; **confidence:** moderate. The existence of state-sensitive functioning is better supported than its causal size, KS3–KS4 specificity or effect on attainment.

For later stages, the constraint is to distinguish capability from momentary deployment. No isolated performance, adversity history, bodily condition or physiological measure is a transparent diagnosis of potential. This does not remove standards or responsibility. It makes interpretation more honest: the learner education sees is partly the learner who could be present under those conditions on that day.

## Chapter 4 – Adolescence Is a Reorientation, Not a Deficit

All human performance is conditional. Adolescence adds a distinctive complication: the capacities, priorities and social worlds involved are themselves being reorganised.

Basic control processes are available before adolescence, while their reliable and flexible coordination continues to develop. Executive functions do not mature as one faculty or along one simple curve, and laboratory tasks provide imperfect access to everyday competence. An adolescent may reason carefully in calm conditions and act differently when social evaluation, emotion, uncertainty or immediate reward becomes salient. This need not mean that reason disappeared; social and emotional salience may have changed the weighting of the problem. [Sources: S1-SRC-042–S1-SRC-047]

Socially significant information can carry unusual weight during this period. Approval, rejection, reputation, novelty or an important group goal may enter a decision that an adult sees as purely academic. Behaviour that looks inconsistent from outside can therefore be coherent relative to a different priority. This preserves agency: context does not merely interfere with a completed decision-maker but helps establish what the decision is about. The evidence supports heightened salience and developmental reorientation, not a claim that every action follows conscious social calculation. [Sources: S1-SRC-042–S1-SRC-044]

Simple dual-systems stories—an overactive reward system defeating an unfinished control system—capture only part of this picture. They may illuminate some risk under some charged conditions, but critical and network-based accounts warn against treating them as a complete model. Risk-taking varies by task, construct and culture; sensation seeking, self-regulation and risk do not share one universal age curve. Adolescents are not uniformly reckless. [Sources: S1-SRC-049–S1-SRC-052]

Nor is adolescence merely vulnerability. Sensitivity to new goals and social information can support exploration, social learning and constructive engagement. The same peer responsiveness that can magnify risk can also magnify contribution. The evidence does not establish a universal sensitive period, but it does rule out treating developmental openness only as failure.

Development is also asynchronous. Chronological age, puberty, executive control, identity and movement into adult roles overlap without being interchangeable. Pubertal timing varies across people and contexts. Identity develops through exploration, commitment, reconsideration and continuity, with average maturation alongside stability and reversal. Future orientation divides into anticipation, planning, expectation and evaluation of delayed outcomes; these components do not mature on one timetable. [Sources: S1-SRC-041, S1-SRC-048, S1-SRC-053–S1-SRC-060]

This makes some familiar judgements unsafe. Uncertainty about identity is not automatically dysfunction. A stated aspiration is not necessarily a fixed self. Weakly articulated plans need not prove low aspiration or poor character: some futures are more credible and accessible than others. Opportunity and socioeconomic context are associated with future orientation, although the evidence cannot diagnose the cause of an individual learner’s short horizon. [Sources: S1-SRC-053, S1-SRC-055, S1-SRC-059, S1-SRC-060]

**Claim type:** Empirical Finding; **method:** cross-source synthesis; **confidence:** moderate. Confidence is limited by Western and selective samples, cross-sectional designs, measurement differences and uncertain ecological validity.

The KS3–KS4 constraint is therefore precise. Adolescents possess meaningful capacities for judgement and control, but having a capacity is not the same as deploying it consistently across social and emotional conditions. Age, puberty, neural measures and inconsistency must not be used to diagnose individual maturity. Later reasoning must avoid both caricatures: the miniature adult whose context is irrelevant and the unfinished child whose agency can be discounted.

## Chapter 5 – Every Task Has a Social Shadow

Adolescence makes social meaning especially visible, but the underlying truth is broader: action takes place among other people. A task can ask one thing cognitively and another thing socially.

An answer offered in public may risk embarrassment. Visible effort may signal commitment in one group and low status in another. Asking for help may demonstrate judgement or expose vulnerability. The learner is not simply choosing whether to perform the task; they may also be managing what the action will mean.

Peer influence is often described as a pressure imposed on an otherwise independent person. The evidence suggests an amplifier. Peer presence has affected risky choices more strongly among adolescents and young people than adults in some laboratory tasks, and social rewards and reputation can become highly salient. Yet visible peer norms have also increased prosocial choices. Responsiveness to peers has no fixed moral direction; it amplifies the local meaning, reward and risk attached to conduct. [Sources: S1-SRC-082–S1-SRC-085, S1-SRC-088]

This claim needs two cautions. First, peers are selected as well as influential: similarity among friends cannot automatically be attributed to social causation. Second, laboratory peer-presence tasks cannot represent all ordinary relationships or cultures. Average resistance to peer influence changes with age, but cross-sectional self-report cannot establish a fixed individual trajectory. [Sources: S1-SRC-081, S1-SRC-086]

Belonging reaches deeper than influence. In the reviewed, mainly school-based and English-language evidence, adolescents described belonging in terms including recognition, accepted identity, membership, safety and security—not merely attendance, proximity or friendliness. Teacher support, peer relationships and institutional climate are associated with belonging, while discrimination is associated with lower belonging in evidence whose cultural and educational scope is limited. Absent, disconnected and alternative-provision learners may be underrepresented. The location of a belonging problem cannot automatically be placed inside the learner. It may lie partly in how legitimacy and recognition are distributed. [Sources: S1-SRC-090–S1-SRC-095]

Status is not one ladder. Likeability and perceived popularity are distinct; visibility can coexist with insecurity, and influence with weak acceptance. Friendship, popularity, recognition, group identification and belonging overlap without becoming interchangeable. Group membership can provide identity and continuity while sharpening boundaries against outsiders. [Sources: S1-SRC-087, S1-SRC-096, S1-SRC-097]

Belonging and connectedness are consistently associated with motivation, adjustment, behaviour, academic outcomes and lower health risks. Those associations matter, but they do not establish belonging as one independent cause. Belonging may influence outcomes, follow from prior success and well-being, or share causes with safety, support and institutional climate. Connectedness is not a substitute for safeguarding or clinical support. [Sources: S1-SRC-091, S1-SRC-093, S1-SRC-094]

Contribution reveals the tension especially clearly. Being genuinely needed can support purpose, social integration and belonging. Being used under coercion, inequality or low autonomy is not the same experience. Cooperation and competition are likewise not inherently beneficial or harmful; their effects depend on goals, rules, relationships and meaning. [Sources: S1-SRC-089, S1-SRC-098–S1-SRC-100]

**Claim type:** Empirical Finding; **method:** cross-source synthesis; **confidence:** moderate for mechanisms and distinctions; high that belonging is consequentially associated with outcomes, but not that it independently causes them.

Education therefore faces two possible layers of interpretation. The learner may anticipate—or respond without explicit deliberation to—what an action means socially. The observer then infers ability, motivation or character from that action. Both interpretations can be wrong. Later reasoning must treat the peer group and institution as part of the conditions under which agency becomes visible, while preserving the possibility of real individual preferences and relatively stable differences. Putting learners together does not guarantee collaboration; enrolling them does not guarantee belonging.

## Chapter 6 – Responsibility Begins Where Compliance Ends

The social chapter leaves a difficult question. If action is conditional and socially meaningful, what becomes of responsibility? The evidence does not permit either moralising individualism—poor action reveals poor character—or contextual determinism—the learner had no agency.

Compliance is an unreliable proxy for moral endorsement. A learner can give the approved answer from fear, reward, habit, reputational pressure or personally accepted conviction. Another can endorse a principle and fail to enact it under social threat. Motivation research therefore meets moral development at a crucial point: the outward behaviour does not reveal the reason for acting. [Sources: S1-SRC-001, S1-SRC-002, S1-SRC-057]

Adolescent moral development is multi-process. It involves cognitive appraisal, moral emotion, relationships, group processes and culturally situated norms rather than a single universal ascent from rule-following to mature judgement. Empirical evidence can describe parts of this development. It cannot decide which conception of the good life or responsibility education should adopt; that is explicitly normative work for Stage 2. [Sources: S1-SRC-056–S1-SRC-058]

Identity and future orientation complicate responsibility further. Future-directed action may depend partly on whether a future feels imaginable, identity-relevant and connected to a credible route from present action. Peer acceptance and group membership are reciprocally associated with identity development. Group identities can make possible selves intelligible while also policing who may inhabit them. Material opportunity is associated with expectations and planning and may shape which futures appear credible without wholly determining either. [Sources: S1-SRC-053–S1-SRC-060, S1-SRC-096, S1-SRC-097]

This matters because responsibility can involve willingly undertaking difficult, uninteresting work for a reason the person endorses. It is not reducible to pleasure. But neither is obedience proof of responsibility. Meaningful contribution can support purpose and belonging; coerced contribution can become burden. The same surface behaviour may express chosen obligation, strategic conformity or constrained necessity. [Sources: S1-SRC-002, S1-SRC-057, S1-SRC-089]

Explanation and excuse must remain distinct. Understanding how social threat, opportunity, development or stress shaped an action does not settle accountability. Equally, assigning accountability without asking what the person could understand, endorse and realistically do risks mistaking circumstance for character. Stage 1 cannot provide a universal responsibility threshold. It can require later stages to preserve the tension.

**Claim type:** Empirical Finding for motivational and developmental distinctions; **method:** cross-source synthesis; **confidence:** moderate. Claims about what responsibility or accountability should mean are Normative Judgements reserved for Stage 2. Cultural scope is limited; identity and future-orientation evidence is often Western, English-language, self-reported and correlational, and causal direction between identity difficulties and mental-health symptoms remains unresolved.

The educational constraint is to distinguish behavioural compliance from endorsed responsibility and to avoid treating one culturally preferred life trajectory as a neutral measure of maturity. Young people remain agents. Their agency is exercised through reasons, relationships and possibilities that are not distributed equally.

## Chapter 7 – There Is No View from the Standard Learner

The preceding chapters have repeatedly shown why identical appearances can mislead: knowledge alters task size; motives alter the meaning of effort; bodily states alter deployment; adolescence alters priorities; groups alter social stakes. The final layer concerns the observer. Educational systems do not merely record variation. Their measures, categories and access conditions participate in making it visible.

Psychological evidence disproportionately represents Western populations, and even multisite studies may use constructs whose meanings are not equivalent across settings. Nationality is an incomplete proxy for culture. Some measures of child and adolescent psychopathology do not yet demonstrate that equal scores carry equal meanings across cultural groups. This does not prove that every psychological finding fails to generalise. It means transportability must be established rather than assumed. [Sources: S1-SRC-101–S1-SRC-105]

Opportunity shapes what can be observed. Socioeconomic position is associated with attainment and executive-function performance, but these relationships are heterogeneous and observational. Measures of socioeconomic status combine different realities; poverty may involve instability, crowding, noise, environmental hazards and psychosocial stress. Disability, poverty, language, migration, gender and geography can interact with institutional barriers. Learners missing from surveys or records do not cease to exist; a system can make variation statistically invisible. [Sources: S1-SRC-106–S1-SRC-112]

Language makes the interpretive problem vivid. Bilingual experience produces neither a universal cognitive advantage nor a universal deficit. Second-language learners show heterogeneous profiles across decoding, comprehension and language measures. Performance in one language cannot stand without qualification as a complete measure of knowledge or learning capacity. [Sources: S1-SRC-113, S1-SRC-114]

Diagnostic and demographic categories can reveal consequential patterns without supplying complete persons. ADHD groups show average executive-function differences, but no single deficit characterises every individual. Autism identification varies with referral and case-finding practices; deficit-only models can obscure person–environment relations, while difference-only accounts can erase impairment and high support needs. Autistic people with high support or communication needs, girls and gender-diverse people affected by referral pathways, and disabled learners absent from datasets remain underrepresented. Average gender differences are often smaller than variation within groups and change across social contexts; some domains show meaningful average differences, while binary and historically bounded datasets limit inference and underrepresent gender-diverse learners. Categories and diagnoses can be useful; category membership cannot determine an individual profile. [Sources: S1-SRC-107, S1-SRC-108, S1-SRC-115–S1-SRC-119]

Inclusion provides a final warning about surfaces. Inclusive placement generally does not harm and may slightly benefit peers without identified special educational needs. That finding neither proves benefit for every disabled learner nor makes physical location equivalent to participation. Accessibility, support, staffing and recognition help condition whether physical presence becomes meaningful participation. [Sources: S1-SRC-107, S1-SRC-120]

**Claim type:** Empirical Finding; **method:** cross-source synthesis; **confidence:** high that no single standard-learner profile is defensible; moderate about the mechanisms producing any observed individual difference; open for many intersectional and cross-cultural questions.

The central thesis can now be stated in full. Educational behaviour is evidence requiring interpretation. The evidence may contain real information about knowledge, skill, motivation or need; measures and group patterns are not meaningless. But interpretation must remain provisional where prior opportunity, language, access, bodily state, social meaning, task design or institutional identification may have shaped what became visible.

The foundational mistake is not measurement itself. It is forgetting that the measure observed a developing person performing under conditions. Education must never confuse the clarity of a score with completeness of understanding.

## Method and Evidence Note

Stage 1 used an extended rapid evidence review rather than conducting a new systematic review or meta-analysis. The accepted corpus contains 120 verified sources divided equally across six workstreams: motivation and agency; cognition and learning; adolescent development; emotion and embodiment; social life and status; and individual variation and generalisability. Reviews, meta-analyses and authoritative reports were prioritised, with strong primary studies used for boundaries or gaps. Contrary, null and scope-limiting evidence was retained. The full source details, evidence weights, limitations and prohibited overextensions remain in `STAGE_LEDGER.csv`.

Specialists extracted the strongest mechanisms from their own workstream evidence, then challenged one another’s overclaims. The integration preserved recurring cautions: association is not causation; laboratory performance is not everyday competence; population patterns do not predict individuals; and contextual explanation must not erase agency or genuine cognitive constraint.

The method is broad but not exhaustive. English-language and Western publication systems remain overrepresented. Evidence varies in age range, construct definition, culture, setting and quality. Several domains rely heavily on self-report, correlational designs or laboratory tasks. The corpus supports a provisional Version 0.1 baseline, not universal laws or precise individual diagnosis. [Sources: S1-SRC-001–S1-SRC-120]

## Minimum Defensible Human Baseline

The following is the shortest account that preserves the report’s core logic:

- Humans are agents, but agency is exercised under cognitive, bodily, social, developmental and material constraints.
- Attention and working memory are bounded; prior knowledge changes effective processing demands and supports further learning. [Sources: S1-SRC-025, S1-SRC-026, S1-SRC-030–S1-SRC-032]
- Retention, understanding and transfer are distinct. Stronger recall does not by itself prove flexible use. [Ledger: S1-CLM-005, S1-EVD-007, S1-EVD-008; sources: S1-SRC-028–S1-SRC-040]
- Motivation is multidimensional. Interest, endorsed value, perceived competence, goals, pressure and incentives can organise action differently. [Sources: S1-SRC-001–S1-SRC-004, S1-SRC-009–S1-SRC-017]
- Capability is not identical to its momentary deployment. Sleep, stress and emotion can alter functioning without diagnosing potential. [Sources: S1-SRC-061–S1-SRC-074]
- Development is patterned but asynchronous. Age and group averages cannot establish an individual’s maturity or competence. [Sources: S1-SRC-041–S1-SRC-060]
- Human action is relational. Peers, recognition, status, belonging and institutional conditions alter what actions mean and cost. [Sources: S1-SRC-081–S1-SRC-100]
- Moral action cannot be inferred from compliance, and responsibility cannot be reduced either to character or circumstance. [Sources: S1-SRC-002, S1-SRC-053–S1-SRC-060, S1-SRC-089]
- Human variation belongs inside the baseline. No demographic, diagnostic, cultural or socioeconomic category supplies a complete learner profile. [Sources: S1-SRC-101–S1-SRC-120]
- Observed performance is evidence about a person acting under conditions, not a transparent reading of the whole person.

## KS3–KS4 Developmental Constraints

| Constraint | Defensible interpretation | Prohibited overextension |
|---|---|---|
| KS3–KS4 substantially overlaps adolescence | Use adolescent evidence as a provisional population lens while preserving variation in age, puberty and social position. | Treat the educational phase as one biological stage. |
| Control is meaningful but variably deployed | Learners may possess reasoning and control that become less consistent under unfamiliar, emotional or socially exposed conditions. | Describe adolescents as generally irrational or incapable. |
| Priorities change | Social evaluation, identity and emotionally important goals can change what an adolescent is trying to achieve. | Assume inconsistency proves immaturity or conscious peer calculation. |
| Development is asynchronous | Control, puberty, identity, regulation and future orientation follow partly independent trajectories. | Infer a universal maturity age or sequence. |
| Peer sensitivity has no fixed direction | Peers can amplify prosocial or risky local norms. | Treat peers as inherently harmful or all similarity as influence. |
| Identity remains revisable | Exploration, commitment, continuity and reconsideration may coexist. | Diagnose uncertainty as failure or prescribe one identity path. |
| Future orientation is multidimensional | Anticipation, planning and delay evaluation differ and are associated with opportunity. | Treat short horizons as a fixed moral defect. |
| Developmental evidence is probabilistic | Population patterns can constrain reasoning. | Use age, puberty, neural or executive-function measures to diagnose an individual. |

## Learner Variation and Non-Standard Profiles

No single standard-learner profile is empirically defensible. This does not mean individual differences are unreal or that every measure is invalid. It means educational interpretation must consider what a task presupposes and what the institution makes possible.

Variation may occur in prior knowledge, language history, sensory and communication experience, executive profiles, motivation, bodily state, developmental timing, social safety, cultural meaning, material opportunity and access. These dimensions interact, and the evidence does not adequately quantify many intersections. [Sources: S1-SRC-101–S1-SRC-120]

Three cautions follow:

1. **Group evidence is not an individual profile.** Average differences may identify population risk, structural inequality or useful questions; they cannot determine a learner’s ability, motivation or need.
2. **Measurement is part of the phenomenon.** Language, construct validity, accessibility, familiarity and referral systems affect what becomes visible.
3. **Context does not explain everything.** Stable differences and genuine impairments exist. The correct response to essentialism is better interpretation, not denial of the person.

## Core Human Tensions Education Must Respect

| Tension | What must remain true at both poles |
|---|---|
| Agency ↔ context | People act and choose; their options, reasons and deployment conditions are socially and materially shaped. |
| Capacity ↔ deployment | A learner may possess a capacity without displaying it consistently; weak performance may also reflect a real knowledge or skill gap. |
| Retention ↔ transfer | Durable knowledge matters; portable use requires recognition and adaptation beyond recall. |
| Autonomy ↔ structure | Meaningful ownership can coexist with guidance and limits; choice alone does not create agency. |
| Belonging ↔ exclusion | Recognition and membership can support participation; groups can also police identities and boundaries. |
| Cooperation ↔ competition | Neither has a fixed educational sign; effects depend on goals, rules, relationships and meaning. |
| Responsibility ↔ compliance | Obedience does not prove endorsement; contextual explanation does not erase accountability. |
| Present salience ↔ future orientation | Immediate social and emotional stakes are real; future-directed action also depends on credible pathways. |
| Population pattern ↔ individual prediction | Group patterns can guide caution; they cannot decide an individual case. |
| Explanation ↔ excuse | Understanding mechanisms improves judgement; it does not automatically settle what should be expected or forgiven. |

## What This Stage Does Not Establish

This stage does not establish:

- a universal or exhaustive theory of human nature;
- a final model of flourishing, responsibility or the good life;
- a universal taxonomy of motivation, identity, morality or culture;
- one adolescent brain model, sensitive period, maturity threshold or developmental sequence;
- that correlation demonstrates causation for autonomy, belonging, sleep, adversity or attainment;
- that all rewards are harmful, all choice is beneficial or all structure is controlling;
- that context explains every performance or removes personal agency;
- that a bodily state, biomarker, diagnosis, demographic category or task score reveals individual potential;
- broad far transfer from narrow training or recall;
- universal attainment gains from improving readiness, sleep, stress or belonging;
- any subject, capability, pedagogy, assessment model, policy or curriculum architecture.

## Uncertainties and Open Questions

1. Which components—autonomy, competence support, relationship quality, prior success or learner interpretation—produce observed autonomy-support associations?
2. How far do retention and strategy effects transfer into complex, collaborative, long-duration adolescent activity?
3. Are there domain-specific adolescent sensitive periods, and where are their boundaries?
4. What is the causal direction among identity processes, mental-health symptoms and shared context?
5. How causal and KS3–KS4-specific are the effects of sleep, stress, regulation and movement?
6. What produces the benefits associated with belonging: belonging itself, prior success, safety, support, institutional climate or their interaction?
7. Which Stage 1 patterns transport across cultures, institutions and languages, and which depend on local meanings or measures?
8. How do disability, neurotype, language, gender, poverty, culture and institutional access interact in development and participation?
9. How should education distinguish a failure of knowledge, recognition, motivation and deployment without pretending to diagnose from one performance?

These questions remain open. They must not be resolved by rhetorical confidence or by selecting a preferred educational design.

## Conclusion

The most powerful story this evidence allows is not that human beings are infinitely malleable, nor that every failure is the environment’s fault. It is that observed capacities are expressed under conditions.

Knowledge changes how large a problem is. Motives change what effort means. Bodies change what can be mobilised today. Adolescence changes priorities and the consistency with which capacities travel across settings. Peers and institutions change the social price of action. Culture, language, opportunity and measurement change what observers are able to see. Through all of this, the person remains an agent—not sovereign over every condition, not reducible to any condition, and never fully disclosed by one performance.

That is the minimum human baseline Stage 2 needs before it can ask what flourishing should mean. Stage 2 should examine the risks of demanding what bounded cognition cannot sustain, mistaking obedience for agency, moralising temporary states, treating belonging and status as decorative, confusing inconsistency with incapacity, or quietly turning an ideal learner into the definition of human. These are cautions for its normative inquiry, not conclusions about flourishing.

Stage 1 does not yet tell the project what education should value. It gives the next stage a harder and more useful starting point: any model of flourishing must be tested against bounded, embodied, social, developing and genuinely different agents. The project adopts, as an explicit ethical commitment rather than an empirical law, that no learner's worth or potential should be inferred from one performance alone.
