# Glossary

This glossary defines the core terms used across Year Zero Education.

Definitions should be updated when terms become unclear, overloaded or misleading.

---

## Adversarial review

A structured attempt to find weaknesses, hidden assumptions, unsupported claims, bias, implementation risks or overconfident conclusions in a stage output.

Adversarial review is not hostile behaviour. It is quality control.

---

## Agency

The capacity to form intentions, make choices, act on reasons, revise behaviour and experience oneself as a participant in one’s own life rather than merely an object shaped by external forces.

Agency is central to the project because AI-rich environments may either increase or weaken human agency depending on design, access and culture.

---

## Agent

An AI role or workflow harness used to perform a particular kind of task in the project.

Agents in this project are reusable functions, not stage-bound characters.

---

## Assumption

A claim adopted to allow reasoning, modelling or design to proceed when evidence is incomplete or uncertainty remains.

Assumptions must be labelled and revisable.

---

## Capability

A developable ability that enables a young person to perform an enduring human function.

Capabilities may include cognitive, social, practical, ethical, creative, emotional and metacognitive dimensions.

A capability is not merely a topic. It is something a learner becomes more able to do, judge, create, sustain or practise.

---

## Claim

Any substantive statement that the project relies on.

Claims may be empirical findings, working assumptions, normative judgements, speculative projections or design decisions.

---

## Claim classification

The process of labelling what kind of claim is being made so that evidence, uncertainty and downstream use are handled honestly.

---

## Curriculum architecture

The high-level structure of a curriculum: aims, principles, domains, pathways, learning experiences, evidence model and implementation logic.

Curriculum architecture comes before detailed lesson design.

---

## Curriculum hypothesis

A proposed curriculum model that is coherent and testable but not yet proven.

Version 0.1 of Year Zero Education is a curriculum hypothesis.

---

## Design decision

A choice made about how the curriculum or project should be structured.

Design decisions should be linked to upstream research, constraints and alternatives.

---

## Enduring human function

Something humans must still do, decide, judge, create, coordinate, protect, experience or become across plausible futures.

Enduring human functions are derived from human invariants and life-within-futures analysis.

---

## Evidence of learning

Observable, reviewable or demonstrable indications that learning has occurred.

Evidence may include performance, explanation, transfer, project artefacts, reflection, judgement, iteration, portfolio work and real-world application.

Evidence of learning is broader than test scores.

---

## Evidence weight

A judgement about how much a source or piece of evidence should influence a conclusion.

Suggested weights include anchor, supporting, contextual, illustrative and non-decision.

---

## Flourishing

A working model of what it means for a person to live and develop well.

In this project, flourishing must be derived from human realities, evidence and explicit normative judgement. It should not be treated as a simple synonym for happiness, achievement or economic success.

---

## Future condition

A plausible feature of the future environment in which young people may live, learn, work or form identity.

Future conditions may relate to AI capability, labour, information, trust, creativity, social life, governance, inequality or human agency.

---

## Human baseline

The minimum defensible set of assumptions about human beings that the project must respect.

The human baseline is not a total theory of human nature. It is a practical foundation for curriculum derivation.

---

## Human invariant

A feature of human life that remains important across plausible technological futures.

Examples may include embodiment, agency, meaning, attention, belonging or moral responsibility, but final invariants must be derived through the stage process rather than assumed in advance.

---

## Implementation feedback

Information from real or simulated use of project materials.

Implementation feedback may show whether an idea is understandable, usable, safe, motivating, burdensome, inaccessible or effective in practice.

It should not be treated as proof of impact unless gathered through suitable methods.

---

## Intelligence Era

The emerging period in which artificial intelligence makes access to cognitive capability increasingly abundant, powerful and unevenly distributed across people, institutions and societies.

The term does not imply that all people benefit equally or that AI determines the future by itself.

---

## Knowledge vehicle

A body of knowledge used to develop a capability or support an enduring human function.

The term reminds the project that knowledge should be justified by its role, not included only because it is inherited.

---

## Learner pathway

A route through the curriculum that helps a learner develop capabilities over time.

Pathways may vary by context, interest, developmental stage, support level and implementation setting.

---

## Normative judgement

A claim about what should matter, what should be valued or what kind of human development is desirable.

Normative judgements are necessary in education, but they must be explicit.

---

## Pass-forward

The controlled handoff from one stage to the next.

A pass-forward output states what later stages may rely on, what remains uncertain and what must not be overextended.

Use “pass-forward” as a noun or adjective and “pass forward” as the verb.

---

## Portfolio

A structured collection of learner artefacts, demonstrations, reflections and evidence of capability development.

A portfolio should support learning and evidence, not become a surveillance file.

---

## Public summary

A plain-language explanation of a stage output for non-specialist readers.

A public summary should make the work understandable without hiding uncertainty.

---

## Research stage

A defined step in the research chain with a specific purpose, inputs, methods, outputs and pass-forward requirements.

---

## Scenario discovery

A method for identifying plausible future conditions, drivers and uncertainties without claiming to predict one future.

Scenario discovery should support curriculum reasoning, not produce entertaining fiction.

---

## Speculative projection

A future-facing claim about what may happen or what conditions may emerge.

Speculative projections must be labelled clearly and linked to uncertainty.

---

## Stage gate

A quality checkpoint that determines whether a stage can pass, pass with cautions, revise or roll back.

---

## Stage ledger

The single structured CSV used by each stage to record sources, evidence, claims, assumptions, uncertainties, risks, decisions, traceability links and challenges.

---

## Traceability

The ability to follow a curriculum element back through the research chain to the claims, assumptions and evidence that support it.

Traceability is central to making Version 0.1 auditable.

---

## Transfer

The ability to apply learning in a different context from the one in which it was first acquired.

Transfer is important because future conditions may require young people to adapt knowledge and capability to unfamiliar situations.

---

## Uncertainty

A known limit, unresolved question, confidence boundary or unknown that affects a claim or decision.

Uncertainty should be preserved and managed, not hidden.

---

## Version 0.1

The first public Year Zero Education curriculum hypothesis.

It should be coherent, traceable and usable, but not presented as proven or final.

---

## Working assumption

A provisional assumption adopted to enable progress.

Working assumptions should be visible, challengeable and linked to revision triggers.
