# Ethics and Public Testing

This file defines the ethical, safety, privacy and public-testing rules for Year Zero Education.

The project concerns young people. That makes caution, dignity and transparency essential.

This file is not legal advice. It is the project’s operating standard for responsible development and early public testing.

---

## 1. Core ethical principle

Young people are not research instruments, marketing assets or proof points.

Any work involving learners must protect their dignity, privacy, safety, autonomy and future interests.

---

## 2. Scope

This file applies to:

- curriculum testing;
- learner feedback;
- parent and educator feedback;
- public examples of learner work;
- AI-assisted learning activities;
- portfolio evidence;
- case studies;
- social media content;
- community contributions;
- implementation pilots;
- future verification systems.

---

## 3. Status of Version 0.1

**Version 0.1 is a curriculum hypothesis, not evidence of proven effectiveness.**

It must not be described as:

- proven;
- clinically validated;
- guaranteed to improve outcomes;
- equivalent to formal qualifications;
- a replacement for statutory education requirements;
- suitable for every learner or context.

Public language should make the experimental status clear.

---

## 4. Learner privacy

Do not publicly collect or publish:

- full names of minors;
- addresses;
- school identifiers where unnecessary;
- private reflections;
- medical or mental health details;
- family circumstances;
- images or videos of minors;
- unredacted AI chat logs;
- assessment records;
- sensitive identity information;
- anything a learner may later reasonably regret being public.

Use privacy minimisation by default.

---

## 5. Consent and assent

When learner involvement goes beyond ordinary private use, the project should require:

- informed consent from a parent, guardian or authorised adult where appropriate;
- assent from the young person;
- clear explanation of what will be used;
- clear explanation of what will not be used;
- ability to withdraw;
- no unnecessary pressure to participate.

A learner should not be publicly identifiable as a condition of participation.

---

## 6. Public learner work

Learner work may be discussed publicly only when:

- it is anonymised or consented to;
- it does not reveal sensitive information;
- the learner and responsible adult understand the use;
- the work is not used to rank or shame the learner;
- the public value justifies inclusion;
- the learner’s future dignity is considered.

Prefer synthetic examples, anonymised summaries or fictionalised composites where possible.

---

## 7. AI interaction logs

AI logs can contain sensitive information even when they appear harmless.

Do not publish learner AI chats unless:

- they are fully reviewed and redacted;
- consent and assent are clear;
- there is a strong educational reason;
- no personal or sensitive data remains;
- the learner is not identifiable.

For most purposes, summarise patterns rather than publishing logs.

---

## 8. Early testing levels

The project should distinguish levels of testing.

### Level 0 — Internal design review

No learner involvement. Agents and adults review logic, safety and usability.

### Level 1 — Adult simulation

Adults test materials as if they were learners, identifying confusion, burden and feasibility issues.

### Level 2 — Informal learner use

A parent, educator or mentor uses a small part of the material with a learner privately. Feedback is general and anonymised.

### Level 3 — Structured pilot

A defined group uses a defined part of the curriculum with consent, privacy process and review.

### Level 4 — Evidence study

Formal research requiring proper design, governance, consent and analysis.

Version 0.1 should begin at Level 0–2 unless a formal testing process is established.

---

## 9. Public testing claims

The project may say:

- “This activity was tried with a small number of learners.”
- “Early feedback suggests this part was engaging/confusing/useful.”
- “This remains exploratory.”

The project must not say:

- “This proves the curriculum works.”
- “Learners using this will outperform others.”
- “This is validated.”
- “This replaces school.”

---

## 10. Safeguarding boundaries

The project should avoid direct unsupervised interaction with minors unless a proper safeguarding structure exists.

If community spaces are created, they should have:

- moderation;
- clear reporting route;
- adult oversight;
- no private adult-minor messaging by default;
- rules against sharing personal information;
- escalation process for concerns.

---

## 11. Emotional and psychological safety

Some Year Zero topics may involve identity, future anxiety, social comparison, capability, failure, agency or meaning.

Materials should avoid:

- fatalistic AI futures;
- implying learners are obsolete;
- ranking human worth by productivity;
- shaming conventional educational paths;
- pressuring learners to optimise every part of life;
- overexposing personal reflections.

Learning should develop agency, not anxiety.

---

## 12. Equity and access

The project should not assume all learners have:

- expensive devices;
- paid AI tools;
- quiet study space;
- highly educated parents;
- reliable internet;
- supportive schools;
- strong English literacy;
- neurotypical learning profiles;
- high confidence;
- stable home environments.

Implementation guides should include low-resource and low-tech alternatives where possible.

---

## 13. Neurodiversity and individual variation

The curriculum should not treat one learner profile as normal and all others as exceptions.

Design should consider:

- attention differences;
- executive function differences;
- sensory needs;
- communication differences;
- anxiety;
- social needs;
- uneven ability profiles;
- giftedness and asynchronous development;
- motivation differences.

Where the project lacks expertise, it should state uncertainty rather than overclaiming accessibility.

---

## 14. Cultural and family pluralism

The project should recognise that families and communities differ in values, beliefs, resources and educational aims.

It should avoid presenting one narrow ideal of flourishing as universal.

Where normative judgements are made, they should be explicit and open to challenge.

---

## 15. Use in schools and alternative provision

If used by schools, tutors, clubs, home educators or alternative provision settings, implementers should consider:

- local legal duties;
- safeguarding policies;
- data protection rules;
- parental communication;
- accessibility requirements;
- assessment burden;
- learner consent and comfort;
- alignment or conflict with existing obligations.

The project should not encourage institutions to bypass their duties.

---

## 16. Portfolio and evidence privacy

Evidence of learning can become sensitive because it may include:

- reflections;
- failures;
- personal goals;
- collaboration records;
- AI logs;
- peer feedback;
- project artefacts;
- self-assessments.

Portfolio systems should default to private ownership by the learner and family unless explicitly shared.

---

## 17. Data minimisation

Collect the least data needed.

Before collecting any data, ask:

1. Why is this needed?
2. Who will see it?
3. How long will it be kept?
4. Could the same learning be evidenced with less personal data?
5. Could it harm the learner if made public?
6. Does the learner understand the use?

---

## 18. Implementation feedback privacy

When collecting feedback from parents, educators or learners, prefer:

- age range rather than exact age;
- broad setting rather than named institution;
- general observations rather than identifiable stories;
- anonymised examples;
- optional participation;
- no unnecessary demographic data.

---

## 19. Commercial and reputational boundaries

If the project later develops partnerships, funding, services or paid support, it must preserve trust by making clear:

- what remains open-source;
- what is paid, if anything;
- whether data is collected;
- who benefits;
- whether claims are evidence-backed;
- how conflicts of interest are handled.

Public trust is easier to lose than regain.

---

## 20. Ethical review before public release

Before Version 0.1 is released, run an ethics review covering:

- public claims;
- learner privacy;
- implementation guidance;
- AI tool guidance;
- portfolio evidence;
- parent and educator expectations;
- risk of misuse;
- accessibility;
- contribution pathway;
- feedback collection.

The review should be recorded in Stage 11.

---

## 21. Red flags requiring revision

Pause or revise if the project:

- implies proven impact without evidence;
- invites minors to share private data publicly;
- creates pressure to leave formal education;
- uses learner work as promotional proof without consent;
- produces anxiety-heavy future narratives;
- makes high-stakes claims from low-stakes testing;
- ignores access constraints;
- hides uncertainty to improve public appeal.

---

## 22. Public communication standard

Public communication should be:

- clear;
- humble;
- truthful;
- non-alarmist;
- non-utopian;
- protective of learners;
- explicit about experimental status;
- inviting of critique.

Do not use young people’s futures as marketing drama.

---

## 23. Minimal safe feedback form

A safe early feedback form should ask only:

- Which material did you use?
- What was your role? Parent, educator, learner, reviewer, other.
- Approximate learner age range, if relevant.
- What worked?
- What was confusing?
- What felt unsafe, unrealistic or inaccessible?
- What would improve it?
- Are you sharing any personal learner information? If yes, remove it before submitting.

---

## 24. Ethics checklist for agents

Before producing implementation or public-facing outputs, agents should check:

- Could this expose learner data?
- Could this overclaim effectiveness?
- Could this pressure families into unsafe decisions?
- Could this increase anxiety or fatalism?
- Could this exclude low-resource learners?
- Could this be misunderstood as formal advice?
- Could this be used to rank or shame learners?
- Could this create private adult-minor contact risks?

If yes, revise or flag for human review.

---

## 25. Final ethical stance

The project should be ambitious about education and conservative about harm.

It should be willing to question inherited systems, but not careless with children’s lives, privacy or confidence.

A future curriculum should help young people become more capable, more thoughtful, more agentic and more human — not more exposed, optimised or anxious.
