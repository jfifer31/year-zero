# Implementation Guide – Version 0.1

This file explains how Year Zero Education Version 0.1 may be used by parents, educators, mentors and learners.

It should be completed during Stage 10 and finalised during Stage 11.

---

## 1. Status

**Status:** `draft | review | release_candidate | published`  
**Authorising stage:** Stage 10  
**Final release gate:** Stage 11  

---

## 2. Important warning

Version 0.1 is a curriculum hypothesis.

It is not:

- a formal qualification;
- proof of educational effectiveness;
- a replacement for statutory duties;
- a guarantee of outcomes;
- a complete school programme;
- suitable for every learner without adaptation.

Use it as an exploratory framework, not as a high-stakes system.

## 4. Starting principles

Implementation should be:

- low-stakes at first;
- privacy-conscious;
- adapted to context;
- honest about uncertainty;
- focused on learner growth;
- realistic about time and resources;
- open to feedback;
- grounded in the curriculum framework.

---

## 5. Minimum viable starting route

For early use, do not try to implement the whole framework at once.

A simple starting route:

1. Read the curriculum overview.
2. Choose one domain or capability area.
3. Choose one project or learning experience.
4. Define the evidence learners will produce.
5. Run a short learning cycle.
6. Review artefacts and reflection.
7. Record what worked and what did not.
8. Submit implementation feedback if useful.

---

## 6. Implementation modes

### 6.1 Parent-led or home education use

Best for:

- families seeking a future-facing enrichment framework;
- home educators wanting capability-based structure;
- learners who benefit from flexible pacing.

Start with:

- one project;
- one evidence artefact;
- one reflection;
- one feedback conversation.

Avoid:

- trying to recreate school at home;
- turning every activity into assessment;
- overloading the learner with abstract theory.

---

### 6.2 Mainstream school enrichment

Best for:

- enrichment days;
- project weeks;
- PSHE/citizenship/STEM/AI literacy extensions;
- interdisciplinary curriculum pilots;
- portfolio or project-based learning experiments.

Start with:

- a short module;
- a cross-curricular project;
- a capability evidence task;
- a public or internal showcase.

Avoid:

- claiming it replaces exam preparation;
- adding workload without removing anything;
- using AI activities as novelty without learning purpose.

---

### 6.3 Alternative provision

Best for:

- learners who need relevance, agency and practical capability;
- flexible project-based settings;
- mentor-led development.

Start with:

- learner interest mapping;
- one practical project;
- visible progress evidence;
- short feedback cycles;
- careful emotional safety.

Avoid:

- high-pressure public performance;
- heavy written reflection for learners who need alternatives;
- assuming all learners trust education systems.

---

### 6.4 Independent learner route

Best for:

- motivated learners;
- older KS3 or KS4 learners;
- learners already using AI tools or independent projects.

Start with:

- learner goal;
- chosen project;
- weekly progress log;
- artefact portfolio;
- periodic mentor/adult review.

Avoid:

- unsupervised sharing of personal data;
- AI outsourcing;
- isolation without feedback.

---

### 6.5 Mentor-led group route

Best for:

- clubs;
- camps;
- community groups;
- online cohorts with safeguards;
- small project teams.

Start with:

- shared challenge;
- team roles;
- project artefact;
- demonstration;
- group reflection;
- evidence collection.

Avoid:

- unclear safeguarding;
- private adult-minor messaging;
- public learner exposure without consent.

---

## 7. Suggested learning cycle

A Year Zero learning cycle may follow this pattern:

```text
Question or challenge
→ Knowledge input
→ Exploration
→ Practice
→ Artefact or demonstration
→ Feedback
→ Reflection
→ Transfer or next challenge
```

Each cycle should produce at least one meaningful piece of evidence.

---

## 8. Role of the adult

Adults should act as:

- guide;
- questioner;
- safety holder;
- feedback provider;
- resource finder;
- accountability partner;
- reflective conversation partner.

Adults should avoid becoming:

- the owner of the learner’s work;
- the judge of the learner’s worth;
- the source of all answers;
- the person who turns every activity into performance.

---

## 9. Role of the learner

Learners should increasingly practise:

- choosing questions;
- explaining reasoning;
- building artefacts;
- asking for feedback;
- using AI critically;
- reflecting honestly;
- revising work;
- connecting learning to life.

The learner should not be expected to understand the whole research framework before beginning.

---

## 10. Use of AI tools

AI tools may be used to:

- ask questions;
- explain concepts;
- compare options;
- simulate audiences;
- generate feedback prompts;
- help plan projects;
- support accessibility;
- create practice challenges.

Learners should also practise:

- checking AI outputs;
- explaining in their own words;
- identifying mistakes;
- making final judgements;
- doing human-only practice where needed.

AI should support capability development, not replace it.

---

## 11. Evidence collection

Use the minimum meaningful evidence model:

1. goal;
2. artefact;
3. process note;
4. feedback;
5. reflection;
6. next step.

Keep evidence private by default and follow `docs/ETHICS_AND_PUBLIC_TESTING.md`.

---

## 12. Time models

| Time available | Suggested use |
|---|---|
| 1 hour | One small challenge, reflection and feedback. |
| Half day | Mini-project with artefact. |
| 1 week | Focused project cycle. |
| 6 weeks | Domain exploration and portfolio evidence. |
| Term | Multi-project capability pathway. |
| Year | Full Version 0.1 implementation experiment. |

---

## 13. Low-resource adaptations

If resources are limited:

- use free tools;
- allow paper portfolios;
- use local environments;
- prioritise discussion and observation;
- use shared devices;
- avoid paid AI dependence;
- use community problems as project contexts;
- keep evidence lightweight.

---

## 14. Accessibility adaptations

Adapt by varying:

- output format;
- reading load;
- collaboration level;
- presentation expectations;
- sensory demands;
- time allowed;
- adult support;
- use of speech, visuals or physical artefacts;
- reflection format.

Do not equate one kind of performance with capability.

---

## 15. Feedback to the project

Implementation feedback should use broad, anonymised summaries only: what was tried, broad age range, broad setting, what worked, what confused users and what should change.

Do not submit private learner data, learner work, images, videos or AI logs. Use the GitHub implementation feedback issue template and follow `docs/ETHICS_AND_PUBLIC_TESTING.md`.

---

## 16. Implementation checklist

Before using a module or project:

- [ ] I understand this is experimental.
- [ ] I know what capability is being developed.
- [ ] The learner knows what they are doing and why.
- [ ] Evidence collection is proportionate.
- [ ] Privacy is protected.
- [ ] AI tool use is bounded.
- [ ] The activity can be adapted if needed.
- [ ] Feedback will be supportive and specific.

---

## 17. What to avoid

Avoid high-stakes claims, public ranking, excessive portfolio burden, AI outsourcing, adult overcontrol and promotional use of learner work. Follow `docs/ETHICS_AND_PUBLIC_TESTING.md`.
