# Evidence of Learning – Version 0.1

This file defines how learning, capability development and transfer may be evidenced in the Year Zero Education curriculum.

It should be completed during Stage 9 and integrated into the curriculum during Stage 10.

---

**Status:** `draft | review | release_candidate | published`

**Integrated by:** Stage 10  
**Final review:** Stage 11

## 2. Purpose

The purpose of the evidence-of-learning model is to answer:

> How can learners show that they are developing the capabilities this curriculum is designed to cultivate?

The model should avoid two extremes:

1. Reducing learning to standardised test scores.
2. Treating all reflection or activity as equally meaningful evidence.

---

## 3. Evidence principles

| Principle ID | Principle | Why it matters | Design implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| E-001 | Evidence should be meaningful. | It should connect to real capability, not just task completion. | Evidence tasks must map to capabilities. |
| E-002 | Evidence should be proportionate. | It should not create excessive burden. | Use lightweight artefacts where possible. |
| E-003 | Evidence should protect privacy. | Learners are young people, not public proof points. | Default to private portfolios; see `docs/ETHICS_AND_PUBLIC_TESTING.md`. |
| E-004 | Evidence should show growth. | Capability development takes time. | Include iteration and reflection. |
| E-005 | Evidence should include transfer. | Future capability requires adaptation. | Include unfamiliar or applied contexts. |

Revise this table after Stage 9.

---

## 4. Evidence types

| Evidence type | What it can show | What it cannot show alone | Example artefacts |
|---|---|---|---|
| Performance | Ability to do something under conditions. | Internal reasoning unless explained. | Demonstration, presentation, prototype. |
| Explanation | Understanding and reasoning. | Transfer into action. | Written explanation, oral defence, diagram. |
| Reflection | Awareness of process and growth. | Objective competence by itself. | Learning journal, after-action review. |
| Artefact | Product of work. | Full process or individual contribution. | Model, essay, video, code, design, investigation. |
| Transfer task | Use in a new context. | Full developmental history. | Applied challenge, scenario response. |
| Collaboration evidence | Contribution to shared work. | Private understanding. | Peer review, role log, team artefact. |
| Iteration record | Growth through feedback. | Final competence alone. | Drafts, revisions, change notes. |
| Mentor observation | Behaviour and process. | Independent evidence unless structured. | Observation note, rubric. |

## 6. Portfolio model

The portfolio is the primary evidence container.

### 6.1 Portfolio purpose

A learner portfolio should:

- show growth over time;
- collect meaningful artefacts;
- include reflection;
- support feedback;
- protect privacy;
- help learners see their own development;
- provide optional evidence for external review later.

### 6.2 Portfolio sections

| Section | Purpose | Example contents |
|---|---|---|
| Learning goals | Show intention and direction. | Goal notes, selected capabilities. |
| Project artefacts | Show produced work. | Essays, prototypes, investigations, designs. |
| Process evidence | Show how work developed. | Drafts, feedback, revisions, decisions. |
| Reflection | Show metacognition. | After-action reviews, learning journals. |
| Demonstrations | Show performance. | Presentations, recordings, live demonstrations. |
| Transfer examples | Show adaptation. | New-context tasks, applied challenges. |
| Review notes | Show feedback. | Mentor comments, peer review, self-review. |

## 7. Demonstration tasks

A demonstration task asks the learner to show capability in action.

| Demonstration type | Suitable for | Design requirements | Risks |
|---|---|---|---|
| Live explanation | Reasoning, understanding, communication. | Clear prompt, low humiliation risk. | Performance anxiety. |
| Built artefact | Design, making, investigation. | Clear criteria and process evidence. | Overvaluing polish. |
| Scenario response | Judgement and transfer. | Realistic scenario and rubric. | Subjective marking. |
| Debate or deliberation | reasoning, listening, civic judgement. | Norms and facilitation. | Dominance by confident speakers. |
| Field or community task | observation, contribution, practical action. | Safety and context. | Access inequality. |

---

## 8. Rubric design principles

Rubrics should:

- describe observable quality;
- avoid vague personality judgements;
- allow multiple ways to show competence;
- include process and judgement where relevant;
- avoid overprecision;
- be usable by non-specialists;
- include space for narrative feedback.

### 8.1 Generic rubric structure

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| Emerging | Learner is beginning to show the capability with support. |
| Developing | Learner shows partial capability and can improve through feedback. |
| Secure | Learner shows the capability reliably in familiar contexts. |
| Extending | Learner adapts the capability to new or more complex contexts. |

Avoid using levels as fixed identity labels.

---

## 9. Evidence validity checks

For each evidence task, ask:

1. Does it actually show the capability?
2. Could a learner perform well without the target capability?
3. Could a capable learner perform poorly for irrelevant reasons?
4. Is it fair across different learner profiles?
5. Is it feasible in low-resource settings?
6. Does it create privacy risk?
7. Can it be reviewed without excessive burden?
8. Does it encourage useful learning behaviour?

---

## 10. Gaming and distortion risks

Evidence systems can distort learning.

| Risk | Example | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Performance theatre | Learners optimise presentation over substance. | Include process evidence and questioning. |
| Reflection fakery | Learners write what adults want to hear. | Use specific prompts and artefact links. |
| AI outsourcing | AI produces artefact without learner capability. | Include oral defence, process logs and decisions. |
| Rubric chasing | Learners optimise checklist compliance. | Keep rubrics broad and feedback-rich. |
| Portfolio overload | Evidence collection becomes burdensome. | Use minimum meaningful evidence. |
| Privacy exposure | Personal reflections become public. | Default private, anonymise examples. |

---

## 11. AI-assisted evidence

AI may support feedback, simulation or review, but should not become the judge of the learner.

AI can help:

- generate practice prompts;
- compare drafts;
- simulate audiences;
- help learners reflect;
- suggest questions;
- support accessibility.

AI should not:

- make high-stakes judgements;
- replace human review where judgement matters;
- store sensitive learner data unnecessarily;
- encourage learners to outsource thinking;
- create opaque assessment decisions.

## 12. Verification maturity levels

| Level | Description | Suitable for Version 0.1? |
|---|---|---|
| Level 0 – Private reflection | Learner keeps evidence privately. | Yes. |
| Level 1 – Parent/mentor review | Trusted adult reviews artefacts. | Yes. |
| Level 2 – Peer/community feedback | Controlled feedback from others. | With safeguards. |
| Level 3 – External moderation | External reviewer checks evidence. | Future work. |
| Level 4 – Formal credential | Recognised qualification or certificate. | Not Version 0.1. |

---

## 13. Privacy rules

Evidence should default to private. Do not require, request or publish private learner data, learner work, images, videos or AI logs.

Follow the full rules in `docs/ETHICS_AND_PUBLIC_TESTING.md`.

---

## 14. Minimum viable evidence model

For early use, the minimum model should include:

1. learner goal;
2. project or task artefact;
3. short process note;
4. feedback or review;
5. learner reflection;
6. next step.

This is enough to support learning without turning evidence into bureaucracy.
